Processes for dealing with anti-competitive activity

A guide to how we deal with anti-competitive activity in business. Includes information on competition authorities and sectoral regulation

Competitive markets provide the best means of ensuring that the economy’s resources are put to their best use by encouraging enterprise and efficiency, widening choice and contributing to our international competitiveness.

However, timely and effective enforcement is essential to identify any anti-competitive practices or structures that may develop and lead to a loss of competition in markets or abuse of market power.

Current competition authorities

Whilst the Secretary of State for Business, Innovation and Skills sets the overall policy framework for competition, it is regulated by the UK’s independent competition authorities.

There are 4 main competition bodies responsible for investigating and enforcing decisions on competition matters in the UK market:

The Office of Fair Trading

The Office of Fair Trading (OFT) is the principal competition authority. Its responsibility is to enforce competition and make markets work well for the benefit of consumers.

The OFT derives most of its powers from the Enterprise Act 2002 and the Competition Act 1998.

The OFT investigates mergers and anti-competitive practices in markets and also enforces consumer law – see Modernisation – Understanding Competition Law for further information. Large mergers with a European dimension may be covered by the European Community Merger Regulation (ECMR).

The Competition Commission

The Competition Commission (CC) conducts in-depth inquiries into mergers, markets and the regulation of certain industries such as utilities and communications. However, the CC only undertakes its inquiries where they have been referred by the OFT, one of the sectoral regulators, or in exceptional circumstances the Secretary of State.

The Competition Appeal Tribunal

The Competition Appeal Tribunal (CAT) is a specialist judicial body whose function is to hear and rule on appeals of decisions made by the competition authorities. The CAT has a cross-disciplinary expertise in law, economics, business, competition and economic regulatory issues.

The European Commission

The European Commission (Directorate General for Competition) has exclusive powers to act on certain large mergers with a European dimension. It also has powers to investigate anti-competitive practices when trade between members of the European Community, or in some cases the European Economic Area (EEA), is affected.

Sectoral regulators

A number of sectoral regulators have a specific role in promoting or facilitating competition within their sectors. Some also have the power to apply the Competition Act 1998 concurrently with the OFT. The regulators with such ‘concurrent powers’ are:

  • Ofgem - in the energy markets
  • Ofwat - in the water industry
  • Ofcom - in the communications sector
  • ORR - for railway services
  • CAA - in relation to air traffic services
  • Utility Regulator - for gas and electricity in Northern Ireland
  • Monitor - for NHS Foundation Trusts.

OFT market studies and CC market investigations

Office of Fair Trading (OFT) market studies are detailed investigations of markets that are not operating well for consumers, but where neither consumer regulation enforcement by the OFT nor competition enforcement through a referral to the Competition Commission (CC) is likely to resolve the issues.

Where those studies make recommendations to government, the Department for Business, Innovation & Skills (BIS) co-ordinates government responses and ensures that they take account not only of competition and consumer interests but wider public policy objectives.

Market studies will usually be performed under section 5 of the Enterprise Act 2002 in order to identify whether perceived problems should be addressed through the OFT’s other functions. There is a range of possible outcomes of a study including:

  • giving the market a clean bill of health (ie a decision that intervention is not appropriate on the evidence available)
  • publishing information to help consumers
  • encouraging firms to take voluntary action
  • encouraging a consumer code of practice
  • making recommendations to the government or regulators
  • investigation or enforcement action against companies or individuals suspected of breaching consumer law or competition law
  • a market investigation reference to the Competition Commission

The CC conducts in-depth inquiries into markets – market investigations – undertaken in response to a reference made by the OFT or sectoral regulator and, in certain limited circumstances, by the Secretary of State for Business, Innovation and Skills. The market studies and market investigations where BIS has played an active role are:

  • medicines distribution 
  • pharmaceutical price regulation 
  • sale and rent back 
  • payment protection insurance 
  • supply of airport services in the UK by BAA 
  • rolling stock leasing (ROSCOs) 
  • property searches 
  • commercial use of public information (CUPI) 
  • supply of groceries in the UK 

To find information on these and other market studies, go to the National Archives market studies pages.

Mergers

A proper framework for control of mergers is important to ensure that competition is not damaged when businesses come together. UK mergers are considered by the competition authorities under the Enterprise Act 2002. Public interest considerations are specified in the Act as being national security, media plurality and stability of the UK financial system.

Generally, mergers will be considered by the UK competition authorities if the annual UK turnover of the enterprise being taken over exceeds £70 million, or the merger creates a 25% share or more in a market for goods or services in the UK or a substantial part of it. There is no statutory requirement to pre-notify mergers to the UK competition authorities.

Investigation by the OFT

The OFT investigates mergers in the first instance and, with the exception of public interest cases, decides whether or not they should be referred to the CC for further investigation. The test is whether the OFT believes a merger has resulted or may be expected to result in a substantial lessening of competition. In making decisions, the OFT will either:

  1. refer to the CC for further investigation
  2. clear the merger
  3. clear it subject to undertakings in lieu of a reference to the CC

Details of mergers investigated by the OFT can be found on the OFT website.

Investigation by the CC

Where a merger is referred to the CC, it has to determine whether it has resulted in a substantial lessening of competition and to take the action it considers reasonable and practicable to address any adverse effects arising from the merger.

All CC reports are published. Details of mergers investigated by the CC can be found on the Competition Commission website. Companies can also obtain guidance and informal advice from the OFT on its merger procedures.

Mergers between water and sewerage companies

A special regime exists for mergers between water and sewerage companies. These are considered under sections 32-35 of the Water Industry Act 1991, as amended by the Enterprise Act and the Water Act.

European Community Merger Regulation

Large mergers with a European dimension that breach certain jurisdictional thresholds of the European Community Merger Regulation (ECMR) – Council Regulation No. 139/2004 – fall to the European Commission for investigation.

Broadly speaking, mergers involving enterprises with an aggregate worldwide turnover of more than €5 billion (around £4 billion) and where the aggregate Community-wide turnover of each of at least 2 of the enterprises concerned is more than €250 million (around £200 million) will be investigated by the European Commission taking into account the views of member states.

However, those mergers where more than two-thirds of the Community-wide turnover of each enterprise concerned falls within one and the same member state, are not caught by the EC Merger Regulation.

The OFT will normally advise the EC on competition issues it sees relevant to cases being considered under the EC Merger Regulation.

Information on individual cases under the ECMR can be found on the Commission’s website.

Public interest cases under the EC Merger Regulation

The Secretary of State may also intervene on public interest grounds in mergers being considered under the EC Merger Regulation, where the public interest consideration in question is also recognised in the EC Merger Regulation. In such cases the European Commission will continue to consider the competition issues whilst the UK authorities consider public interest aspects.

New processes and competition authorities from April 2014

The government’s 2011 consultation on competition reform confirmed that many aspects of the UK’s competition regime are world class, but it also highlighted some significant challenges for the current system and made a number of proposals to enhance the regime’s performance.

The government’s response (PDF, 591 Kb), published in March 2012, set out the reforms that will come into effect from April 2014.

These reforms - currently being considered by Parliament in the Enterprise and Regulatory Reform Bill – will bring together the best of the OFT and CC into a single Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) to become a world-leading competition authority, advocating competition both at home and abroad. It will have a greater role in ensuring competition in regulated sectors is being addressed, through enhanced cooperation and information sharing between itself and the sector regulators. It will make better use of public resources, and business will benefit from having a single agency to deal with.

1) The Markets and Mergers regimes

When the CMA is set up, we shall maintain the separation of decision-making between the 2 phases of market and merger investigations [see page 94 of the government’s response (PDF, 591 Kb)]. Currently the 2 phases of an investigation are carried out by separate organisations:

  • “Phase 1”, the OFT, which can undertake an initial study into a merger or market to see if there is a competition issue to be addressed. If so, it can refer the case to:
  • The Competition Commission, “Phase 2”, which will carry out a deeper study to get to the nub of the problem and impose remedies

The separation of Phase 1 and Phase 2 decision-making on regulatory appeals will also be maintained and provided for in legislation.

The CMA will have powers, additionally, to conduct investigations into practices that impact on more than one market [see page 30 of the government’s response (PDF, 591 Kb)], such as extended warranties and other secondary point of sale practices, without the need to make multiple market investigation references. These powers will enable the CMA to take a more targeted approach to tackling recurring sources of consumer complaints.

The CMA will have discretion to stop a proposed merger [see page 42 of the government’s response (PDF, 591 Kb)] where there are concerns the merger may be anti-competitive, and to consider with the companies involved whether the merger should be allowed to proceed. Legislation will clarify that the Authority can reverse mergers that have already taken place where this is deemed appropriate after investigation.

The government also proposes to improve remedies by enabling the CMA to require parties to appoint an independent third party [see page 48 of the government’s response (PDF, 591 Kb)] to monitor, arbitrate, and/or implement remedies; and by giving the CMA the power to require parties to publish certain non-price information.

Streamlining and strengthening the competition tools

A minor change will give the Secretary of State the power to request the CMA to investigate public interest issues [see page 30of the government’s response (PDF, 591 Kb)] alongside competition issues in a market investigation.

In order to ensure greater certainty and to reduce the burden to business, the government proposes introducing or reducing statutory time limits at all stages of market investigations. This will be supported by information gathering powers at all stages.

Statutory time limits will also be introduced at all stages of the mergers process [see page 44 of the government’s response (PDF, 591 Kb)].

2) Anti-trust and Cartels

The system for enforcing the legal prohibitions in anti-trust (abuse of market dominance and other anti-competitive agreements) and cartel cases (eg agreement between companies to fix prices) contained in the Competition Act 1998 (‘anti-trust’) is being strengthened. Proposals include statutory change to bolster separation between investigation and decision making and new powers for the CMA to improve the efficiency of investigations and transparency of the decision making process [see page 54 of the Government’s response (PDF, 591 Kb)].

This is work currently carried out by the OFT. Appeals against decisions by the OFT (and the CMA once the reforms have been fully introduced) can be made to the Competition Appeal Tribunal

3) Sectoral regulation and ‘concurrency’

Sectoral regulators already have the power to refer cases to the OFT but rarely use them. We want to encourage the CMA to be a more proactive central resource for these regulators.

Some sectoral regulators have ‘concurrent competition powers’, ie they have a choice between using their own sectoral powers and making use of the 1998 Competition Act to refer issues to the OFT. We want to strengthen the primacy of the Competition Act by requiring sectoral regulators with concurrent powers to consider that route first; and also to give the CMA the power to take Competition Act cases from the sectoral regulators where it is better placed to proceed with the case.

Competition complaints

If you believe a company or group of companies are acting anti-competitively, then you may wish to consider making a complaint to the OFT via its special hotline and email address for complaints under the Competition Act:

Competition Act Enquiry Line: 08457 22 44 99 e-mail enquiries: enquiries.competitionact@oft.gov.uk

There is also a detailed how to complain section on the OFT website. This gives examples of behaviour which may indicate that a company is breaching the Competition Act. It also gives information on what to do in order to complain.
