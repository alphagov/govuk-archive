Part 6: Inheritance Tax when someone living outside the UK dies

When someone living abroad dies, the rules for paying Inheritance Tax usually depend on the following:

  • where they were domiciled
  • where their assets are situated
  • if they have any ‘excluded assets’
  • if the assets were settled (put in trust)

Domicile

In general, someone is domiciled in the country where they have their permanent home. A person can only have one domicile at a time.

When someone from the UK moves abroad their domicile will remain the UK for Inheritance Tax purposes until they’ve decided to choose a new domicile and have met all the following conditions:

  • they’re at least 16 years old
  • they’ve left the UK with the intention to settle permanently in another country
  • they’ve not been domiciled (ie had their permanent home) in the UK for at least 3 years
  • they’ve not been resident in the UK for more than 16 of the last 20 Income Tax years

If they meet these conditions the person is ‘deemed domiciled’ in their adopted country.

A change of domicile will only be recognised if there’s strong evidence that someone intends to live abroad indefinitely, eg they own a property or have business interests.

What assets are subject to Inheritance Tax

Where the deceased was domiciled Inheritance Tax is due on assets
In the UK Everywhere
Outside the UK In the UK (except excluded assets)

Settled assets

If there are assets outside the UK that were put in trust while the grantor (the person who set up the trust) was in the UK, Inheritance Tax is payable on these.

Excluded assets

Certain assets situated in the UK are excluded from Inheritance Tax for those domiciled abroad, including:

  • British government securities if they weren’t ordinarily resident in the UK (ie spent less than 183 days of the year in the UK)
  • holdings in authorised unit trusts and open-ended investment companies
  • property they put in trust when they were domiciled outside the UK
  • non-sterling accounts with a bank or the Post Office
  • reversionary interest (where their property was granted to them for the duration of their life, with the condition that when they died it would revert to the person who gave it to them)
  • for those domiciled in the Channel Islands or the Isle of Man, National Savings Certificates or certain other forms of small savings

Double taxation treaties

If the country where the deceased was living charges Inheritance Tax on the same property the UK is taxing, you might be able to avoid or reclaim the tax through a double taxation treaty.
