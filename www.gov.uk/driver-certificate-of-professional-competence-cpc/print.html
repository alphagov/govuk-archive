Driver Certificate of Professional Competence (Driver CPC), a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

If you’re a new professional bus, coach or lorry driver in most cases you must pass the initial Driver Certificate of Professional Competence (Driver CPC) qualification before driving professionally.

You’ll get a Driver Qualification Card (DQC) which you’ll need to keep with you when you’re driving professionally.

If you’re already a professional bus or lorry driver you might not have to do the tests and can get Driver CPC through ‘acquired rights’.

There are also some other situations when you don’t need Driver CPC.

Keeping your Driver CPC

You’ll need to do 35 hours of periodic training every 5 years to keep your Driver CPC.

Buses and coaches are known as Passenger Carrying Vehicles (PCV) and lorries are called Large Goods Vehicles (LGV).

Part 2: The initial Driver CPC qualification

The qualification is split into 4 parts:

  • part 1 - theory test (multiple-choice and hazard perception)
  • part 2 - case studies
  • part 3 - practical test of driving ability
  • part 4 - vehicle safety demonstration

You must pass all 4 parts to get your Driver Certificate of Professional Competence (Driver CPC).

Part 3: Booking Driver CPC

You can book parts 1 and 2 online using the theory test booking service.

You can book parts 3 and 4 online using the practical test booking service.

As well as booking online, the links have details of how to book by phone or by post.

Part 4: Driver CPC fees

Driver Certificate of Professional Competence (Driver CPC) initial qualification test fees

Part 1(a) - theory test (multiple choice) £35
Part 1(b) - theory test (hazard perception) £15
Part 2 - theory test (case studies) £30
Part 3 - vocational licence acquisition practical test (driving ability) £115
Part 4 - Driver CPC practical test (vehicle safety demonstration) £55 weekday, £63 evening or weekend

Driver Qualification Card (DQC) fees

DQC (non-UK driving licences only) £25
Replacement for lost, stolen or damaged DQC £25

Part 5: The Driver Qualification Card (DQC)

After you’ve passed the initial qualification you’ll get a Driver Qualification Card (DQC).

You must carry your DQC while driving a large goods vehicle (LGV) or passenger carrying vehicle (PCV).

If you’ve got your Driver Certificate of Professional Competence (Driver CPC) through ‘acquired rights’ you won’t get a DQC and your licence is proof of Driver CPC.

You don’t need to keep your licence with you, but if asked by the police, you must show it at a later date as proof of your ‘acquired rights’.

Getting your DQC

If you have a photocard licence

You’ll get your DQC when you’ve completed either your initial qualification or your 35 hours of periodic training if you have a Great Britain (GB) photocard licence. It will be sent to the address on your driving licence.

If you have a paper licence

If you have a paper licence you’ll need to swap it for a photocard licence before you can get your DQC. This is because the DQC needs a photograph and signature, which are taken from the photocard details.

Applying for a replacement DQC

If your DQC has been lost, stolen or damaged you should write to or email the Driving Standards Agency (DSA).

Tell DSA your:

  • name
  • driving licence number
  • address
  • telephone number

DSA will then call you back. You’ll need a credit or debit card to pay for the replacement.

You can still drive professionally in the UK while you’re waiting for your replacement DQC to arrive.

Part 6: Getting Driver CPC through 'acquired rights'

If you’re an existing professional driver, you could get Driver CPC through ‘acquired rights’. This means that because you’ve already been working as a lorry or bus driver your experience is counted as equivalent to taking the Driver CPC tests.

You’ll still need to complete 35 hours of periodic training every 5 years to keep your Driver CPC.

This applies if you are a:

  • bus or coach driver and got your vocational licence (D, D1, D+E and D1+E) before 10 September 2008
  • lorry driver and got your vocational licence (C, C1, C+E and C1+E) before 10 September 2009

When you need to complete periodic training

If you’ve got your Driver CPC by ‘acquired rights’ you must do 35 hours of periodic training by:

  • 9 September 2013 if you are a bus or coach driver
  • 9 September 2014 if you are a lorry driver

Licences where you don’t have ‘acquired rights’

You don’t have ‘acquired rights’ if you have a D, D1, D+E or D1+E licence with ‘not for hire or reward’ or ‘101’ on it.

You will have to take the Driver CPC initial qualification as normal to drive professionally, and then do periodic training.

Driving a larger or different type of vehicle and adding trailer entitlements

If you’ve already got a vocational licence and want to add a trailer entitlement or drive a larger vehicle of the same type you won’t have ‘acquired rights’ to do this automatically, but will only have to pass Driver CPC modules 1 and 3.

If you have only a lorry or bus licence and want to drive both, you don’t have ‘acquired rights’ to do so. You’ll need to pass all 4 modules or the relevant bus or lorry initial Driver CPC qualification to start driving the new type of vehicle professionally.

Part 7: Driver CPC periodic training

You must do 35 hours of periodic training every 5 years to keep your Driver Certificate of Professional Competence (CPC).

You can check how many hours you’ve done in the current 5-year period.

How to find training courses

Only approved courses taken with approved training centres will count towards periodic training. It’s up to you to decide which to attend.

There is no test to pass and courses can be practical or classroom based.

If you drive both lorries and buses professionally you only need to complete 1 set of training every 5 years.

When to take Driver CPC periodic training

It’s up to you when you take the training courses, as long as you do them within 5 years.

When the 5-year period starts

Each 5-year period starts from 5 years before your current Driver CPC qualification runs out - not from the date you completed your last 35 hours of training.

For all ‘acquired rights’ drivers it started on:

  • 10 September 2008 for category D, D1, D+E and D1+E drivers
  • 10 September 2009 for category C, C1, C+E and C1+E drivers

If you’ve got bus and lorry entitlements and want to keep your Driver CPC for both, you must complete your 35 hours of periodic training in the 5 years before the earliest expiry date.

Part 8: When you don’t need Driver CPC

You might not need Driver Certificate of Professional Competence (Driver CPC), even if you’re driving a large goods vehicle (LGV) or passenger carrying vehicle (PCV). It depends on:

  • the vehicle
  • what the vehicle is being used for

Vehicles you can drive without Driver CPC

You don’t need Driver CPC if the vehicle you’re driving is:

  • being road tested after repair, maintenance or technical development
  • new or rebuilt and not been put into service yet
  • limited to a top speed of 28mph

Vehicle uses where you don’t need Driver CPC

You don’t need Driver CPC if the vehicle you drive is:

  • used for non-commercial carriage of passengers or goods for personal use
  • used to carry material or equipment you use for your job - but driving the vehicle can’t be the main part of your job
  • used for driving lessons for anyone who wants to get a driving licence or a Driver CPC
  • used by, or is under the control of, the armed forces, civil defence, the fire service and forces responsible for maintaining public order
  • used in states of emergency or for rescue missions

Part 9: National Vocational Training (NVT) concessions

If you’re taking certain National Vocational Training (NVT) driving courses you can drive professionally for up to 12 months. This is called an ‘NVT concession’.

During this time you must pass the Driver CPC parts 2 and 4. If you don’t you must then stop driving professionally.

You can only use the NVT concession once.

It’s valid from the start of the NVT programme or when you get your vocational driving licence, whichever is the most recent.

Find out more about NVT concessions on the Businesslink website.
