Preventing health problems and accidents among employees, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

You must do everything you reasonably can to prevent employees from having health problems and accidents in the workplace.

These rules also apply to employees who work from home or from different locations.

Health problems and accidents can be caused by:

  • materials
  • equipment
  • working practices

Health and safety actions

You can help prevent health problems and accidents by:

  • carrying out a risk assessment
  • controlling, reducing and monitoring the use of hazardous substances
  • preventing smoking on work premises
  • putting together a first aid kit
  • getting your staff involved through consultations
  • preparing for health and safety inspections

Part 2: Manual handling

Manual handling means lifting or carrying loads without mechanical assistance - ie using your arms and/or legs.

This can be hazardous when:

  • a load is too heavy, large or difficult to grasp
  • lifting involves awkward movements
  • lifting takes place in difficult conditions - eg on slippery or uneven floors, or in extreme temperatures

Where manual handling under these conditions is unavoidable, you must:

  • minimise and if possible get rid of the need for it
  • assess any risk of injury
  • reduce any risk of injury as much as possible

How to avoid manual handling

If you don’t have automated or mechanical tools (eg conveyers or pallet trucks) to help with lifting or moving loads, consider getting some. If you don’t you must find other ways to avoid manual handling health problems and accidents, eg by assessing and reducing the risk to your staff.

Part 3: Hazardous substances

If your employees must work with hazardous substances as part of their job, you must take steps to safeguard their health.

Industries like manufacturing and motor vehicle repair pose clear risks, but many other jobs - eg hairdressing and catering - can also expose workers to hazardous substances.

There are a clear-cut set of rules on protecting your employees from these risks.

For further information download the Health and Safety Executive’s brochure on controlling hazardous substances.

Download ‘What you need to know about controlling hazardous substances’ (PDF, 923KB)

Part 4: Slip and trip hazards

You must make sure that your employees are safe when they move around your premises by checking all areas that could cause them to slip or trip.

Flooring

You must check for any hazards including loose, cracked or worn flooring, and replace where necessary. Look out for other hazards such as liquid, grease, dirt or dust on floors, and promptly clean up any you find.

Choose your flooring according to how the area will be used.

Lighting

Maintain lighting so employees can clearly see any obstructions or other hazards like wet or slippery floors, or steps.

You must also clean the lighting regularly and change bulbs when needed.

Cluttered areas

Clear clutter from floors and doorways. If some things can’t be moved out of the way, put up notices to warn staff.

Footwear

You must give your employees footwear if it’s necessary to protect their safety. If they work in areas where floors can’t be kept dry, for example, they must have non-slip shoes or boots.

If footwear is supplied as personal protective equipment (PPE), it must be the right size, comfortable and fit for purpose. It must also be free to employees.

Managing workflow

Make sure work is organised to avoid rushing or overcrowding. Keep employees informed about and involved in decisions that affect them, such as changes in cleaning methods.

Part 5: Using work equipment safely

Work equipment covers any equipment used while at work, including:

  • machines - eg circular saws, photocopiers, tractors
  • hand tools - eg screwdrivers, knives, meat cleavers
  • lifting equipment - eg vehicle hoists, lifting slings, bath lifts
  • other equipment - eg ladders, water pressure cleaners

All new equipment must meet European safety standards.

For more information, download the Health and Safety Executive’s brochure on the safe use of work equipment.

Download ‘Using work equipment safely’ (PDF, 194KB)

Assessing equipment

Examine the equipment you or your employees use, look at the risks and think of ways to reduce or remove those risks.

Work equipment must be:

  • suitable for use
  • regularly maintained
  • inspected (before first use and then regularly)
  • assessed for noise and vibration

Training employees

Prevent staff from endangering themselves or others by making sure:

  • they’re properly trained to use the equipment
  • instructions are in plain English

Buying new equipment

Make sure any new equipment you buy meets safety standards.

Part 6: Working with computers

If your employees work on computers you should:

  • carry out a risk assessment of the hazards to those who use computer monitors - sometimes called visual display units (VDUs)
  • make sure computer workstations meet safety standards, eg have adjustable chairs and suitable lighting
  • give employees health and safety training and information

Download ‘Working with VDUs’ (PDF, 102KB)

Breaks

You must give your employees who use computer monitors breaks away from their screens. Employees who spend most of their time at a computer monitor should have longer breaks.

Adjusting equipment

You must also make reasonable adjustments for disabled workers or employees who need certain equipment - eg making a desk higher or lower - so they can use the equipment safely.

Free eye tests

You must either give free sight tests or cover the cost of a sight test for employees who work with computer monitors.

You must also cover the cost of:

  • other tests recommended by an optician
  • a basic pair of glasses if your staff need them for work on monitors

These rules also apply to employees who work from home or from different locations.

Part 7: Personal protective equipment (PPE)

You must give your employees suitable personal protective equipment (PPE) where it can help protect them from a risk to their health or safety. PPE includes:

  • safety helmets
  • high-visibility clothing
  • safety footwear

Download ‘A short guide to Personal Protective Equipment at Work Regulations 1992’ (PDF, 77KB)

Before providing PPE, you must check if the equipment:

  • meets European safety standards and carries the CE mark
  • is suitable for use - eg protective boots are the right size
  • is maintained properly and is in full working order
  • is used correctly by employees
  • is the right equipment for specific jobs
  • includes clear instructions for use
