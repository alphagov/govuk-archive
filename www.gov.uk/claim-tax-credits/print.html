How to claim tax credits, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: How to claim

If you’re already claiming tax credits you don’t need a claim form - just call the helpline to update your claim.

If you’re new to tax credits, contact the Tax Credit Helpline for a claim form. You can’t apply online or download the form and it takes up to 2 weeks to arrive.

You can apply for tax credits as a single person or as a couple (known as a ‘joint claim’).

Tax Credit Helpline

Telephone: 0345 300 3900
Textphone: 0345 300 3909
Outside the UK: +44 2890 538 192
Monday to Friday, 8am to 8pm
Saturday 8am to 4pm

What happens when you call

Before you’re sent the form, you’ll be asked for the following and given an estimate of how much you may get:

  • your National Insurance number (if you have one)
  • your income for the last tax year
  • details of any benefits you get
  • details of any childcare payments

Where to send the form

The address is on the form. If you claim benefits you can give it to your Jobcentre Plus, or Social Security Office if you live in Northern Ireland.

Penalties

You may be charged a penalty (up to £3,000) if you give wrong information:

  • fraudulently (on purpose)
  • negligently (you didn’t take enough care)

You won’t get a penalty if you made a mistake but took reasonable care when making a claim.

Get help with your application

You’re sent guidance notes with your claim form. You can also get help from the Tax Credit Office, your benefits office or an organisation like Citizens Advice.

If you want someone else to talk to the Tax Credit Office for you - you must authorise them.

Part 2: When to claim

Eligibility

Use the tax credits calculator to check what you’re entitled to.

Deadlines

You can claim tax credits at any time of the year.

Usually, tax credits are backdated by 1 month from the date of your claim.

Apply as soon as you know you’re eligible to get all the money you’re entitled to.

Example: baby born 1 September
You claim tax credits on 1 January so your tax credits get backdated to 1 December.

If you’d claimed them by 1 October you’d have been getting tax credits from 1 September.

Applying if you’re not eligible

If you know your income will drop to a level that means you qualify for tax credits they can be backdated to either:

  • 6 April - if your claim is received by 5 May
  • 1 month before your claim - if your claim is received after 5 May

You might know your income is going to drop if, for example, you or your partner are being made redundant, leaving a job or taking a pay cut.

The income levels for Working Tax Credit and Child Tax Credit are different.

Part 3: Joint claims

You can apply for tax credits as a single person or as a couple (known as a ‘joint claim’).

You must make a joint claim if:

  • you’re married or in a civil partnership
  • you’re not permanently or legally separated
  • you live with your partner as though you’re married or in a civil partnership
  • you’re temporarily living away from your partner - eg to look after a relative
  • your partner lives with you in the UK but works abroad
  • you and your partner live abroad but work in the UK and don’t have children

Exception

Claim as a single person if all the following apply:

  • you regularly travel to or from the UK for work
  • you don’t live in the UK
  • you have no children
  • your partner doesn’t work in the UK (if you have one)

If you don’t make the right sort of application you must pay back any tax credits you’re not entitled to.

Part 4: Backdate a claim

When you make a claim your tax credits are backdated by 1 month.

How to get tax credits backdated

Usually, you don’t have to do anything - it happens automatically. The date they’re backdated to is shown on your award notice.

Exceptions

You have to ask for your tax credits to be backdated if you get:

  • Income Support
  • Jobseeker’s Allowance (income-based)
  • Employment and Support Allowance (income-related)
  • Pension Credit
  • Working Tax Credit and you’re not claiming for any children

To do this include a page with your claim form confirming:

  • your name, address and National Insurance number
  • the date you started work
  • if you’re not in work - the date you started getting one of the benefits above

Backdating more than a month

Claims can be backdated by more than a month if:

  • you or your child start to get a disability benefit - eg Disability Living Allowance
  • you start to get a sickness benefit - eg Statutory Sick Pay
  • you get refugee status

You must send your claim within 1 month of qualifying for the above. The Tax Credit Office will tell you how much more you’ll get.

Part 5: What counts as income

Usually, what you’re entitled to is based on your income for the last tax year (6 April 2011 to 5 April 2012).

Income includes:

  • work - your earnings before tax and national insurance (check your P60 or payslips)
  • certain benefits
  • money from a pension - including your state pension
  • interest on savings - if it’s more £300 a year
  • your partner’s income - if you make a joint claim

Income includes the money you get from state benefits but not the following:

  • Child Benefit
  • Housing Benefit
  • Council Tax Benefit
  • Attendance Allowance
  • Disability Living Allowance

The guidance notes sent with your claim form tell you how to work out your income.

To support your claim, keep records about your income, bills, payslips, benefits, tax credits and child care.
