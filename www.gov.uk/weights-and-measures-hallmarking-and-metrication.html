Weights and measures: hallmarking and metrication

How the National Measurement Office (NMO) regulates weights, measures and hallmarking of precious metals.

Introduction

The National Measurement Office (NMO) is an executive agency of the Department for Business, Innovation & Skills.

It works to develop a regulatory regime to promote standards in weights and measurements, and quality standards of precious metals - hallmarking - to help ensure that consumers and businesses are protected and international trading is fair.

This guide explains the legislation surrounding weights and measures and hallmarking, the role of Assay Offices, the Hallmarking Convention and the Common Control Mark. It also details the implications for businesses when selling or trading articles made of precious metals, and gives sources of further information.

Better regulation

The National Measurement Office (NMO) has a rigorous approach to regulation, especially towards industries that would have the biggest impact if they didn’t comply with regulations, such as the medical and manufacturing industries.

Regulation plays a crucial role in:

  • correcting market failures
  • promoting fairness
  • maintaining and increasing competition

Better regulation should be:

  • transparent
  • accountable
  • proportionate
  • consistent
  • targeted only at cases where action is needed

The NMO’s weights and measures and hallmarking regulatory regime provides information to, and protection for, consumers and businesses, and helps to minimise the costs of compliance for business.

Weights and measurement regulation

The NMO’s aims are protection for consumers and fair compliance costs for businesses. Consistent units of measurement allow consumers to make easy price and quantity comparisons when it’s difficult to check the quantity they’re paying for, eg when buying petrol. Businesses, too, expect a consistent and proportionate enforcement regime.

The European Commission Units of Measurement Directive 1989 has called for a metric system since 1 January 1995 for measuring instruments used, measurements made and for quantities applied to economics, public health or safety or administration. Businesses that previously used imperial units could be excluded from trading with other member states until they convert to metric units.

Hallmarking regulation

The Hallmarking Convention of 1972 is an international treaty which sets technical requirements for Common Control Marks (CCMs), and enables cross-border trade between Convention members without the need for additional UK hallmarking. Download Hallmarking Convention information from the Hallmarking Convention website (PDF, 1.18MB).

The British Hallmarking Council advises the Secretary of State on hallmarking matters and directs the Assay Offices as to the regulations for standards, practices and procedures.

Hallmarking in business

Precious metals are usually too soft to be used in a pure form, so they must be mixed (alloyed) with harder metals. Hallmarking involves testing and marking items made in or imported into the UK that are made of precious metal - gold, silver and platinum and, since 22 July 2009, palladium - to certify their metallurgical content (‘fineness’).

Tests are carried out in the UK by Assay Offices in London, Birmingham, Sheffield and Edinburgh.

Anyone who sends an article to be hallmarked by a UK Assay Office will need to be registered as a sponsor by that Assay Office. If a sponsor sends goods to be marked by a different Assay Office, they will need to be registered there too.

The three compulsory hallmarks are:

  • the sponsor’s mark, indicating manufacturer, importer or distributor of the article
  • the standard mark indicating type and fineness of metal
  • the Assay Office mark

Optional hallmarks are:

  • a pictorial mark
  • a date mark - although this is still compulsory in some Assay Offices

The penalty for breaking hallmarking regulations can be a fine or up to two years in prison. Penalties are enforced by local Trading Standards departments.

Exemptions

Goods made with precious metals do not need to be hallmarked by an Assay Office if they:

  • weigh less than 0.5 grams (platinum), 1 gram (gold or palladium) or 7.5 grams (silver)
  • carry the Hallmarking Convention Common Control Mark
  • have other European national hallmarks which are recognised by the UK and which bear equivalent information to that carried by UK marks

Metrication in business

The Weights and Measures Act of 1985 regulates common consumer goods that are sold by weight, volume or number. This includes most groceries (foods and non-foods), and many DIY goods and related products.

The metric system of units of measurements is an international decimalised measurement system. Since 1960, the International System of Units - a system that came from the convenience of using the number ten - has been the internationally recognised standard metric system.

Metrication is the process of introducing metric units for measurement of weight, length and volume etc. The UK agreed to adopt metric weights and measures in 1965, and has adopted metric units in a series of stages since then.

Metric units are used for most transactions and it is government policy to use this system of measurement for other purposes, for example public administration.

It has been compulsory to sell packaged goods in metric weight or volume (in grams and kilograms or millilitres and litres) since 1995 and loose goods or goods in bulk in metric weight or volume (grams and kilograms or millilitres and litres) since 2000. However, in 2009 a new directive was agreed which stated that metric-only labelling was no longer compulsory - for easier trading with the US. Duel labelling (in metric and imperial units) can now continue indefinitely.

Further information on metrication and hallmarking legislation

The following organisations can help you with queries about legislation relating to metrication or hallmarking.

National Measurement Office (NMO)

International Organisation of Legal Metrology

The International Organisation of Legal Metrology (OIML) promotes global harmonisation of legal metrology so that instrument manufacturers’ products will be acceptable in markets around the world. The OIML is an intergovernmental organisation that currently represents 116 countries. It produces ‘recommendations’ or model laws which form the basis of countries’ legal metrology laws.

You can read about the structure and role of the OIML on the OIML website.

National Physical Laboratory (NPL)

The NPL is the UK’s National Measurement Institute. It carries out research into new measurement techniques, technology and best practice for measurement standards, science and technology which underlie quality of life, innovation and competitiveness for UK businesses and consumers.

You can read about NPL’s measurement services on the NPL website.

WELMEC

WELMEC - originally the Western European Legal Metrology Cooperation - aims to establish a harmonised and consistent approach to European legal metrology. Founded in 1990, WELMEC now has member representatives from Central, Eastern, Northern and Southern Europe. Its aims are to:

  • develop mutual confidence between legal metrology services
  • identify areas of legal metrology which need to be reflected in the European metrology, certification and testing framework
  • organise the exchange of information
  • promote the removal of barriers to trade in the field of measuring instruments
  • identify technical problems which might form the subject of collaborative projects
  • maintain working links with other relevant bodies with an interest in legal metrology

You can download an information pack about WELMEC from the WELMEC website (PDF, 114K).
