Benefits & credits: Attendance Allowance

Notes

Part 1: Overview

You could get £51.85 or £77.45 a week to help with personal care because you’re physically or mentally disabled and you’re aged 65 or over.

This is called Attendance Allowance. It’s paid at 2 different rates and how much you get depends on the level of care that you need because of your disability.

The other benefits you get can increase if you get Attendance Allowance.

If you’re the carer, you could apply for Carer’s Allowance to help you look after someone with substantial caring needs.

Part 2: What you'll get

Attendance Allowance is paid weekly at 2 different rates - the one you get depends on the level of help you need.

Rate Level of help you need
Lower rate - £51.85 Frequent help and constant supervision during the day, or supervision at night
Higher rate - £77.45 Help or supervision throughout both day and night, or you’re terminally ill

How you’re paid

All benefits, pensions and allowances are paid into an account, eg your bank account.

Changes of circumstances

It’s your responsibility to tell the helpline if your circumstances change, as this can affect how much you get. For example, you or the person you claim for needs more help, goes into hospital or moves abroad.

Usually your Attendance Allowance won’t be affected if you go:

  • into hospital or a care home for less than 4 weeks
  • abroad for less than 26 weeks
  • abroad to get medical treatment for a condition which began before you left

You could get extra Pension Credit, Housing Benefit or Council Tax Benefit if you get Attendance Allowance - check with the helpline or office dealing with your benefit.

Part 3: Eligibility

You can get Attendance Allowance if you’re 65 or over and the following apply:

  • you have a physical disability (including sensory disability, eg blindness), a mental disability (including learning difficulties), or both
  • your disability is severe enough for you to need help caring for yourself or someone to supervise you, for your own or someone else’s safety

Use the benefits adviser to check your eligibility.

Medical assessments

You might get a letter saying you need to attend an assessment to check your eligibility. The letter will explain why and where you must go.

At the assessment, you’ll be asked for identification. You can use a passport or any 3 of the following:

  • birth certificate
  • a full driving licence
  • life assurance policy
  • bank statements

Part 4: How to claim

You can apply for Attendance Allowance:

  • online
  • by post - use form AA1A, the address is on the form
  • by phone

Form AA1A comes with notes to fill it out. The interactive version can be filled out on a computer.

What you need to know

Attendance Allowance can be backdated to the date of your claim. This is usually the date your form is received or the date you call the enquiry line (if you return the claim pack within 6 weeks).

It usually takes 40 working days to deal with your claim. It can be quicker if you’re terminally ill.

You might have to have a medical assessment to complete your application.

Special rules - if you’re terminally ill

If you’re not expected to live more than 6 months, there are ‘special rules’ so you get Attendance Allowance more quickly. You must:

  • complete an Attendance Allowance claim form
  • include a DS1500 form - you can only get these from a doctor, specialist or consultant

You can do this on behalf of someone else without their permission. The letter about the money awarded won’t mention ‘special rules’.

Appeal a decision

You can ask for your Attendance Allowance decision to be reconsidered if you’re unhappy with it, or you can appeal it.
