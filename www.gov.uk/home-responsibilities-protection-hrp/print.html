Benefits & credits: Home Responsibilities Protection

Notes

Part 1: Overview

Home Responsibilities Protection (HRP) was a scheme to help protect a person’s State Pension. It has been replaced with National Insurance credits for parents and carers.

You may still be able to apply for HRP if you were:

  • caring for a sick or disabled person for a complete tax year before April 2010
  • a foster carer for a complete tax year before April 2010

National Insurance credits for parents and carers replaced HRP from 6 April 2010. If you reach State Pension age on or after 6 April 2010 and you had years of HRP before 6 April 2010, these years have been converted into National Insurance credits, up to a maximum of 22 years. These will go towards your basic State Pension.

Part 2: What you'll get

The amount of basic State Pension you may become entitled to at State Pension age is based on the number of years of National Insurance contributions you have.

If you reached State Pension age before 6 April 2010, Home Responsibilities Protection (HRP) worked by reducing the number of qualifying years you needed for a full basic State Pension. HRP couldn’t reduce the number of qualifying years below 20.

Example

A woman reaching State Pension age before 6 April 2010 normally needed 39 qualifying years for a full basic State Pension. If she had 10 full years of HRP these were deducted from 39. This meant that she only needed 29 qualifying years for a full basic State Pension.

You may also be entitled to additional State Pension if you qualified for HRP because you:

  • got Child Benefit for a child under the age of 6
  • were looking after a sick or disabled person (including a child over the age of 6)

Part 3: Eligibility

Depending on your circumstances, either you qualified automatically for Home Responsibilities Protection (HRP) or you will have to apply.

Qualifying automatically for HRP

Most people got HRP automatically if they were:

  • getting Child Benefit in their name for a child under the age of 16 and they had given the Child Benefit Office their National Insurance number
  • getting Income Support and they didn’t need to register for work because they were caring for someone who was sick or disabled

Applying for HRP

You have to apply for HRP if you :

  • were regularly spending at least 35 hours a week looking after someone getting Attendance Allowance, Disability Living Allowance at the middle or highest rate for personal care, or Constant Attendance Allowance
  • were a registered foster carer throughout a full tax year from April 2003 and were not getting Child Benefit and were not in paid work, or did not earn enough in a tax year for it to count towards the basic State Pension
  • think HRP is missing from your record

Who wasn’t eligible

In the following circumstances you would not usually have got HRP.

Carer’s Allowance

If you get Carer’s Allowance you’ll automatically get National Insurance credits and would not usually have needed HRP.

Married women or widows

If you were a married woman or a widow, you couldn’t get HRP for any complete tax year if:

  • you had chosen to pay reduced rate Class 1 National Insurance contributions as an employee (commonly known as the small stamp)
  • you had chosen not to pay Class 2 National Insurance contributions when self-employed

Part 4: How to claim

You may still be able to apply for Home Responsibilities Protection (HRP) if you were:

  • caring for a sick or disabled person for a complete tax year before April 2010
  • a foster carer for a complete tax year between April 2003 and April 2010

To claim HRP, download application form CF411 and CF411 notes from the HM Revenue & Customs website.

Foster carers

When you fill in form CF411, if you’re a foster carer you need to give a letter from your local council or the agency you worked for. This is to confirm you were an approved foster carer throughout the full tax year.

When to claim

If you’re claiming HRP for years you’ve spent caring for someone with a long-term illness or disability between April 1978 and April 2002, you can claim at any time. If you’re over State Pension age you can still apply. You will not normally be paid any increase of State Pension that may have been due for previous years.

From April 2002 onwards, HRP has had to be claimed within 3 years of the end of any tax year you spent caring for someone with a long-term illness or disability.

Part 5: Further information

Changes from April 2010 for parents and carers

Home Responsibilities Protection (HRP) has been replaced for people reaching State Pension age on or after 6 April 2010.

From 6 April 2010, parents and carers may become entitled to basic State Pension and additional State Pension through new weekly credits for the basic State Pension and additional State Pension. If you’re a parent or carer, you may get a credit for each week in which you:

  • are getting Child Benefit for children aged under 12
  • are an approved foster carer
  • are caring for at least 20 hours a week for people who are getting Attendance Allowance, the middle-rate or highest-rate care component of Disability Living Allowance, or Constant Attendance Allowance, or the need for care has been certified

There will be no limit to the credits awarded to parents and carers after April 2010, as long as you meet the qualifying rules.

If you reach State Pension age on or after 6 April 2010, complete tax years of HRP have been converted into qualifying years up to a maximum of 22 years. These qualifying years will also count towards bereavement benefits.
