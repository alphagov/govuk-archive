Importing live fish, molluscs and crustacea

Authorisations and documentation you need in order to import live fish, molluscs and crustacea, steps you must take when exporting and what to do if errors occur.

Introduction

Before you can import live fish, molluscs and/or crustacea into the UK, you must be authorised as an aquaculture production business by the Fish Health Inspectorate (FHI).

All consignments of fish, molluscs and/or crustacea from outside the European Union (EU) need to be accompanied by a health certificate. This is issued by the competent authority in the country of origin and confirms the health status of the fish, molluscs or crustacea in the consignment.

If you are importing fish, molluscs and/or crustacea from within the EU, your consignments only need to be accompanied by a health certificate if they include species that are susceptible to, or carriers of, an EU controlled disease. In both cases you need to notify the appropriate authority before your imports arrive.

This guide explains the authorisations and documentation you need in order to import live fish, molluscs and crustacea. It also gives guidance on what happens if errors occur, and the steps you must take when exporting.

Authorisation and documentation for live fish, mollusc and crustacea importers

Anyone importing live fish, molluscs and crustacea into Great Britain needs to be authorised by the FHI. This includes imports intended for:

  • farming
  • relaying
  • ‘put and take’ fisheries
  • restocking
  • open and closed ornamental facilities, including pets and purchases for own collection
  • scientific research
  • public aquaria and zoos
  • spas and medical use (nibble fish)

If you hold fish, molluscs or crustacea on your own site, your application to be authorised may need to include a biosecurity measures plan. For guidance on biosecurity measures plans, you can download biosecurity measures guidance for importers from the Aquatic Animal Health and Movements (AAHM) website (PDF, 453KB).

Authorisation is free of charge and you can apply using form AAH2. You can download FHI form AAH2 from the AAHM website (PDF, 51KB).

Importing live fish, mollusc and crustacea from outside the EU

Countries that are not in the EU or a member of the European Free Trade Association (EFTA) are treated as ‘third countries’ for import purposes.

Imports of fish, molluscs and crustacea from third countries must be accompanied by a health certificate issued by the competent authority in the country of origin. The authority must follow model documents issued by the EU and will often charge for the service.

Imports that arrive in Great Britain directly from a third country must go through a border inspection post (BIP) where the health certificate is inspected by Animal Health (AH). You will need to inform AH about your import at least one clear working day in advance using a Common Veterinary Entry Document (CVED). You can download a CVED notification form from the AAHM website (PDF, 67KB). For details of the timescales to observe when using a CVED and also for contact details of BIPs in Great Britain, download the CVED timetable for notifications of intended imports from the AAHM website (PDF, 35KB).

Importing live fish, molluscs and crustacea from within the EU

Different rules apply for movements from EU and EFTA member countries. Health certification is only required if the species being imported is either a vector of - or is susceptible to - an EU controlled disease. You can read a list of controlled diseases, and vector and susceptible species on the Scottish Government website.

You must also notify the FHI of all movements of fish, molluscs or crustacea into Great Britain from EU and EFTA member countries using form AAH1, except tropical species for ornamental use. Download FHI form AAH1 from the AAHM website (PDF, 265KB).

However, although the Isle of Man, Northern Ireland, Jersey and Guernsey are all part of the UK, they are recognised as separate ‘health zones’ to Great Britain. This means that movements from these areas must be accompanied by any necessary health certificates.

Fish and shellfish intended for human consumption

If you import live or dead fish and shellfish for immediate human consumption or immediate processing for human consumption, your business doesn’t need to be authorised. But if your imports are from outside the EU, they will require health and hygiene certification. You can find out about importing fish and shellfish for human consumption on the Food Standards Agency (FSA) website.

Additional import requirements and post import controls

In addition to general import requirements, there are other controls that may be required when importing endangered and non-native species. And there are also controls when you introduce species to UK waters or export them.

Endangered species

There are some species that are recognised world-wide as being endangered. The Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES) is an international agreement designed to control the import, export and commercial use of these species. If you want to import any species that are on the CITES list you will need a CITES permit. You can find out about CITES on the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra) website.

Additional controls

Imports of certain species into the UK are controlled by additional licensing regimes. These include:

  • non-native crayfish - see the guide on non-native crayfish and lobster deposits 
  • non-native fish - see the guide on non-native fish 

Movement controls

For all introductions of fish or shellfish into open waters in the UK, you must get ‘Section 30’ consent. For more information, see the guide on moving fish and shellfish.

If you are planning on exporting fish from the UK, there are certain steps you need to take. These depend on whether you are exporting to another EU member state or to a country outside the EU.

When importing errors occur

Sometimes, a mistake can occur with the legal process of importing live fish, molluscs or crustacea into Great Britain. While the consignment is at a border inspection post an error with the consignment or the accompanying documentation may be identified. For example:

  • some or all of the documentation may become lost in transit
  • errors may occur in the quantity of animals in the consignment
  • errors might happen in the accompanying health certificate
  • the health certificate may be incomplete

What is a Regulation 23?

A ‘Regulation 23’ is the name given to a notice issued under Regulation 23(1) of the Animal and Animal Products (Import and Export) Regulations 2006. A Regulation 23 allows animals, including fish, molluscs or crustacea, to enter Great Britain even though there has been an error with the import process. It will be issued to prevent a consignment of animals being destroyed, as long as they can be isolated well enough to pose a low health risk to Great Britain.

Who is a Regulation 23 issued to?

A Regulation 23 is served on the importer of the consignment. This is because they are the person who appears to be in charge of the animals at the time of the importation and when the veterinary checks were made. As an importer, it is your responsibility to make sure that all the terms of the Regulation 23 are complied with.

Isolation of the consignment

Before the Regulation 23 is served, as an importer you will be consulted about where the consignment can be isolated to. This may be a facility run by you or a site operated by a third party.

All isolation sites must have been approved by the FHI and must be totally physically separate. They must have separate equipment and procedures, and the stock must remain in the facility until the Regulation 23 has been released.

It is your responsibility as the importer to make sure that:

  • any third parties involved are aware of the terms of Regulation 23
  • the animals are isolated to prevent the introduction or spread of disease

How and when is a Regulation 23 released?

Before a Regulation 23 can be released, you must resolve the problem that led to the Regulation 23 being issued.

If there is an error with the health certificate, you must arrange for the certificate to be amended or replaced by the competent authority in the country of origin.

If problems with the documentation can’t be resolved, the FHI may sample and test the consignment for disease. This involves the sacrifice of a minimum of 30 animals and a delay of between two and three weeks.

If the species are non-susceptible tropical species

If the species involved aren’t a vector of - or susceptible to - a controlled disease, a Regulation 23 won’t be issued for documentation errors. Instead, the consignment will be issued with a warning letter requiring the importer to correct paperwork errors but not requiring the animals to be isolated.

Newly listed exotic diseases

In October 2006, Epizootic Ulcerative Syndrome (EUS) was added to the list of European Community exotic diseases. A transitional period, ending 31 December 2012, was allowed so that the disease could be monitored and tested and controls for EUS could be implemented around the world.

Species affected by EUS

As with other fish diseases, there are species that are susceptible to EUS and those that are carriers of it - ‘vector’ species.

Susceptible species are:

  • gibelion, catla
  • snakeheads
  • multiple species of labeo
  • tyre track eels
  • mullets
  • barbs
  • gouramis

Vector species are:

  • goldfish
  • crucian carp
  • common carp
  • koi carp
  • chub
  • roach
  • rudd
  • tench
  • bighead carp
  • silver carp
  • swan mussel
  • noble crayfish
  • signal crayfish
  • redswamp fish

Importing species affected by EUS

From 1 January 2013, if you import susceptible and vector species into Great Britain, they must be accompanied by the correct health certificate declaring them to be free from EUS.

If you import species from outside the EU, you need to confirm with your suppliers that they can obtain the correct health certification from their competent authority declaring the species free from EUS. If they aren’t able to, you should not use those suppliers to import these species of fish and shellfish.

What health certification is required?

Species affected by EUS are imported under two different types of certificates:

  • Open certificates are used for fish intended for farming, relaying, ‘put and take’ fisheries and open ornamental facilities. Download a FHI example of an open certificate from AAHM website (PDF, 2.02MB).
  • Closed certificates are used for ornamental fish intended for closed ornamental facilities. Download an FHI example of a closed certificate from the AAHM website (PDF, 1.3MB).

All of the susceptible species are tropical species and can be imported on either an open or closed certificate, depending on their intended use. In both cases a EUS declaration must be made at paragraph II.2 for susceptible species.

All of the vector species are coldwater species and can only be imported to sites in Great Britain under an open certificate. The EUS declaration must be made at paragraph II.3 for vector species.

A mixed consignment of susceptible and vector species should be imported under an open certificate, with both paragraphs II.2 and II.3 completed for EUS.

Exporting fish, molluscs and crustacea from Great Britain

Exports of live fish, molluscs and crustacea from Great Britain often require health certification. The steps you need to take depend on whether you are exporting to other EU and EFTA member states or to countries outside the EU.

Exporting to EU and EFTA countries

When you export to EU and EFTA member states, your consignments only need to be accompanied by health certificates if the species you are exporting are susceptible to - or vectors of - EU controlled diseases. If they are, you will need to get the consignment inspected by the FHI before the health certificate is issued. This is carried out free of charge but you do need to provide five working days notice to the FHI.

Exporting to countries outside the EU

The requirements for exporting to countries outside the EU are set by the competent authority in the country of destination. It is your responsibility as the exporter to find out what the requirements are and to obtain copies of all necessary health certificates - including translations - that must accompany your consignments. You must also clarify who is an acceptable signatory: a qualified veterinarian or fish health specialist, for example. You must provide these certificates to the FHI and ensure they are accurate and will be accepted by the destination country’s competent authority.

The FHI will then decide if your aquaculture production business site can meet the requirements. If they decide that it can’t, they will contact you to discuss the matter further and outline the options available. If your site can’t meet the requirements, you may decide to contact the competent authority to ask them to revise their requirements.

If an FHI signature is required, and possible, the FHI will arrange a visit, as well as discussing charges and issuing with you before they proceed.
