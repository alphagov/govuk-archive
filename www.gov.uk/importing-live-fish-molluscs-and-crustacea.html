Importing and exporting live fish, molluscs and crustacea

The steps you must take to import and export live fish, molluscs and crustacea, and what to do if errors occur.

Only applies to England and Wales

Introduction

Before you can import live fish, molluscs and/or crustacea into the UK, you must be authorised as an aquaculture production business by the Fish Health Inspectorate (FHI).

All consignments of fish, molluscs and/or crustacea from outside the European Union (EU) need to be accompanied by a health certificate. This is issued by the competent authority in the country of origin and confirms the health status of the fish, molluscs or crustacea in the consignment.

If you are importing fish, molluscs and/or crustacea from within the EU, your consignments only need to be accompanied by a health certificate if they include species that are susceptible to, or carriers of, a disease controlled in Great Britain (GB). In both cases you need to notify the appropriate authority before your imports arrive.

This guide explains the authorisations and documentation you need in order to import live fish, molluscs and/or crustacea. It also gives guidance on what happens if errors occur, and the steps you must take when exporting.

Authorisation and documentation for live fish, mollusc and crustacea importers

Anyone importing live fish, molluscs and crustacea into Great Britain needs to be authorised by the FHI. This includes imports intended for:

  • farming
  • relaying
  • ‘put and take’ fisheries
  • restocking
  • open and closed ornamental facilities, including pets and purchases for own collection
  • scientific research
  • public aquaria and zoos
  • spas and medical use (nibble fish)

If you hold fish, molluscs and/or crustacea on your own site, your application to be authorised may need to include a biosecurity measures plan. For guidance on biosecurity measures plans, you can download biosecurity measures guidance for importers from the Defra website (PDF, 453KB).

Authorisation is free of charge and you can apply using form AAH2. You can download FHI form AAH2 from the Defra website (PDF, 51KB).

Importing live fish, mollusc and crustacea from outside the EU

Countries that are not in the EU or a member of the European Free Trade Association (EFTA) are treated as ‘third countries’ for import purposes.

Imports of fish, molluscs and/or crustacea from third countries must be accompanied by a health certificate issued by the competent authority in the country of origin. The authority must follow model documents issued by the EU and will often charge for the service.

  • Open certificates are required for fish, mollusc, crustacean intended for farming, relaying, put and take fisheries, open ornamental facilities and susceptible/ vector species for scientific research or public display. Stock must originate from a country/ territory permitted by the EU.
  • Closed certificates are required for ornamental fish intended for closed facilities, nibble fish for medical/ spa use and non susceptible/ vector species for scientific research or public display

Imports that arrive in Great Britain directly from a third country must go through a border inspection post (BIP) where the health certificate is inspected by Animal Health (AH). You will need to inform AH about your import at least one clear working day in advance using a Common Veterinary Entry Document (CVED). You can download a CVED notification form from the Defra website (PDF, 67KB). For details of the timescales to observe when using a CVED and also for contact details of BIPs in Great Britain, download the CVED timetable for notifications of intended imports from the Defra website (PDF, 35KB).

Importing live fish, molluscs and crustacea from within the EU

Different rules apply for movements from EU and EFTA member countries. Health certification is only required if the species being imported is either a vector/carrier of or is susceptible to an EU controlled disease. You can read a list of controlled diseases, and vector and susceptible species on the Scottish Government website.

You must also notify the FHI of all movements of fish, molluscs or crustacea into Great Britain from EU and EFTA member countries using form AAH1, except tropical species for ornamental use. Download FHI form AAH1 from the Defra website (PDF, 265KB).

However, although the Isle of Man, Northern Ireland, Jersey and Guernsey are all part of the UK, they are recognised as separate ‘health zones’ to Great Britain. This means that movements from these areas must be accompanied by any necessary health certificates.

Fish and shellfish intended for human consumption

If you import live or dead fish and shellfish for immediate human consumption or immediate processing for human consumption, your business doesn’t need to be authorised. If your imports are from outside the EU or consist of fish from another Member State, they will require health and hygiene certification. You can find out about importing fish and shellfish for human consumption on the Food Standards Agency (FSA) website.

Additional import requirements and post import controls

In addition to general import requirements, there are other controls that may be required when importing endangered. And there are also post import controls when you introduce species to UK waters, hold non-native species or export them.

Endangered species

There are some species that are recognised world-wide as being endangered. The Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES) is an international agreement designed to control the import, export and commercial use of these species. If you want to import any species that are on the CITES list you will need a CITES permit. You can find out about CITES on the Defra website.

Additional controls

Certain species are controlled by additional licensing regimes after their import. These include:

  • non-native crayfish and lobster deposits 
  • non-native fish 

Movement controls

For all introductions of fish into open inland waters in the UK, you must get ‘Section 30’ consent.

If you are planning on exporting fish from GB, there are certain steps you need to take. These depend on whether you are exporting to another EU member state or to a country outside the EU.

When importing errors occur

Sometimes, a mistake can occur with the legal process of importing live fish, molluscs and/or crustacea into Great Britain. While the consignment is at a border inspection post an error with the consignment or the accompanying documentation may be identified. For example:

  • some or all of the documentation may become lost in transit
  • errors may occur in the quantity of animals in the consignment
  • errors might happen in the accompanying health certificate
  • the health certificate may be incomplete

What is a Regulation 23?

A ‘Regulation 23’ is the name given to a notice issued under Regulation 23(1) of the Trade in Animals and Related Products Regulations 2011. A Regulation 23 allows animals, including fish, molluscs or crustacea, to enter Great Britain even though there has been an error with the import process. It will be issued to prevent a consignment of animals being destroyed, as long as they can be isolated well enough to pose a low health risk to Great Britain.

Who is a Regulation 23 issued to?

A Regulation 23 is served on the importer of the consignment. This is because they are the person who appears to be in charge of the animals at the time of the importation and when the veterinary checks were made. As an importer, it is your responsibility to make sure that all the terms of the Regulation 23 are complied with.

Isolation of the consignment

Before the Regulation 23 is served, as an importer you will be consulted about where the consignment can be isolated to. This may be a facility run by you or a site operated by a third party.

All isolation sites must have been approved by the FHI and must be totally physically separate. They must have separate equipment and procedures, and the stock must remain in the facility until the Regulation 23 has been released.

It is your responsibility as the importer to make sure that:

  • any third parties involved are aware of the terms of Regulation 23
  • the animals are isolated to prevent the introduction or spread of disease

How and when is a Regulation 23 released?

Before a Regulation 23 can be released, you must resolve the problem that led to the Regulation 23 being issued.

If there is an error with the health certificate, you must arrange for the certificate to be amended or replaced by the competent authority in the country of origin.

If problems with the documentation can’t be resolved, the FHI may sample and test the consignment for disease. This involves the sacrifice of a minimum of 30 animals and a delay of between two and three weeks.

If the species are non-susceptible tropical species

If the species involved aren’t a vector/carrier of or susceptible to a controlled disease, a Regulation 23 won’t be issued for documentation errors. Instead, the consignment will be issued with a Regulation 16 requiring the importer to correct paperwork errors but not requiring the animals to be isolated.

Exporting fish, molluscs and crustacea from Great Britain

Exports of live fish, molluscs and crustacea from Great Britain often require health certification. The steps you need to take depend on whether you are exporting to other EU and EFTA member states or to countries outside the EU.

Exporting to EU and EFTA countries

When you export to EU and EFTA member states, your consignments only need to be accompanied by health certificates if the species you are exporting are susceptible to or vectors of EU controlled diseases. If they are, you will need to get the consignment inspected by the FHI before the health certificate is issued. This is carried out free of charge but you do need to provide five working days notice to the FHI.

Exporting to countries outside the EU

The requirements for exporting to countries outside the EU are set by the competent authority in the country of destination. It is your responsibility as the exporter to find out what the requirements are and to obtain copies of all necessary health certificates, including translations, that must accompany your consignments. You must also clarify who is an acceptable signatory: a qualified veterinarian or fish health specialist, for example. You must provide these certificates to the FHI and ensure they are accurate and will be accepted by the destination country’s competent authority.

The FHI will then decide if your aquaculture production business site can meet the requirements. If they decide that it can’t, they will contact you to discuss the matter further and outline the options available. If your site can’t meet the requirements, you may decide to contact the competent authority to ask them to revise their requirements.

If an FHI signature is required, and possible, the FHI will arrange a visit, as well as discussing charges and issuing with you before they proceed.
