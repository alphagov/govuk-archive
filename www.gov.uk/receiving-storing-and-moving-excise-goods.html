Receiving, storing and moving excise goods

How to move, store and trade duty-suspended and duty-paid excise goods, and how to use the Excise Movement and Control System.

Introduction

The purpose of this guide is to explain the UK’s requirements for businesses involved in the holding and movement of excise goods.

It provides an overview of the procedures that apply if you intend to receive or dispatch excise goods that are moving within the UK, throughout the EU or to and from non-EU countries. It covers the movement of both duty-suspended and duty-paid excise goods and gives guidance on what you need to do and where you can find further information.

Moving excise goods in duty-suspension

When excise goods are moving within the EU in excise duty-suspension, the movement must take place between persons and premises that have been approved for that purpose by the competent authorities in the member state where they are based.

Excise goods may only be moved in excise duty-suspension once they are in ‘free circulation’. This applies to goods that have been wholly produced in the EU or goods that have been imported into the EU and have been released to free circulation on completion of all import formalities and payment of any customs duties.

Movement guarantees

Generally, excise goods that are moving within the EU in excise duty-suspension must be covered by financial security in the form of a movement guarantee. It is the consignor’s responsibility to ensure that a valid movement guarantee is in place, with detail of the guarantee recorded on the appropriate movement documentation prior to the goods being dispatched in duty-suspension.

Excise Movement and Control System

All duty-suspended movements of excise goods taking place within the EU must be recorded on the Excise Movement and Control System (EMCS) unless the goods are allowed to move under simplified procedures. EMCS captures and processes the information about the movement online, validates the data entered and allows real time notification of the dispatch and receipt of duty-suspended excise goods.

EMCS allows anyone who is approved to receive or dispatch excise goods in duty-suspension to exchange messages online with their EU trading partners about specific consignments and other movement information.

Registering and enrolling for EMCS using the HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) online service

UK consignors and consignees of duty-suspended excise goods can use the free EMCS service on the HMRC website or use a commercial software package or other in-house option.

Read about EMCS options available on the HMRC website.

If you are not already registered and enrolled to use other HMRC Online Services you will need to do so before you can use EMCS.

You will need an Excise ID number, also referred to as the ‘Excise Registration Number’. Your Excise ID number is quoted on the letter of approval or certificate of registration that approves you to dispatch and/or receive excise goods under duty-suspension arrangements. This number is also held on the System for the Exchange of Excise Data (SEED), which is the database used by EMCS to validate your approval.

Read about SEED and the Excise ID number on the HMRC website.

Once you have your Excise ID number, the action you will need to take to register and enrol for EMCS will depend on whether you are a new or existing user of HMRC Online Services.

Read about how to register and enrol for EMCS on the HMRC website.

Find contact details for EMCS - Online Services Helpdesk on the HMRC website.

Dispatching goods using EMCS

If you dispatch duty-suspended excise goods using EMCS, as the consignor you will need to complete and submit a message known as an electronic administrative document (eAD) through EMCS before the movement takes place. Once the detail entered on the eAD has been validated, EMCS generates a unique Administrative Reference Code (ARC) for that particular movement. As the ARC is required to travel with the goods, you must provide the person accompanying the goods (for example, the driver of the vehicle transporting the goods) with a printed version of the eAD or any other commercial document on which the ARC is clearly stated.

If you receive duty-suspended goods that are dispatched under EMCS procedures, you will need to access EMCS in order to receive notification about the expected consignment. Once the goods arrive, you should check the consignment against the eAD and then complete and submit a report of receipt through EMCS. If you discover shortages or surpluses on receipt, you should record this detail on the report of receipt. The report of receipt should be submitted through EMCS no later than five business days after the goods are received.

Procedures to use when EMCS is not available

If EMCS is not available, there are special procedures, known as ‘fallback procedures’, which should be followed to allow consignments of excise goods to continue to move under duty-suspension arrangements.

The UK’s fallback procedures allow UK consignors to dispatch or change the destination of duty-suspended excise goods when EMCS is not available.

If EMCS is not available for a UK consignee to submit the report of receipt, they must wait for EMCS to become available before doing so.

Read about EMCS fallback procedures on the HMRC website

Movements of duty-suspended excise goods wholly within the UK

EMCS must also be used for duty-suspended movements of excise goods taking place wholly within the UK except for those involving energy products or where simplified procedures apply.

When energy products are moving within the UK in excise duty-suspension, they must be accompanied by a document known as a W8 or a commercial equivalent.

Read about moving duty-suspended energy products within the UK in Notice 179 on the HMRC website.

The UK simplified procedures

Simplified procedures allow certain duty-suspended movements taking place wholly within the UK to be accompanied by alternative movement documentation instead of under cover of an eAD generated through EMCS. This applies to the following duty-suspended movements.

Movements of alcoholic liquors taking place between UK premises that are registered or approved for the production or storage of alcohol products, subject to the following conditions:

  • ownership of the alcoholic liquor must remain with its producer during the movement unless the approved person operating the premises and the producer are both members of the same VAT group
  • in cases where a movement guarantee is required, the alcoholic liquor must be accompanied by a W8 or commercial equivalent
  • in cases where no movement guarantee is required, the alcoholic liquor must be accompanied by a commercial document containing certain required information

Movements of tobacco products taking place between UK premises that are registered or approved for the production or storage of tobacco, where the following conditions apply:

  • ownership of the tobacco product must remain with its manufacturer during the movement
  • the tobacco product must be accompanied by a commercial document containing certain required information

Movements of alcoholic liquors or tobacco products from UK registered premises or an excise warehouse to a place in the UK from where the goods will leave the territory of the EU for direct export, subject to the following:

  • the occupier of the premises must be authorised to use Local Clearance Procedures
  • a full customs export declaration must be made
  • in cases where a movement guarantee is required, the export declaration must indicate the person providing the guarantee

Except for direct exports from the UK, consignees receiving alcoholic liquors or tobacco products under the simplified procedures must complete and return a certificate of receipt to the consignor no later than five business days after receiving the goods.

Read about the simplified procedures in Notice 197 on the HMRC website.

Storing excise goods in duty-suspension in the UK

The excise warehousing regime

Excise goods that are in free circulation can only be stored in excise duty-suspension whilst in the EU if they are held in approved premises known as tax warehouses. If you are based in the UK and intend to receive, store and dispatch excise goods in duty-suspension, you will need to have your premises approved as an excise warehouse, which is a specific type of tax warehouse. As the operator of the excise warehouse, you will also need to be approved as an authorised warehousekeeper.

An excise warehouse is any place of security approved by HMRC where goods liable for excise duty can be stored without payment of the duty for such periods and subject to such conditions as they see fit. Excise duty is suspended whilst goods are held in an excise warehouse but you must get permission from HMRC if you intend to store duty-suspended and duty-paid goods in the same premises.

The three categories of excise goods that can be held in an excise warehouse in duty-suspension are:

  • alcohol products including beers, wines and spirits
  • tobacco products including cigarettes, cigars and loose tobacco
  • energy products including hydrocarbon oils and biofuels for use as motor or heating fuel

Please note that HMRC does not allow UK manufactured tobacco products intended for use in the UK to be stored in an excise warehouse. Such goods can only be warehoused if they are intended for a duty-free purpose such as:

  • goods for export
  • for sale from an export shop
  • for supply to embassies within the UK
  • for use by visiting forces
  • for use as ships’ or aircraft stores

Read about Tobacco Products Duty in Notice 476 on the HMRC website.

The different types of excise warehouse approval

HMRC will consider applications for the following types of excise warehouse approval, subject to certain conditions.

General storage and distribution warehouses

These are premises used for the general storage and dispatch of alcohol and tobacco goods in duty-suspension. In order for these premises to be considered for approval, they must meet one of the following minimum throughput levels:

  • a minimum potential duty liability of £500,000 on the average monthly stockholding of duty-suspended excise goods
  • a duty liability of at least £2 million on an annual throughput of duty-suspended excise goods

Trade facility warehouses

These are premises where excise goods can be stored in duty-suspension for one of the following purposes: * the maturation of spirits produced at an associated distillery * goods which will not bear UK excise duty, for example, goods for export, goods used in pharmaceuticals, goods used in foodstuffs, goods to be removed for use as ship or aircraft stores * performing an allowable operation on the goods * any other purpose allowed by HMRC

Trade facility warehouses do not have to meet the minimum throughput levels of a general storage and distribution warehouse, but approval will be restricted to the specific trade need applied for, for example, the warehouse approval may be limited to the storage of spirits for bottling.

Motor and heating fuels warehouses

These are premises where mineral (hydrocarbon) oil, biofuels and special energy products may be stored in duty-suspension.

These warehouses are approved for a specific purpose and specified products, and are normally required to provide a minimum of one million litres bulk storage capacity. Further details about the conditions and requirements can be found in Notice 179.

Read about motor and heating fuels warehouses in Notice 179 on the HMRC website

General conditions of approval for excise warehouses

The general conditions of approval that excise warehouses must meet are:

  • the premises must be covered by a financial security in the form of a premises guarantee provided by the authorised warehousekeeper (if needed)
  • they must be physically secure
  • they must allow HMRC staff safe access to all approved areas and duty-suspended stock
  • they must have systems in place which correctly fulfil the obligations placed by Notices 179, 196 and 197

HMRC also applies ‘fit and proper’ tests to all approved warehouses, monitoring them regularly to ensure that they remain suitable for continued approval.

Applying for approval to operate an excise warehouse

If you wish to operate an excise warehouse, you will need to:

  • apply to become an authorised excise warehousekeeper
  • have your premises approved by HMRC

HMRC approval allows an authorised warehousekeeper to receive, store and dispatch excise goods in duty-suspension at their approved warehouse premises.

Applications for approval as an authorised excise warehousekeeper should be made using form EX61.

Find form EX 61 - Excise Warehousekeeper - Application for Registration on the HMRC website

Find form EX70 - Excise warehousing - motor and heating fuels warehouse application/amendment form on the HMRC website

Find form EX71 - Schedule on the HMRC website

Who can store goods in an excise warehouse?

If you own excise goods and you want to store them in duty-suspension, you will need to be approved by HMRC in order to deposit those goods in an excise warehouse.

If you are a UK-based owner of excise goods, you will need to be approved by HMRC as a registered owner. If you are an overseas owner without a fixed UK address, you will need to appoint someone who is approved by HMRC as a duty representative to act on your behalf.

In general, only the warehousekeeper of the warehouse concerned, a registered owner, or an overseas owner with a duty representative acting on their behalf can deposit goods in an excise warehouse.

Owners wishing to deposit their goods in an excise warehouse do not need to be registered by HMRC, or appoint a duty representative, if:

  • the owner of the goods is also the authorised warehousekeeper for the excise warehouse where the goods are to be stored
  • the owner of the goods is not a revenue trader
  • the goods are not classed as ‘relevant goods’, in other words, the goods being warehoused are either wine or made-wine, certain energy products or goods that are being warehoused for export for duty drawback purposes

Read about owners and duty representatives in Notice 196 on the HMRC website.

Applications for approval as a registered owner must be made using form EX60. If you want to act as a duty representative, you should apply for approval using form EX64.

Find form EX 60 - Owners of duty-suspended goods held in excise warehouses - application for registration on the HMRC website.

Financial security required when storing and dispatching excise goods in duty-suspension

HMRC requires financial security (in the form of a guarantee) relating to the storage of excise goods in an excise warehouse and the movement of excise goods from an excise warehouse in duty-suspension. HMRC will provide a guarantee form for completion by a bank or other approved guarantor who undertakes to pay money to HMRC in the event of an irregularity occurring which is covered by the guarantee.

Premises security is intended to cover the potential duty liability on the duty-suspended stock in the excise warehouse and should normally be provided by the authorised warehousekeeper. The level of guarantee required varies depending on the potential duty on the warehouse’s month-end stockholding - from zero where the potential duty is less than £100,000, up to £1 million where the potential duty exceeds £100 million.

However, new general storage and distribution warehouses are normally required to provide a minimum premises guarantee level of £250,000.

Read about premises guarantees in Notice 196 on the HMRC website.

Movement security is intended to cover the potential duty liability on the excise goods being dispatched from the warehouse in duty-suspension and must be provided by the dispatching warehousekeeper, the last owner of the goods whilst warehoused or the transporter. The level of guarantee required is based on the potential duty on an average week’s movements. The minimum level of security for movement guarantees is normally £20,000, otherwise, it varies from 25% of any potential duty less than £1 million, up to a flat guarantee of £1 million where the potential duty exceeds £100 million.

Read about movement guarantees in Notice 197 on the HMRC website.

Significant reductions to both premises and movement security are available where established traders have demonstrated sufficient compliance over a period of time.

Moving goods into and out of an excise warehouse

Excise warehousekeepers are responsible for accounting for all excise goods entering or leaving their warehouse premises.

The list below highlights some of your key responsibilities as an excise warehousekeeper when receiving excise goods in duty-suspension.

Read about receipt into and removal from an excise warehouse in Notice 197 on the HMRC website.

When receiving goods, you must:

  • ensure that your premises are approved to receive that type of goods
  • ensure that the owner, or their duty representative, is entitled to deposit the goods
  • place the goods in your warehouse without delay
  • enter the goods into your stock account records ensuring that all goods are adequately marked and identifiable in the warehouse
  • check all consignments have arrived intact
  • investigate any irregularities or indications of interference
  • discharge the movement by providing a report of receipt under EMCS procedures, or by endorsing and returning the certificate of receipt required for movements under simplified procedures, within the specified time limits

Receiving excise goods from countries not in the EU - Registered Consignors

As an authorised warehousekeeper, if you are receiving excise goods that have been imported from a non-EU country and the intention is to store those goods free of all duties, you must hold both an excise warehouse and customs warehouse approval for your premises.

If the person importing the goods wishes to suspend the excise duty only, they must arrange for the goods to be placed in an approved excise warehouse. However, this can only be done once the excise goods have been released to free circulation on completion of all import formalities and payment of any customs duties.

Read about customs procedures for imports from non-EU countries in Notice 199 on the HMRC website.

The excise duty-suspended movement from the place where the goods are released to free circulation, for example a port or airport in the UK, to the excise warehouse must be carried out by someone who is approved as a Registered Consignor.

HMRC will only approve the import agent for the goods or the authorised warehousekeeper receiving the goods from the place of release for free circulation as a Registered Consignor. If either of these persons wishes to apply for approval, they must complete and submit from EX72, and form EXCISE 102 in the case of a partnership.

Find form EX72 - Excise: Registered Consignor - application to register on the HMRC website.

Find form EXCISE 102 - Excise: about the partners on the HMRC website.

Read about getting approval as a Registered Consignor in Notice 196 on the HMRC website.

Please note that duty-suspended movements of excise goods from Registered Consignors are subject to EMCS procedures. Therefore if you are approved as a Registered Consignor, you will also need to register and enrol for EMCS.

Read about excise duty-suspended movements from Registered Consignors in Notice 197 on the HMRC website.

Removing or dispatching goods from an excise warehouse

As an excise warehousekeeper, you may remove goods from your excise warehouse for:

  • home use on payment of duty (sometimes referred to as the goods being ‘released for consumption’ in the UK)
  • dispatch under duty-suspension to other approved UK warehouses
  • dispatch under duty-suspension to approved persons or premises in other EU member states
  • export to non-EU countries in duty-suspension

You may also be allowed to carry out the following miscellaneous removals:

  • supplies to diplomats and visiting forces within the UK
  • supplies to entitled international organisations, embassies and forces in other member states
  • supplies to HM ships
  • removal of goods for use as ships or aircraft stores
  • removals to authorised duty-free spirits users
  • delivery of spirit-based flavourings and essences that are exempt from excise duty

Read about receipt into and removal from an excise warehouse in Notice 197 on the HMRC website.

Warehousekeeper responsibilities when removing goods

When removing goods from your excise warehouse, you must:

  • complete the appropriate movement documentation depending on the type of movement taking place
  • unless we have agreed otherwise, take account of the goods being removed and carry out any necessary examination
  • write the goods out of your stock account
  • ensure that duty is paid or accounted for on removals to home use
  • ensure that a valid movement guarantee is in place and recorded on the movement documentation for all appropriate dispatches in duty-suspension
  • supervise the removal to ensure it is correct

Exporting duty-suspended excise goods to non-EU countries

As the dispatching warehousekeeper, when exporting duty-suspended excise goods from the UK to non-EU countries, you must ensure that you fully comply with all excise requirements as well as any separate customs export declaration requirements.

Read about exporting excise goods from an excise warehouse to non-EU countries in Notice 197 on the HMRC website.

Read about export procedures in Notice 275 on the HMRC website.

Excise warehouse returns (form W1)

All authorised warehousekeepers (except those currently operating certain trade facility warehouses such as export shops and aircraft store floors) are required to submit a W1 excise warehouse return for each set of premises they operate, in respect of alcohol and tobacco goods.

The W1 return is for submitting details of the warehouse stock, receipts and removals, and the registered owners storing goods in the warehouse. It is also used to report any undischarged duty-suspended movements and any shortages that are notified by the consignees. As an excise warehousekeeper, you will need to send a W1 return for each individual warehouse you operate, normally on a calendar month basis.

Find form W1 and W1 (Notes) - Excise warehouse return and notes to help you complete form W1 on the HMRC website.

You can submit your W1 returns electronically using the Alcohol & Tobacco Warehousing Declarations (ATWD) service. Alternatively, you can download the W1 form from the HMRC website and send your completed return through the post.

Read about registering, enrolling and activating to use the ATWD service on the HMRC website.

Paying or deferring duty when moving excise goods

Excise duty payment

When goods are released from an excise warehouse for consumption, you must ensure that the excise duty has been paid or accounted for prior to the removal of the goods. For immediate payment of duty you must use cash or an equivalent method such as a banker’s draft, a guaranteed cheque (by use of guarantee form C&E 307 or by individual bank endorsement), Bacs or CHAPS.

If payment is being made by cash or equivalent, you should submit the following payment warrants to HMRC along with the remittance:

  • W5 for the removal of alcohol goods
  • W6 for the removal of tobacco goods
  • W50 for the immediate payment of excise duty including Mineral (Hydrocarbon) Oil Duty

Find form W5 - Excise warehouse - remittance advice for alcohol goods on the HMRC website.

Find form W6 - Excise warehouse - remittance advice for tobacco goods on the HMRC website.

Form W50 is not available via the HMRC website but you can order supplies from the Excise and Customs Helpline.

Find contact details for HMRC excise & customs helpline on the HMRC website.

Read about duty payment for warehouse removals in Notice 197 on the HMRC website.

Excise duty deferment

Duty can only be deferred if you have been authorised to do so by HMRC.

Read about applying for duty deferment approval in Notice 101 on the HMRC website.

If you have been approved to defer payment of the excise duty and wish to account for the duty under deferment arrangements, you must submit the following warrants to HMRC:

  • W5D for the removal of alcohol goods
  • W6D for the removal of tobacco goods
  • HO10 for the removal of mineral (hydrocarbon) oils

Find form W5D - Excise warehouse - deferment advice for alcohol goods on the HMRC website.

Find form W6D - Excise warehouse - deferment advice for tobacco goods on the HMRC website

Form HO10 is not available via the HMRC website but you can order supplies from the excise and customs helpline.

Find contact details for HMRC Excise & Customs Helpline on the HMRC website.

You can also submit the W5, W5D, W6 and W6D forms online using the ATWD system.

Read about submitting duty payment forms electronically using ATWD on the HMRC website.

Read about excise duty deferment, VAT and other charges in Notice 101 on the HMRC website.

Excise Payment Security System (EPSS)

In order to defer duty, you are normally required to take out a guarantee to cover your total monthly liabilities. Under EPSS you can apply for authorisation to reduce the level of security required to cover your deferred payments of excise duty to nil. However, you must continue to secure all other deferred payments for import VAT (unless you have been approved for Simplified Import VAT Accounting (SIVA)) and customs duty. If you wish to apply for EPSS and SIVA, you may apply for both at the same time.

If you wish to apply for EPSS, you must meet the eligibility and authorisation criteria. If you are authorised you will receive information on how to reduce the level of your guarantee for excise duty payments to nil.

Read about EPSS on the HMRC website.

Registered Consignees

Registered Consignees are revenue traders who are approved and registered by HMRC to receive and account for the duty on duty-suspended excise goods from other EU member states. They must account for the UK excise duty when the goods are received in the UK. They cannot receive duty-suspended excise goods from outside the EU (not to be confused with Registered Consignors) nor can they hold or dispatch goods in duty-suspension.

Applying for the Registered Consignee scheme

In order to apply for approval as a Registered Consignee, you will need to meet certain criteria, for example, you must have a place of business in the UK, be registered for VAT in the UK and be able to account for the duty under deferment arrangements.

You should apply for approval by completing and submitting form HM1 together with supporting forms EXCISE 102 (if your business is a partnership) and HM8 if appropriate.

Read about approval and registration in section 4 of Notice 203A on the HMRC website.

Find form HM1 - Application to register for the Registered Consignee scheme on the HMRC website.

Find form EXCISE 102 - Excise: about the partners on the HMRC website.

Find form HM8 - Authority to use another trader’s Deferment Approval Number (DAN) on the HMRC website.

Accounting for duty as a Registered Consignee

Registered Consignees use deferment arrangements to account for the UK excise duty so you will need to have a Deferment Approval Number (DAN).

You can use your own DAN or someone else’s. If you intend to use another person’s DAN, you will need to provide a completed form HM8 Authority for each deferment account you intend to use. For more information on how deferment works, refer to HMRC Notice 101.

Read about Registered Consignees in Notice 203A on the HMRC website.

Receiving goods under the Registered Consignee scheme

Normally goods are delivered to the Registered Consignee’s registered address. However, as a Registered Consignee, you can apply to HMRC for approval to operate ‘direct delivery’. This allows you to have goods delivered to another business address.

The prime liability for the UK excise duty always lies with the Registered Consignee where the goods are correctly consigned to them as the legal consignee, even if the goods are physically delivered to a direct delivery address.

Receipts by Registered Consignees are subject to EMCS procedures.

If you have a Government Gateway account, and are approved as a Registered Consignee, you can add the EMCS service to your existing User ID using the instructions supplied.

Read about Excise Movement and Control Systems in section 8 of Notice 203A on the HMRC website.

Temporary Registered Consignees

Temporary Registered Consignees (TRCs) are excise traders that are registered and approved by HMRC to import duty-suspended excise goods into the UK from other EU member states on a consignment-by-consignment basis. They are not permitted to hold or dispatch excise goods in duty-suspension or to receive excise duty-suspended goods from UK suppliers or from outside the EU.

Applying for the TRC Scheme

In order to be approved as a TRC, you must:

  • have a place of business in the UK at which to receive your goods
  • demonstrate a business need to become a TRC, for example, you should be able to present a viable business plan for your proposed business, including details of who you intend buying goods from and details of your customer base
  • demonstrate suitability to be a TRC, for example, that neither you or any key personnel have any unspent convictions or a recent compounded settlement for any HMRC offence

You should apply for TRC approval by completing and submitting form TRC1 and supporting form EXCISE 102 if your business is a partnership.

Read about applying for TRC approval in Notice 204A on the HMRC website.

Find form TRC1 - Application to register for the TRC scheme on the HMRC website.

Find form EXCISE 102 - Excise: about the partners on the HMRC website.

Receiving goods under the TRC scheme

Goods can only be delivered to the registered address you give on your application. Before arranging the dispatch, as a TRC you must:

  • give HMRC advance information of the consignment
  • send HMRC a TRC2 movement request form and secure payment of the UK duty due
  • give the consignor the Temporary Consignment Authorisation (TCA) number that HMRC allocates for each consignment when the movement request has been authorised

When you receive the goods you must provide a final accounting declaration to HMRC by returning the fully completed form TRC2 for that movement and pay any duty or VAT due in full.

Receipts by TRC are subject to EMCS procedures.

If you have a Government Gateway account and are approved as a TRC, you can add the EMCS service to your existing User ID using the instructions supplied.

Read about TRC receipts and EMCS procedures in sections 6 and 11 of Notice 204A on the HMRC website.

Dealing in duty-paid excise goods

If you intend to import into the UK, or arrange the importation of, excise goods that are duty-paid, or released for consumption, in another EU member state for a commercial purpose, the goods will be liable to excise duty in the UK. There are three different ways in which duty-paid excise goods can be imported into the UK for a commercial purpose:

  • the standard UK duty-paid scheme for unregistered commercial importers
  • the Registered Commercial Importer scheme
  • distance selling arrangements for sales to private individuals

Unless the goods are being dispatched under distance selling arrangements, movements of duty-paid excise goods between EU member states must be covered by a three-part document known as a Simplified Accompanying Administrative Document (SAAD).

The standard duty-paid scheme

You do not need to be registered with HMRC to use this scheme. However, before commercially importing any duty-paid excise goods, you should notify HMRC by submitting a form HM4 at least 15 working days in advance of the intended date of each dispatch. You must secure the UK excise duty that is due on the goods by submitting your payment, in the form of a banker’s draft, postal order or cheque, along with the HM4. HMRC will return the HM4 to you, endorsed with a unique reference number for that particular consignment.

Your supplier should not dispatch the goods until you have notified them of the unique reference number, which should then accompany the goods along with a completed SAAD. Under the standard scheme, the goods can only be delivered to your business premises, as stated on the HM4.

You must inform HMRC of any changes to the information you have supplied as soon as they occur, and certainly before the goods arrive in the UK. You must notify HMRC as soon as you receive the goods and then, within four working days, return the HM4 form to HMRC along with a copy of the receipted SAAD and, if appropriate, payment for any outstanding duty or VAT.

Find form HM4 - Request to import excise goods bought duty-paid in another EU member state on the HMRC website.

Read about your requirements when receiving duty-paid excise goods in Notice 204B on the HMRC website.

The Registered Commercial Importer Scheme

This scheme allows you to import duty-paid excise goods into the UK and defer payment of the duty using your own or someone else’s duty deferment account. Under this arrangement the duty is guaranteed by the duty deferment guarantee.

Apart from the difference in the duty payment method and the ability to have the goods delivered to other business addresses, the same procedures apply to Registered Commercial Importers as to those traders using the standard duty-paid scheme.

If you wish to apply for approval as a Registered Commercial Importer, you will need to meet certain criteria, for example, you must have a place of business in the UK and be able to demonstrate a business need and suitability to be a Registered Commercial Importer.

You should apply for approval by completing and submitting form HM3. You will have to provide a DAN on the HM3 - this can be your own or someone else’s. If you intend to use someone else’s, you will also need to submit a completed form HM8 for each deferment account you intend to use.

Find form HM3 - Application to register as a Registered Commercial Importer on the HMRC website.

Find form HM8 - Authority to use another trader’s DAN on the HMRC website.

Read about how to register as a Commercial Importer in section 8 of Notice 204B on the HMRC website

Distance selling of excise goods

Distance selling takes place when a trader in one EU member state supplies goods to a private individual in another member state and the vendor (seller) is responsible, directly or indirectly, for delivery of the goods. Distance selling includes mail order and internet sales.

When excise goods are being supplied under distance selling arrangements, they must be taken from duty-paid stock. The vendor is liable to pay the excise duty and VAT of the member state of destination at the time of delivery. The excise duty must be guaranteed to the satisfaction of the fiscal authority of destination before the goods are dispatched.

In the UK, it is a tax representative approved by HMRC, who guarantees to pay the excise duty on the vendor’s behalf. Following the dispatch, the tax representative must account for and pay the full amount of excise duty and VAT due on the goods sent by the vendor.

When making distance sales to the UK, it is the vendor’s responsibility to ensure that a UK Tax Representative is appointed.

UK Tax Representatives are traders who are approved and registered in the UK to account for excise duty on distance sales to private individuals in the UK on behalf of vendors in other EU member states.

If you wish to apply for approval as a UK Tax Representative, you must have a place of business in the UK and you must have your own deferment account in place as you will need to use it to account for the duty. You are not permitted to use anyone else’s deferment account to account for the duty on distance sales.

You should apply for approval by completing and submitting form HM9.

The requirement to submit an HM4 for each import of duty-paid excise goods also applies to UK Tax Representatives.

Find form HM9 - Application for registration as a tax representative on the HMRC website.

Read about Commercial Importers and Tax Representatives in Notice 204B on the HMRC website.

If you’re a UK vendor supplying excise goods to private individuals in other member states under distance selling arrangements, you will be responsible for paying the excise duty and VAT in the member state of destination. You should find out from the authorities in that member state how you must do this, including whether you will need to appoint a Tax Representative in that member state.

Although there is no requirement for any official documentation to accompany excise goods moving under distance selling arrangements, it is advisable that commercial documentation accompanies the consignment showing details of the tax representative in the member State of destination who is to account for the duty.

EU suppliers should also be aware of the rules about registering for VAT when making distance sales to other member states including the UK.

Read about the UK’s requirements relating to VAT and distance selling in section 5 of Notice 700/1 on the HMRC website.

Read about the statutory VAT obligations for dispatches to other EU member states in Notice 725 on the HMRC website.

UK Duty Stamps and fiscal marks

The UK Duty Stamps Scheme applies to bottles of spirits, wine or made wine with alcohol by volume (ABV) strength of 30 per cent or more, contained in bottles of 35 centilitres or above, for consumption in the UK.

Each bottle must bear a duty stamp indicating that excise duty has either been paid, or is due to be paid, before it is released onto the UK market. Stamps are available either as a freestanding product-specific stamp or a design which can be incorporated into the bottle’s back label.

You need to register with HMRC to obtain duty stamps, unless you are a Temporary Registered Consignee or using the standard UK duty-paid scheme as an unregistered Commercial Importer, in which case you should order stamps direct from HMRC. To register for duty stamps, you will need to complete and submit form DS1.

You can authorise someone to affix stamps on your behalf, or acquire and affix stamps for other businesses such as owners of goods in a warehouse. Owners of goods may register in their own right but they can only obtain the label stamp design.

If you are a Tax Representative, any product you receive must be stamped before the goods arrive in the UK. It is an offence for retailers, wholesalers and transporters, to hold transport or deal in unstamped stock.

Find form DS 1 - Duty Stamps - Application for Registration on the HMRC website.

Read about the UK Duty Stamps scheme in Notice DS5 on the HMRC website.

Certain tobacco products are also required to carry a fiscal mark by law.

Read about tobacco products duty in Notice 476 on the HMRC website

Excise duty drawback

Excise duty drawback is a refund of UK excise duty. Drawback is paid to eligible claimants, providing certain conditions are met, when UK duty-paid excise goods have not and will not be consumed in the UK because they are:

  • dispatched to another member state (under cover of a SAAD or as distance sales)
  • exported outside the EU (in accordance with export procedures)
  • warehoused for export
  • destroyed

You can claim drawback within three years of paying the excise duty if your duty-paid goods are removed from the UK or destroyed in accordance with the conditions of drawback laid down in the relevant HMRC Notices.

When you send UK duty-paid goods to another EU member state, HMRC will only grant drawback when you can provide evidence that the duty has been paid and collected in the receiving member state. Please note that duty-paid alcoholic drinks can no longer be ‘warehoused for export’ as a way of claiming drawback. However, you may continue to claim drawback on such goods using direct dispatch or export arrangements subject to the relevant conditions being met.

If you want to apply for drawback, it’s important you keep all the necessary commercial documentation to support your claim. HMRC have the right to examine your records. You may also have to allow HMRC to verify the destruction of your goods, or examine them before they are exported or dispatched. You and your goods must be eligible in order to claim drawback and you must follow the correct claim procedures when making your claim.

Read about excise duty drawback in Notice 207 on the HMRC website.

Your claim for drawback must normally be submitted at least two business days before the goods are exported or destroyed. You can inform HMRC of your plans by completing and submitting form EX75 - Notice of intention to claim drawback.

Find form EX75 - Notice of intention to claim drawback on the HMRC website.

Please note that bottles of spirits, wine or made-wine bearing duty stamps are not eligible goods for the purposes of drawback. If you want to make a drawback claim you must first obliterate the duty stamp.

Similarly, you can only claim drawback on tobacco products that have been fiscally marked, provided the fiscal mark is obliterated to HMRC’s satisfaction.

You can also claim drawback on oils that are loaded into a ship or aircraft for use as stores on a foreign voyage or flight.

Read about the procedures for making a claim in Notice 172 - Excise duty drawback for ships and aircraft stores on the HMRC website.

If you export goods by post from your premises, in addition to completing an export declaration, you must also complete form C&E 132. You must label each package with form C&E 83A and make sure you complete an International Customs Declaration (available from the Post Office) to go with it. You can use your own equivalent of form C&E 132, provided each parcel has its own unique identification number.

Find form C&E 132 - Certificate of posting of goods delivered from a Customs and Excise postal depot or from an exporter’s premises for exportation by post on the HMRC website. If you regularly pay excise duty it’s sometimes possible to offset the amount of drawback being claimed against duty that is due to be paid.

Read about how Registered Brewers and packagers can offset drawback against duty in Notice 226 on the HMRC website.

Read about how some oil traders can offset drawback against duty in Notice 179 on the HMRC website.

If you are involved in the dispatch of excise goods to other EU member states, you need to be aware of any statutory VAT obligations that may apply in the receiving member state.

Read about the statutory VAT obligations for dispatches to other EU member states in Notice 725 on the HMRC website.
