Expenses and benefits: clothing, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

As an employer providing clothing to your employees, you have certain tax, National Insurance and reporting obligations.

What’s included

This includes purchase, cleaning and repair costs for all clothing.

What you have to pay and report depends on if it’s:

  • protective clothing that your employee needs to do their job
  • a uniform they only wear at work

Part 2: What's exempt

You don’t have to report anything to HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) or pay tax and National Insurance if you:

  • lend clothing to employees who earn at a rate of less than £8,500 a year
  • pay to clean or repair protective clothing or uniforms for employees who earn at a rate of less than £8,500 a year

You don’t have to pay tax and National Insurance on most uniforms or protective clothing, but you may still have to report these expenses to HMRC.

Part 3: What to report and pay

If the clothing you provide isn’t exempt, you may have to report it to HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC), and you may have to pay tax and National Insurance (NI) on the value.

Uniforms or protective clothing

If you provide uniforms or protective clothing, you can apply for a dispensation, which means you won’t have to include them on your end-of-year reports.

The following table shows what you have to do if you don’t have a dispensation.

Clothing you give to directors Fill in form P11D, section A Nothing
Clothing you lend to employees earning at a rate of £8,500 or more a year Fill in form P11D, section L Nothing

Any other clothing

This includes clothing employees wear at work that isn’t necessary protective clothing or a uniform.

Clothing you give to employees earning at a rate of less than £8,500 a year Fill in form P9D, section A(2) Nothing
Clothing you give to employees earning at a rate of £8,500 or more a year Fill in form P11D, section A Pay Class 1A National Insurance on the value of the benefit

Part 4: Work out the value

The value of clothing for tax and reporting depends on whether you give or lend it, who initially pays for it, and how much the employee earns.

Clothing you give to employees

If the employee earns at a rate of more than £8,500 a year, or is a director, use whichever is the higher of:

  • the second-hand value of the clothing when you give it to your employee
  • the initial cost to you of the clothing

If they earn at a rate of less than £8,500 a year, use the second-hand value of the clothing when you gave it to your employee.

Clothing you lend to employees

Use whichever is the higher of:

  • 20% of the clothing’s market value when you first provided it
  • any annual rental or hire charges you pay for it

Clothing your employees buy and you pay them back for, or cleaning and repair costs

Use the amount of money you give you employee for the clothing, cleaning or repair.
