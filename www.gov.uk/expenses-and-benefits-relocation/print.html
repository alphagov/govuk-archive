Expenses and benefits: relocation costs, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

As an employer contributing to an employee’s relocation costs, you have certain tax, National Insurance and reporting obligations.

What’s included

The costs of:

  • buying or selling a home
  • moving
  • buying certain things for a  new home
  • bridging loans
  • other relocation costs (these are counted as ‘non-qualifying’ costs and have different rules)

Part 2: What's exempt

Up to £8,000, some relocation costs are exempt from reporting and paying tax and National Insurance. These are called ‘qualifying’ costs and include:

  • the costs of buying or selling a home
  • moving costs
  • buying certain things for a  new home
  • bridging loans

These are only qualifying costs when:

  • a new employee is moving area to start a job with you
  • an existing employee is changing their place of work within your organisation
  • the costs are paid in the same tax year as the move took place
  • the employee’s new home is reasonably close to the workplace and their old home isn’t

For qualifying costs over £8,000, you may have to report and pay tax and National Insurance.

Bridging loans

For bridging loans to count as qualifying costs:

  • the employee (and/or members of their family) must sell their old home and buy a new one
  • they must need a loan to bridge the gap between buying the new house and getting the money from their sale of the old one
  • the loan must be used only to buy the new house or pay off loans relating to the old home
  • the loan can’t be for more than the market value of the old home at the time the new home is bought

Part 3: What to report and pay

For qualifying costs up to £8,000 there is nothing to report and nothing to deduct or pay.

Qualifying costs over £8,000

Who gets the benefit What to do What to deduct or pay
Employees earning at a rate of less than £8,500 a year Report on form P9D, section A(2) Nothing
Employees earning at a rate of £8,500 or more a year Report on P11D, section J Class 1A National Insurance on the amount above £8,000
Directors Report on form P11D, section J Class 1A National Insurance on the amount above £8,000

Non-qualifying benefits that you arrange and pay the supplier directly

Who gets the benefit What to do What to deduct or pay
Employees at a rate of less than £8,500 a year Report on form P9D, section A(2) Nothing
Employees earning at a rate of £8,500 or more a year Report on form P11D, section A, K or L Class 1A National Insurance on the cost to you
Directors Report on form P11D, section A, K or L Class 1A National Insurance on the cost to you

Non-qualifying benefits that your employee arranges, but you pay the supplier directly

Who gets the benefit What to do What to deduct or pay
Employees at a rate of less than £8,500 a year Report on form P9D, section A(1) Add the cost to the employee’s other earnings and deduct and pay Class 1 National Insurance (but not PAYE tax) through payroll
Employees earning at a rate of £8,500 or more a year Report on form P11D, section M or N Add the cost to the employee’s other earnings and deduct and pay Class 1 National Insurance (but not PAYE tax) through payroll
Directors Report on form P11D, section M or N Add the cost to the employee’s other earnings and deduct and pay Class 1 National Insurance (but not PAYE tax) through payroll

Non-qualifying expenses or benefits where you reimburse your employee’s costs

These reimbursements count as earnings, so:

  • add them to the employee’s other earnings
  • deduct and pay PAYE tax and Class 1 National Insurance through payroll

Part 4: Work out the value

For qualifying costs the value to use is any amount above £8,000.

For all other costs, use the full value of the expense or benefit to calculate tax or National Insurance due.
