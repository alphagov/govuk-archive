Planning permission, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

You’ll need planning permission from your local planning authority (LPA) if you want to develop land or buildings.

Your LPA is usually your local council. In national parks the LPA is the national park authority.

Find your LPA on the Planning Portal website.

You may need planning permission for:

  • constructing or changing buildings
  • changing the use of land or buildings
  • demolishing some buildings

The LPA can either:

  • give you planning permission (possibly with conditions)
  • turn you down

Part 2: Do you need planning permission?

It’s your responsibility to check with your local planning authority (LPA) if you need planning permission before you start any development.

The interactive house shows you what planning permission you may need for different projects around your home.

Smaller building works don’t usually need planning permission, as long as you meet certain conditions. These smaller projects include:

  • extensions
  • conservatories
  • loft conversions
  • sheds

These are called ‘permitted developments’. Some LPAs may not allow this, though, in which case you still need to apply for planning permission.

You might not have permitted development rights if you live in:

  • a listed building
  • a conservation area
  • a national park
  • an Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty
  • the Norfolk or Suffolk Broads

Find out more about permitted developments on the Planning Portal website.

Part 3: How to apply

Apply online

You can apply for planning permission online through the Planning Portal website. This sends your application to your local planning authority (LPA).

Apply on paper

You can download planning application forms from the Planning Portal website to send to your LPA.

Get more advice on how to apply on the Planning Portal website.

What to submit

Different LPAs can ask you for different documents to support your application. However, you always need:

  • a site location plan (showing the proposed development in relation to surrounding properties)
  • a block plan (showing the proposed development in relation to the property boundary)

Planning officers at your local council can check if you have all the documents you need to apply. Some councils charge for this service.

Application fee

You have to pay a fee for most planning applications. The amount depends on your application.

You can use an online calculator to work out fees in England and Wales.

Who to tell

If you’re not the sole owner of your property, you must tell the other part owner.

If the property is leasehold, you must tell any leaseholders with at least 7 years left on their lease.

Letting someone else handle your application

If you don’t want to handle the application yourself, you can ask your agent, architect, solicitor or builder to do it for you.

Part 4: What happens to your application

  1. The Planning Portal sends your application to the local planning authority (LPA).

  2. The LPA checks your application and makes it public to let other people comment on it.

  3. The LPA approves or refuses your application.

Only start your building work after the LPA tells you that you have planning permission.

The LPA will try to decide your application within 8 weeks. If it needs longer, it will usually write to you and ask for an extension. This might happen if your building project is complex or affects lots of people.

Find out more about the decision-making process on the Planning Portal website.

Part 5: Your application’s turned down

If you’re refused planning permission, ask your local planning authority (LPA) why. You can then make changes to your application and send it in again.

As a last resort, if you think the LPA’s decision is unreasonable, you can appeal.

Part 6: Commenting on planning applications

All building projects that need planning permission must be made public. You can look at the plans and comment on them.

The local planning authority (LPA) will post notices near the building site and/or write letters to people who live nearby to ask if they agree with the plans.

For large projects, like a school or supermarket, the LPA will also put a notice in the local newspaper.

You’ll need to look at the plans and make comments before the deadline set by the LPA.

You can also go to council meetings about the development. You can speak in these meetings but you can’t vote on the planning application.

Once the LPA has given planning permission for a new development, you can’t change the permission.
