Ownerless property (bona vacantia), a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

Bona vacantia is the legal name for ownerless property that passes to the Crown (the government) by law. It can be any kind of property, like buildings, money or personal possessions.

You could be entitled to a share in a deceased relative’s estate (their possessions) that has passed to the Crown or be affected by a company being closed down (dissolved).

Who represents the Crown

The Treasury Solicitor looks after bona vacantia for the Crown. It gets involved when:

  • companies are dissolved
  • a person dies with no will or known family and their estate is referred to the Treasury Solicitor

The Treasury Solicitor has 2 groups dealing with bona vacantia.

The Estates Group

This group looks after people’s estates, including:

  • bank and building society accounts
  • life insurance
  • property
  • anything else owned by the person when they died

There’s a list of unclaimed estates and some frequently-asked questions on the Treasury Solicitor’s website.

Adverts and claims

The Treasury Solicitor advertises to find entitled family. If you reply to an advert, you have to prove your relationship with evidence like birth, marriage or death certificates.

The Companies Group

This group deals with any assets held by companies when they are dissolved. This is mostly:

  • cash balances
  • freehold and leasehold property
  • trademarks
  • copyright
  • mortgages

What the Treasury Solicitor doesn’t deal with

The Treasury Solicitor handles bona vacantia in England and Wales (except the Duchies of Cornwall and Lancaster).

Elsewhere it’s dealt with by other organisations. They are:

  • in the Duchies of Cornwall and Lancaster - Farrer and Co
  • in Scotland - The Queen’s and Lord Treasurer’s Remembrancer
  • in Northern Ireland - the Crown Solicitor’s Office for Northern Ireland - you can write to them at:


Crown Solicitor
Royal Courts of Justice
Chichester Street
Belfast BT1 3JY

Part 2: Refer an estate to the Treasury Solicitor

Referring an estate is when you tell the Treasury Solicitor about someone who has died with no will or relatives that you know of.

Before you refer an estate, ask:

  • did the person leave a will - can you contact the executor and beneficiaries?
  • can you get in touch with any blood relatives entitled to the estate?
  • did the person leave more debts than funds (an ‘insolvent’ estate)?

If the answer to any of these questions is ‘yes’, you shouldn’t refer the estate. If the answer to all the questions is ‘no’ you can refer the estate online.

The Treasury Solicitor only handles estates worth £500 or more. When the the value is less than £500:

  • you can keep the money, if the estate is just cash
  • any saleable belongings should be sold and if they sell for more than £500, you need to refer the estate

Part 3: Check if you're an entitled relative

Before making a claim on an estate you need to know if you’re entitled. There are some general rules:

  • if there’s no will, the person’s spouse or civil partner and then any children have first claim to the estate
  • if there’s no spouse or child, anyone descended from a grandparent of the person is entitled to a share in the estate
  • if you’re related by marriage you have no entitlement

The Treasury Solicitor has drawn up a family tree to help you check if you’re entitled.

Adopted people

If you’re adopted:

  • you have the same rights as if you’d been born into your adoptive family
  • you have no rights to an estate of your original birth family

If the deceased person was adopted, only the adoptive family have rights to the estate.

Part 4: Make a claim on an estate

If you think you’re entitled:

  1. Go to ‘Are you an entitled relative?’ on the Treasury Solicitor website.

  2. Enter the surname of the person or use the alphabetical search to find the right estate.

  3. On the results page, click on the person’s surname, which will open the claim form.

  4. Fill in your details and submit.

If you can’t find the estate

You can write to the Treasury Solicitor enclosing a simple family tree:


Treasury Solicitor's Office (BV)
1 Kemble Street
London WC2B 4TS


Evidence you need

You’ll be asked to send a family tree showing your relationship and 2 pieces of identification:

  • one showing your name
  • one showing your name and address, dated within the last 6 months

You might also be asked to send birth, death or marriage certificates.

Part 5: Dissolved companies

There are lots of reasons for referring a dissolved company asset to the Treasury Solicitor.

You might be affected by the closing down of a company or just want to buy something the company owned.

Some of the reasons include:

  • you’re leasing something where the freehold was owned by the company
  • you want to buy or are affected by land owned by the company
  • you want to buy other assets of the company like shares, trade marks or copyrights
  • you’re a shareholder trying to get cash held by the company

How to refer an asset

Use the dissolved company notification form to let the Treasury Solicitor know.

Or you can write to:


Treasury Solicitor's Office (BV)

London WC2B 4TS

Part 6: Discretionary payments and grants

You might be entitled to a grant from a dissolved company’s assets or a deceased person’s estate.

Grants from dissolved companies

If you were a member of a dissolved company, you can apply to the Treasury Solicitor for a discretionary grant. Use the dissolved company notification form and confirm the:

  • full name of the company
  • company registration number
  • name and address of your bank
  • your bank account number and sort code

The Treasury Solicitor has published guidelines to help you:

  • discretionary grants - where the company can be restored
  • discretionary grants - where the company can’t be restored

Grants from deceased estates

You can apply to the Treasury Solicitor for a grant from a deceased person’s estate if you could have expected to benefit from it. This could be where:

  • you provided the person with free services like washing, cleaning, cooking, shopping, home repairs or care where they might otherwise have had to pay
  • you lived together with the person (as their partner or as a friend) but were not married
  • you represent a charity or other body that cared for the person at considerable expense

You don’t have to be related to the person to make a claim.

How to apply

The Treasury Solicitor has published guidelines on grants from estates to help you apply.

There isn’t a form to fill out - contact the Treasury Solicitor, including as much information as possible, at:
