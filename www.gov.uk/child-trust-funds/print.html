Child Trust Fund, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

A Child Trust Fund (CTF) is a long-term tax-free savings account for children. All children eligible for a CTF account should now have one.

Eligibility

Your child is entitled if all the following apply:

  • they were born between 1 September 2002 and 2 January 2011
  • you were paid Child Benefit for them for at least one day before 4 January 2011
  • your child lives in the UK
  • they aren’t subject to any immigration restriction

If your child is looked after by a local council

They got a CTF account if both the following apply:

  • they were looked after by a local council before 3 April 2011
  • they meet the other qualifying conditions above (excluding child benefit)

If you work overseas and get a European family benefit

Your child got a CTF account if all of the following apply:

  • they were born between 1 September 2002 and 2 January 2011
  • your child lives in the UK
  • you were paid a family benefit by another European Union country for at least one day before 4 January 2011
  • you work in that same country

How it works

You can add up to £3,600 a year to the account. The money belongs to the child but can’t be taken out until they’re 18.

You could get extra money paid directly into the account - if you’re on a low income, for example.

There’s no tax to pay on the CTF income or any profits it makes. It won’t affect any benefits or tax credits you receive.

If your child isn’t eligible for a CTF account, you may be able to open a Junior ISA instead.

Part 2: Child Trust Fund vouchers

Vouchers worth £250 or £50, depending on when your child became entitled, were issued to Child Benefit claimants to open an account.

The voucher was sent to the person who gets Child Benefit (or the European family benefit).

HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) opened an account for your child if one of the following applied:

  • the voucher wasn’t used
  • a local council started to look after your child before you claimed Child Benefit

Part 3: Extra payments

Your child could get extra payments in some circumstances.

If you’re on a low income

Your child could get an extra payment of £250 or £50 on top of the starting payment. The amount you get will be the same as on your Child Trust Fund (CTF) voucher.

To get one of these payments both of the following must apply:

  • you qualify for Child Tax Credit
  • you are on a ‘low income’

‘Low income’ means your total household income before tax was:

  • £16,190 or less between 6 April 2010 and 5 April 2011
  • £16,040 or less between 6 April 2009 and 5 April 2010

HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) will pay these extra payments directly into your child’s account after you have:

  • renewed your tax credits (usually between April and July)
  • been sent your final tax credits award notice

Your child could also qualify for the extra CTF payment if you get any of the following for your child:

  • Income Support
  • income-based Jobseeker’s Allowance
  • Pension Credit
  • Employment and Support Allowance

If a local council looks after your child

If the local council started looking after your child before Child Benefit was claimed, the normal starting payment is doubled:

  • £500 - paid if your child was born between 1 September 2002 and 1 August 2010
  • £100 - paid if your child was born between 2 August 2010 and 2 January 2011

If the local council started to look after your child after Child Benefit was claimed, your child won’t be entitled to any extra money. They will just get the normal starting payment.

Backdated claims

Children who turned 7 between 1 September 2009 and 31 July 2010 or qualified for Disability Living Allowance (DLA) between 6 April 2009 and 5 April 2011 may be able to claim backdated payments.

Part 4: Account types

There are 3 main types of Child Trust Fund (CTF) account.

Stakeholder accounts

These have to follow government rules, including:

  • investing the money in a number of companies, not just one
  • moving the money to lower-risk investments when your child is 13

This helps protect your child’s money. All CTF providers offer stakeholder accounts.

Share accounts

These invest your child’s money by buying shares in companies. The value can go up or down.

Savings accounts

These work in the same way as a bank or building society account. Money invested is secure and earns interest.

For more help and general advice on money, visit the Money Advice Service website.

Part 6: Managing the account

If you’re the main contact for the Child Trust Fund (CTF) account you’re called the ‘registered contact’ and have certain responsibilities until the child is 16 and can manage their own account.

Your responsibilities as the registered contact

You’re the only person who can:

  • tell the account provider how to invest the fund and run the account
  • move the account to another provider
  • change the type of account

Paperwork you need to keep

Keep the following paperwork:

  • your child’s Unique Reference Number - this is shown on the voucher
  • the statements for the account
  • details of the type of account and the provider

This will make it easier if you need to query anything or make any changes to the account.

Changing accounts or providers

Check if the current provider offers the type of account you want to change to. If they don’t, you may need to try another provider.

To change provider, you need to:

  • choose a new provider
  • contact the new provider to check what forms you need to fill in

The new provider will get in touch with your existing account provider to arrange for the money to be moved. They will also tell HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) about the change.

From April 2013, new providers will no longer tell HMRC about account changes.

Changing a registered contact

You can change the registered contact if your family circumstances have changed.

Follow these steps:

  1. Get an application form from the provider.

  2. Both you - and the person who wants to be the new registered contact - sign the form.

  3. Return the form to the provider.

Only someone with parental responsibility for the child can be the registered contact, like a parent, step-parent or legal guardian.

If you split up from someone who is the registered contact and want to take this role on instead, both of you will need to agree to this.

When your child is 16 or 18

When your child is 16 they can take over the account and manage it. When they’re 18, they can take the money out.

A child can still choose to let their registered contact manage the account when they turn 16.

Part 7: Adding money to the account

Anyone can pay money into a Child Trust Fund (CTF) account.

How much you can add

You can put £3,600 a year into the account - the start date for the year is the child’s birthday and the end date is the day before their next birthday.

If the £3,600 limit isn’t used in a year, you can’t carry any unused amount forward to the following year to make up the difference.

How to pay money in

For stakeholder accounts you can add money by:

  • cheque
  • standing order
  • direct debit

For savings or share accounts, check with your provider.

Government payments

Payments made by the government don’t count towards the £3,600, apart from:

  • payments made by the Welsh government up to 31 January 2011
  • payments made by a local council to a child they are looking after

The year you open the account

There are different rules for the first year. The start date is the day the account is opened but the end date is still the day before the child’s next birthday.

Part 8: If your child is terminally ill or dies

You can take money out of your child’s Child Trust Fund (CTF) account if they are terminally ill. If your child has died, the money passes to whoever inherits the child’s estate (their property and possessions).

If your child is terminally ill

‘Terminally ill’ means that they have a disease or illness that is going to get worse and are likely to die within 6 months. Only the registered contact can take money out.

How to take money out

Contact the CTF Helpline to let them know that:

  • your child is terminally ill
  • you want to take the money out of the CTF account

You’ll need to provide evidence that your child is terminally ill.

If your child is already getting Disability Living Allowance (DLA) because of their illness, tell the CTF Helpline when you contact them.

If your child doesn’t get DLA, the CTF office will send you a form for your doctor to complete and return to them.

If your child dies

The money in the account will be paid to the person who inherits the child’s estate. This is often one of the child’s parents, but if your child was married, it could be their husband or wife.

If you weren’t able to open the account before your child died, HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) will still pay you what your child was entitled to.

If you get Child Benefit for a child who has died this can still carry on for a short time. It may also help with any extra costs you face at this time.

What you need to do

Tell the account provider. They’ll usually need proof, like the death certificate.
