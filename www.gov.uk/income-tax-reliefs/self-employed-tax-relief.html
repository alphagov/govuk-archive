Part 3: Self employed: tax relief

If you’re self-employed (a sole trader or partner in a partnership), you get tax relief on what you spend running your business.

You only pay Income Tax and National Insurance contributions on your profits.

You claim tax relief by filling in your Self Assessment tax return.

You must claim within 5 years of the end of the tax year that you spent the money.

Costs you can claim

You can claim tax relief on money that you must spend to run your business. For example:

  • wages and salaries you pay to staff
  • the cost of goods for your business (like stock for a retailer or materials for a builder)
  • rent of business premises (like an office or shop)
  • rent, rates, power and water your business premises uses
  • motor and travel expenses if they’re only for business (like visiting customers or delivering goods)
  • bad debts (money your business is owed but isn’t paid)
  • losses your business made in previous tax years
  • accountancy and professional fees
  • advertising
  • business bank, PayPal and credit card charges
  • business insurance
  • interest on business loans
  • a uniform with your business logo on it
  • postage
  • safety clothing that you need for your business

Costs you can’t claim

You can’t claim ‘private expenditure’ - money you spend on things that aren’t directly related to your business. For example, you can’t claim:

  • day-to-day living expenses, like food or clothing that isn’t related to work
  • household expenses
  • the amount you take as wages (sometimes called ‘drawings’)

If you use something both personally and for business - eg a landline at home - you can only claim for the part of the cost that you’ve used for business.

Equipment, machinery and property

If you buy equipment or machinery for your business, or things like furniture that you expect to last, you must claim the cost of them in a different way. These are called ‘capital allowances’.

More information

HM Revenue & Customs has more details on what you can and can’t claim when you’re self employed.
