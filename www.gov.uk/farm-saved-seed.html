Introduction

If you are a grower or farmer, you may use seed that you have saved for your own use to grow and harvest a crop - this is known as Farm Saved Seed (FSS).

Under section nine of the Plant Varieties Act 1997, farmers must declare all their use of FSS and pay for their use of eligible varieties. These royalty payments support continued investment in improved varieties, bred for UK conditions.

This guide explains which FSS species are eligible for payment, and how growers and farmers can ensure they have complied with the FSS rules. It also explains how to declare and make payments for FSS online, and offers helpful sources of further information. You can also find out what Plant Breeders’ Rights (PBR) are and how they may apply to your business.

Plant Breeders’ Rights and compliance with FSS rules

Plant breeding has vastly improved the yield, quality and performance of agricultural crops in the UK. It remains a vital industry to keep Great Britain internationally competitive.

Plant Breeders’ Rights give the breeder legal rights over the plant varieties they have developed and allow them to charge royalties for using these ‘protected’ varieties. If you are a breeder, this can provide funding for your work.

In the UK, PBR are administered by the Food and Environmental Research Agency (Fera) Plant Variety Rights Team in Cambridge.

FSS is seed planted on a farmer’s own land using seed harvested from their own holding. Your ‘own holding’ may comprise of more than one farm, as long as all holdings are managed under the same business. By law, you cannot market, purchase, transfer or give seed from one business to another for subsequent re-planting.

For more information, see the guide on Plant Breeders’ Rights (PBR).

How to comply with the FSS rules

You must declare all your use of FSS to the British Society of Plant Breeders (BSPB) and pay royalties on eligible varieties.

The legal requirement to declare applies whether the seed has been processed or taken straight from the barn.

If you receive an FSS declaration form from the BSPB, you are legally obliged to complete and return it to the BSPB if you have:

  • used 100% certified seed - you should simply declare this, sign the form and return it to the BSPB. You need take no further action.
  • had all your FSS processed via a registered seed processor, and have already paid to use eligible varieties - you should declare this, provide the relevant details, sign the form and return it to the BSPB. You need take no further action.
  • used FSS which has not been paid for via a registered processor, eg if it has been self-processed or sown straight from the barn, you must declare your use of that seed and make any payment due direct to the BSPB.

Farm Saved Seed and cereals

Under the Plant Varieties Act 1997, farmers must declare and pay for all eligible varieties of FSS they use to the BSPB.

The BSPB collects royalties on FSS from growers for the following cereals:

  • oats
  • barley
  • rice
  • canary grass
  • rye
  • triticale
  • wheat
  • durum wheat
  • spelt wheat

However, the BSPB and the UK farming unions have agreed that you do not need to pay for the genuine use of certain older varieties as FSS. These formerly zero-rated varieties now qualify for a 100% instant refund:

  • winter wheat
  • winter barley
  • spring barley
  • fresco
  • halcyon
  • chariot
  • haven
  • intro
  • dandy
  • hereward
  • manitou
  • hart
  • riband
  • tyne
  • soissons

You can find current payment rates and eligible varieties for combinable crops on the BSPB website.

Certification, marketing, packaging and labelling of cereal seed

If you produce and/or market cereal seed, your seed must be certified by Fera before it can be marketed. If you attempt to market seed that has not been certified, you could face legal action.

To be certified, the seed must be a variety on the UK National List or in the European Community Common Catalogue. For information on national listing, see the guide on the agricultural and vegetable crops: the National Lists.

Your crop must also be inspected and a sample from your seed lot must be tested. NIAB deals with all the applications for seed certification. You must lodge a crop inspection report with NIAB within one month of receiving it.

You must be registered with Fera before you can market seed covered by the cereal seed regulations. Packages of seed must be properly sealed and labelled, usually by a licensed seed sampler.

Farm Saved Seed and fodder plants

Under the Plant Varieties Act 1997, farmers must declare and pay for all eligible varieties of FSS they use to the BSPB.

The BSPB collects royalties on FSS from growers for these fodder plants:

  • chickpea milkvetch
  • yellow lupin
  • lucerne
  • field pea (except the Princess variety, which now qualifies for a 100% instant refund)
  • berseem/Egyptian clover
  • Persian clover
  • field bean (except the Victor variety, which now qualifies for a 100% instant refund)
  • common vetch

Fodder plant seeds marketing

Your seed must be officially certified by the Fera seed team, if you produce and/or market these fodder seeds in England:

  • fine grasses - including annual meadowgrass, brown top, creeping bent grass, festulolium, red fescuse, red top, wood meadowgrass and similar
  • fodder grasses - including Alaska brome-grass, cocksfoot, hybrid ryegrass, meadow fescue, tall oatgrass, Timothy and similar
  • small seeded legumes - including Alsike clover, birdsfoot trefoil, lucerne, red clover, sainfoin and similar
  • large seeded legumes - including blue lupin, field bean, field pea, hairy vetch, yellow lupin and similar
  • crucifers - including fodder kale, fodder radish and swede

Your seed will be certified if it meets these conditions:

  • Listing - the seed must be a variety or a hybrid on the UK National List or European Community Common Catalogue. For information on national listing, see the guide on agricultural and vegetable crops: the National Lists.
  • Crop inspection - your crop must be inspected and must meet prescribed crop conditions.
  • Seed sample testing - a sample from your seed lot must be tested and must meet prescribed seed conditions.

It is permitted to market certain combinations of fodder plant seeds and other species, but you need to be registered to market certified seed. All certified seed - including mixtures - must be packaged, sealed and officially labelled in accordance with the regulations. Fera maintains a record of all registered seed mixtures and preservation mixtures.

You could face legal action if you do not comply with the regulations.

Farm Saved Seed and oil and fibre plants

Under the Plant Varieties Act 1997, farmers must declare and pay for all eligible varieties of FSS they use to the BSPB.

The BSPB collects royalties on FSS from growers for the following oil and fibre plants:

  • swede rape
  • turnip rape
  • linseed, excluding flax

Certification, marketing and packaging of oil and fibre plant seed

If you produce and/or market oil and fibre plant seeds, your seed must be certified by Fera before it can be marketed. To become certified, the seed must be a variety or hybrid on the UK National List or in the European Community Common Catalogue. For information on national listing, see the guide on agricultural and vegetable crops: the National Lists.

Your crop must also be inspected and a sample from your seed lot tested. You could face legal action if you do not comply with the regulations.

NIAB deals with all applications for seed certification. This includes arranging crop inspections to ensure the crop meets specified standards. You must lodge a crop inspection report with NIAB within one month of receiving it.

If certification is withdrawn from a seed lot, you must notify anyone you have marketed the seed to within seven days of receiving the notice.

If you are marketing imported seed, you must supply Fera with the following details within a month of marketing the seed:

  • species
  • variety
  • category
  • quantity
  • country of production
  • official inspection authority
  • country of dispatch
  • importer

Packages of seed must be properly sealed and labelled, usually by a licensed seed sampler.

Farm Saved Seed and potatoes

Under the Plant Varieties Act 1997, farmers must declare and pay for all eligible varieties of FSS they use to the BSPB.

The BSPB collects royalties on FSS from growers of eligible potato varieties.

FSS potato rates for each eligible potato variety are set by the variety owners. Find information about payment rates and eligible varieties for potatoes on the BSPB website.

Under plant health regulations, potatoes can only be sown as FSS for one generation after certified seed. Growers who have purchased certified seed of potato varieties eligible for FSS payments will automatically receive an FSS declaration form to complete and return to BSPB the following year.

Farm Saved Seed and beet

Under the Plant Varieties Act 1997, farmers must declare all FSS they use to the BSPB and pay for eligible varieties. The BSPB collects royalties on FSS from growers of beet.

Certification, marketing, packaging and labelling of beet seeds

If you produce and/or market beet seed, your seed must be certified by the Fera before it can be marketed. To be certified, the seed must be a variety on the UK National List or in the European Community Common Catalogue. For more information, see the guide on agricultural and vegetable crops: the National Lists.

Your crop must also be inspected and a sample from your seed lot must be tested. NIAB deals with all applications for seed certification. You must lodge a crop inspection report with NIAB within one month of receiving it.

If certification is withdrawn from a seed lot, you must notify anyone you have marketed the seed to within seven days of receiving the notice. You must be registered with Fera before you can market seed covered by the beet seed regulations.

Packages of seed must be properly sealed and labelled, usually by a licensed seed sampler.

You may face legal action if you do not comply with the regulations.

Declaring and paying for Farm Saved Seed

There are several key FSS rules that farmers must follow. As a farmer, you:

  • Must declare all the FSS you use, whether it is eligible for payment or zero-rated. You must pay for eligible varieties. However, small farmers - as defined under EU law - are exempt and do not have to declare or pay for their use of FSS.
  • Can only use FSS that has been produced and saved within your own business - ie land farmed as the same business.
  • Cannot sell, buy, barter or transfer FSS outside your own holding.

These FSS rules always apply, whether the seed has been processed or taken straight from the barn.

Reporting FSS use

You can report your FSS use to the BSPB using any of these four methods:

  • send back the declaration form in the envelope provided
  • email the BSPB at enquiries@bspb.co.uk
  • make an online declaration for combinable crops and/or potatoes on the BSPB website
  • call the BSPB Farm Saved Seed Helpline on Tel 01353 653 209

Who sets the payment rates on FSS?

FSS payment rates for each crop species are agreed annually between the BSPB and farming unions, taking into account trends in the use of certified FSS and average royalty rates on certified seed. The exception is potatoes.

The BSPB publishes details of eligible varieties and payment rates annually, and automatically calculates and invoices any outstanding payments.

Who collects FSS payments and where does the money go?

BSPB collects FSS payments on behalf of plant breeders. Administration costs for the UK system amount to just seven pence per £1 collected, significantly less than equivalent systems in other EU member states. The remaining income is disbursed to individual plant breeders.

How can farmers pay BSPB to collect FSS payments?

There are two ways to pay BSPB for using eligible seed varieties:

  • via your National Association of Agricultural Contractors or BSPB-registered seed processor as a component of the processing invoice at a tonnage rate - most farmers pay this way
  • direct to the BSPB at a hectarage rate - for farmers who have not paid through their seed processor, or who have sown straight from the barn

BSPB publishes tonnage and hectarage rates annually.

Find information on FSS declarations and payments on the Fair Play website.

Find out about FSS payments on the BSPB website.

The Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra) helps the farming industry to operate as efficiently as possible. Defra administers European support policies that provide around £3 billion to UK agriculture. It administers the Whole Farm Approach, which offers a faster, more efficient way for farmers and growers to meet regulatory requirements. Defra also oversees the Fresh Start initiative to co-ordinate training and mentoring for rural entrepreneurs. You can call the Defra Helpline on 08459 33 55 77.

The Rural Payments Agency (RPA) is responsible for licences and schemes for growers as well as for running the Single Payment Scheme (SPS). For more information about SPS and how RPA can help your farming business, you can call the RPA Helpline on 0845 603 7777. You can also read the guide on the Single Payment Scheme (SPS).

In England, the Farm Advisory System advises farmers about cross compliance. For further information, call the Cross Compliance Helpline on 0845 345 1302. Alternatively, find information on cross compliance requirements on the Cross Compliance website.

The National Farmers’ Union (NFU) represents the farmers and growers of England and Wales. It aims to promote successful and socially responsible agriculture and horticulture, while ensuring the long-term viability of rural communities. You can call the NFU Callfirst Helpline on 0870 845 8458, or find out about their work and services on their website.

Farmers are likely to come into contact with local authorities, which are responsible for enforcing various regulations about farming, land use, food standards and environmental matters. Your local authority may also be able to provide further information or resources.

On 1 April 2009, four elements of Defra’s service came together to form a new Executive Agency of Defra, known as Fera. These four organisations were:

  • the Plant Health Division, which includes bee health
  • the Plant Health and Seeds Inspectorate
  • the Plant Variety Rights Office and Seeds Division
  • the Central Science Laboratory

The main purposes of Fera are to support and develop a sustainable food chain and a healthy natural environment, and to protect the global community from biological and chemical risks.

Through science and research, Fera is also responsible for policy and regulations on food and drink, wildlife, plant, bee and seed health, and the environment.

You can call the Fera Helpline on 01904 462 000, or find out about Fera’s work on their website.
