Introduction

Your metal production and processing business may need a permit, licence, authorisation or exemption to prevent your activities from causing pollution or harming human health. Metal production and processing includes manufacturing ferrous, non-ferrous and precious metals and alloys; metal forming processes; bar, wire and tube drawing; and metal casting.

Metal production and processing businesses can have a number of impacts on the environment, including:

  • air pollution from scrap metal processing, furnace fumes, dust emissions, mould production, and casting and cooling processes
  • land contamination from accidental oil and chemical spills, or past use of the site
  • noise pollution from materials handling, rolling mills, billet casting, air extraction equipment and vehicles
  • waste disposal such as off-cuts, refractories, slags, sludges, fluxes and lubricating fluids
  • water discharges including contaminated water from cooling and wet scrubbing processes, cleaning and testing

This guide will help you check if your business needs an environmental permit, registered exemption or other environmental authorisation, such as a trade effluent consent or hazardous waste registration.

You can be fined or even sent to prison if you don’t have the correct permit, licence, authorisation or exemption.

Listed activity permits for metal producers

You must have an environmental permit from the Environment Agency or your local authority if you carry out listed activities at an installation or mobile plant. You must ensure that the installation or mobile plant you use to carry out listed activities does not cause pollution.

An installation is a stationary technical unit, such as a self-contained building, permanent structure or fixed plant, used for listed activities.

Mobile plant is equipment that is designed to be, and will be, moved and is used for listed activities. When mobile plant is used on an installation it may become part of the installation activity, and so may be included in the permit of the installation.

Listed activities include industrial and waste activities that have an environmental impact. They are split into three categories - Part A(1), A(2) and B. Part A permits control a range of environmental impacts, including emissions to air, land and water. Part B permits control emissions to air only. 

Part A listed activities

Activities at metal production and processing sites regulated under Part A include:

  • producing iron and steel
  • producing non-ferrous metals from ore, concentrates or secondary raw materials by metallurgical, chemical or electrolytic activities
  • operating a coke oven
  • using hot rolling mills with a production capacity of more than 20 tonnes of crude steel per hour
  • using a furnace with a melting capacity of more than 4 tonnes per day for lead or cadmium, or more than 20 tonnes for all other non-ferrous metals
  • casting ferrous metals at a foundry with a production capacity of more than 20 tonnes per day

Part B listed activities

Activities at metal production and processing sites regulated under Part B include:

  • using a furnace with a net rated thermal input of 20-50 megawatts
  • using a furnace with a melting capacity of 4 tonnes or less per day for lead or cadmium, or 20 tonnes or less for all other non-ferrous metal
  • using a furnace with a production capacity of 20 tonnes or less per day for ferrous metal
  • desulphurising iron, steel or any ferrous alloy
  • heating metal to remove any non-metallic contaminant, unless the process is carried out in furnaces or appliances which together have a rated thermal input of less than 0.2 megawatts

See the guide on environmental permits - who needs one and how to comply.

Contact the Environment Agency or your local authority further information about listed activities.

Waste permits, exemptions and registrations for metal producers

If your business uses, stores, recovers or disposes of waste you must ensure it does not cause pollution. You must check if your waste activities need a permit or exemption from the Environment Agency. You must have the appropriate authorisations in place before you start an activity.

Environmental permits for waste activities: when you need one

You must have an environmental permit if your waste activities are classified as listed activities or waste operations.

Listed waste activities include some waste incineration plants and landfills.

Waste operations are waste disposal or recovery activities that require an environmental permit. Mobile plant can be used to carry out waste operations - this is known as waste mobile plant.

Waste operations that your metal production and processing business might carry out include:

  • recovering or recycling scrap metal
  • treating, storing and disposing of scrap metal

See the guide on environmental permits - who needs one and how to comply.

Standard rules are available for some waste operations, including:

  • metal recycling sites
  • storing furnace ready scrap metal for recovery

Find out about standard permits for waste operations on the Environment Agency website.

Waste exemptions: which activities they cover

You must usually register an exemption from environmental permitting if you carry out an exempt waste operation. You must comply with the conditions in your registered exemption.

Exempt waste operations for your metal production and processing business

Exempt waste operation Exemption reference Registration requirements
Storing any waste (non-hazardous or hazardous) temporarily on the site where it is produced in a secure place for no longer than 12 months, eg off-cuts, sand from casting processes Non-Waste Framework Directive exemption No need to register
Heating metals and metal alloys to remove grease, oil or any other non-metal contaminant using a furnace with a total rated thermal input of less than 0.2 megawatts T3 exemption Must be registered with your local authority
Running a scrap metal furnace You will need a part B environmental permit, unless your furnaces have a combined rated thermal input of less than 0.2 megawatts Must be registered with your local authority
Recovering scrap metal T9 exemption Must be registered with the Environment Agency

Find out about waste exemptions on the Environment Agency website. If you are not sure whether your waste operation is covered by an exemption, contact the Environment Agency Helpline on 03708 506 506 for advice.

You must renew your registered exemption every three years.

If you had a registered waste exemption before 6 April 2010 you may need to register a new exemption from environmental permitting or apply for an environmental permit. Download a timetable for when you need to apply for a new exemption or permit from the Environment Agency website (PDF, 62KB).

Waste carrier or broker: when you need to register

You can transport waste produced by your own business directly to an authorised waste management site or recycling facility without being registered, unless it is building or demolition waste.

You must register with the Environment Agency as a waste carrier if you transport:

  • construction and demolition waste produced by your own business
  • any waste produced by another business

See the guide on waste carriers, brokers and dealers.

Hazardous waste: when you need to register your premises

You must register your premises with the Environment Agency before any hazardous waste leaves your premises. The Environment Agency will issue you with a unique premises code.

Your premises will be exempt from registering if you produce less than 500 kilograms of hazardous waste in any 12-month period. You must use a registered or exempt waste carrier to remove the hazardous waste from your premises.

See the guide on managing your hazardous waste.

Radioactive substances permits and exemptions for metal producers

If your business uses radioactive substances you must take care to ensure you do not damage the environment or harm human health. You must minimise the amount of radioactive waste you create and dispose of any radioactive waste safely.

Metals production and processing businesses may have radioactive sources in:

  • radiology equipment for testing finished products
  • level-detection devices on furnaces
  • smoke-detection devices on furnaces
  • scrap metal that you buy to use in your furnace

Environmental permits: when you need one

You must have an environmental permit if you carry out radioactive substance activities, including:

  • keeping or using radioactive material at premises
  • disposing of radioactive waste on or from premises
  • accumulating radioactive waste on premises
  • receiving radioactive waste for disposal
  • keeping or using mobile radioactive apparatus for certain purposes

You may create radioactive waste from producing metals from primary minerals, or from reworking slags. See the guide on managing radioactive substances.

Excluded or exempt activities

You do not need environmental permitan if your activities are covered by an exemption order. Find out about exemption orders on the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs website.

The UK government is currently reviewing exemption orders. Find the review of exemption orders for radioactive materials and waste on the Department of Energy and Climate Change (DECC) website.

Radioactivity in scrap metal

Check scrap metal for radioactivity using a fixed or hand-held monitor. If you receive old factory machinery, always ask whether it uses or has ever used any radioactive sources.

Be suspicious of objects that are very heavy for their size. They may contain lead, but could also be made of depleted uranium which is radioactive and can be used to shield radioactive sources.

If you find an item with the radiation warning symbol, or you detect radiation with a fixed or hand-held monitor, contact the Environment Agency Helpline on 03708 506 506.

Water discharge permits for metal producers

Metals production and processing businesses may use large quantities of water in manufacturing processes and to cool equipment. Water discharges that your business might make include wastewater produced from:

  • discharge and blowdown from wet scrubbers
  • boiler operations
  • slag cooling processes
  • cooling baths
  • sand reclamation processes
  • leachate from slag and scrap metal tips
  • sludges from scrubbers
  • site drainage and stormwater run-off
  • washing and cleaning processes

Wastewater may contain contaminants such as heavy metals, eg zinc, cadmium and lead. It may also contain oils and greases from scrap metals or lubricants. Rainwater run-off may also be contaminated by materials you store or handle on your site.

If you discharge anything other than clean, uncontaminated run-off to surface water or groundwater you could cause water pollution. Surface waters include rivers, reservoirs and canals. Groundwater is all water located below the water table.

If you carry out a water discharge activity or a groundwater activity you must have an environmental permit or exemption from the Environment Agency.

For more information, see the guide on environmental permits - who needs one and how to comply.

Trade effluent consents and agreements for metal producers

Trade effluent is any liquid waste, other than domestic sewage and uncontaminated surface water, that you discharge from your business premises. 

Discharges that your metal production and processing business might make to a sewer include:

  • water running off car parking areas
  • trade effluent from industrial processes, eg sludges
  • water from cooling processes
  • water from washing and cleaning materials and equipment

A public foul sewer collects foul water (sewage and trade effluent) only. A surface water sewer collects surface water drainage only. A public combined sewer collects both foul and surface water drainage.

You must dispose of sewage from domestic facilities, eg toilets, basins and kitchens, to either a public foul sewer or to a public combined sewer.

You must dispose of trade effluent to either a public foul sewer or to a public combined sewer.

You must dispose of clean uncontaminated water to either a separate surface water sewer or to a public combined sewer.

Discharges to public sewers: when you need permission

You must get permission from your water and sewerage company before you discharge to their drainage system. If you are a tenant you may need your landlord’s permission to connect to a private surface water drain.

Before you release trade effluent into a public sewer you must have a trade effluent consent or enter into a trade effluent agreement with your water and sewerage company. Once you have a consent you must comply with its conditions. See the guide on discharging trade effluent.

You do not need permission to discharge sewage from domestic facilities to the nearest public foul sewer or to a public combined sewer. If you have any concerns about your discharges to sewer, contact your water and sewerage company.

If your business is in an area where you can’t be connected to a public sewer, you may have a septic tank or package treatment plant. However, you will need authorisation from the Environment Agency.

Download Pollution prevention guideline: Treatment and disposal of sewage where no foul sewer is available (PPG4) from the Environment Agency website (PDF, 245KB).

Water use licences for metal producers

If you take or store surface water or groundwater from any source, you are abstracting or impounding water.

If you abstract or impound surface water or groundwater you can affect the environment and other water users.

You may abstract or impound water to use in cooling processes in your metals production and processing business.

Water abstraction licence: when you need one

You must have an abstraction licence from the Environment Agency if you abstract more than 20 cubic metres (m³) of water per day from groundwater or surface waters.

Water impoundment licence: when you need one

You must have an impoundment licence from the Environment Agency if you impound (store) water on a watercourse, for example using a dam, weir or in a reservoir.

Flood defence consent: when you need one

You must have a flood defence consent from the Environment Agency before you:

  • carry out any work in, over, under or adjacent to a main river
  • build a flow control structure such as a culvert or a weir on an ordinary watercourse

Read about flood defence consents on the Environment Agency website.

Download Pollution prevention guideline: Works and maintenance in or near water (PPG5) from the Environment Agency website (PDF, 895KB).

Carbon and other air emission authorisations for metal producers

CO2 and other air emissions from your business’ activities can cause air pollution, contribute to climate change and harm human health. You must check if your emissions need a permit, registration or other consent from the Environment Agency or your local authority.

Greenhouse gas permit: when you need one

Some energy intensive businesses are required to reduce CO2 emissions and trade allowances under the European Union (EU) Emissions Trading System (ETS). You should check if your business needs a permit under the EU ETS.

You must have a greenhouse gas permit and report on your CO2 emissions, for example, if you:

  • carry out combustion activities with a rated thermal input exceeding 20 megawatts
  • roast and sinter metal ore - including sulphide ore
  • produce pig iron or steel (primary or secondary fusion), including by continuous casting, at a rate of more than 2.5 tonnes per hour

See the guide on how to meet EU ETS requirements.

Carbon Reduction Commitment Energy Efficiency Scheme: check if the scheme applies to you

The Carbon Reduction Commitment (CRC) Energy Efficiency Scheme, formerly the carbon reduction commitment, is designed to promote energy efficiency and reduce carbon emissions in the UK. It started in April 2010.

The scheme affects you if your business’ electricity is metered by at least one half hour meter and if you buy on the half hour market. Your electricity supplier can tell you if you buy electricity on the half hour market.

You are responsible for checking if the scheme applies to you. To check, you can email the CRC help team at CRChelp@environment-agency.gov.uk.

See the guide on the CRC Energy Efficiency Scheme.

Furnace or boiler approval: check if you need it

Your local authority must approve your plans and specifications before you can use a new furnace (except a domestic furnace), fixed boiler or industrial plant, or if you make changes to an existing furnace. Planning permission or a building warrant is not sufficient. You must be able to operate the furnace continuously without emitting smoke when using the correct type of fuel.

See the guide on preventing air pollution.
