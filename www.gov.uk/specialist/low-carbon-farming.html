Introduction

By 2020, the agriculture sector must reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from farming and agriculture by 3 million tonnes a year. This means that you will need to work to reduce your own GHG emissions, and there are some funding opportunities available to encourage you to do this.

Low carbon farming also presents business opportunities and can improve your business efficiency. It could help you meet growing customer demands for sustainability, save you money and increase your base income. It could also help to safeguard the future of your farm business.

This guide outlines some of the most effective ways of moving towards low carbon farming, explains the business and environmental benefits of these farming methods, and provides information about support and incentives that will help you to take up low carbon farming techniques.

Agriculture and climate change

Climate change is already affecting farming in the UK. Weather has become more unpredictable, the timing of seasonal events is changing and there are more extreme weather events such as droughts, storms and floods. As a result, climate change brings many threats to farmers, as well as opportunities - such as longer growing seasons and the possibility of new crops. There are already proven methods that farmers can use to reduce climate change, and to adapt to climate change that’s already occurred.

The main threats to farming from climate change could include:

  • reduced crop yields of certain crops, as well as lower crop quality and viability
  • an increase in pests and diseases
  • increased heat stress for livestock and farm workers
  • livestock feed contamination and mouldiness as a result of warmer, wetter weather
  • loss of biodiversity from flooding, drought, loss of pasture, increased risk of wild fires, pollution and soil erosion

However, the key climate change opportunities for farmers could include:

  • an increase in crop yields of certain crops as a result of longer growing seasons and reduced frost damage
  • lower energy costs in some areas of the UK due to a reduced need to dry crops
  • new opportunities for different crops and livestock species

How you can help mitigate climate change

As a farmer, there are several ways that you can reduce the impact that climate change could have on your business. These include:

  • reducing your energy use - see the guide on how to save money by using energy more efficiently
  • reducing your GHG emissions, particularly methane and nitrous oxide - see air pollution on farms
  • managing any woodland areas more efficiently - see trees and woodland
  • using biomass heating and other renewable energy - see using renewable energy 
  • using suppliers who are also reducing GHG emissions in their business activities - see reduce the environmental impact of your supply chain
  • using minimum tillage methods 
  • reducing the amount of fertiliser you use through efficient nutrient management - see managing nutrients and fertilisers

Adapting to the effects of climate change

As a farmer, climate change could directly affect your business. However, there are several ways that you can prepare your farm business to be ready to adapt to the impact of climate change. These include:

  • diversifying your crops to suit changes in the weather - see ideas and funding for diversification
  • installing water management systems
  • increasing biodiversity and helping different plants and animals to adapt - see conservation and biodiversity - an overview

Find the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra) farm biodiversity and climate change guidance on the Agricultural Document Library (ADLib) website.

You can also find Defra’s water management factsheet on the ADLib website.

Find Defra’s guidance on diversifying arable crops for climate change on the ADLib website.

Farm carbon auditing

One of the key steps in moving into low carbon farming is to work out the amount of GHG emissions your farm produces.

A whole farm carbon audit shows which parts of your farm produce the most GHG emissions, particularly CO2, methane and nitrous oxide. This will let you focus on how to reduce the worst of these emissions. A farm carbon audit will also let you estimate how much carbon you lock up (or sequester) through managing your soil and woodland.

A product life cycle carbon audit lets you estimate the GHG emissions associated with a specific product. For example, you could measure the GHG emissions created by producing 1 litre of milk or 1 tonne of grain. You can then identify where the biggest GHG emissions occur in the life cycle of the product and work out how to reduce them. You can also use this information to carbon label specific food products. This will help consumers identify and buy low carbon food.

Free carbon auditing tools

There are several free calculators that you can use to carbon audit your farm, including:

  • the Country Land and Business Association (CLA) Carbon Accounting for Land Managers (CALM) tool. Register to use the CALM calculator on the CLA website and read guidance on using the CALM tool on the CLA website
  • the CPLAN GHG calculator - use the carbon calculator for farms on the CPLAN website
  • the Cool Farm Tool - find out about the Cool Farm Tool on the Growing For The Future website

For a product life cycle carbon audit, you could use the Publicly Available Specification (PAS) 2050 - find guidance on downloading and using PAS2050 for your farm’s carbon audit on the British Standards Institution (BSI) website (registration required).

To estimate lifecycle GHG emissions resulting from the production and supply of your biofuels, you can use the biofuel GHG calculator on the Home Grown Cereals Authority (HGCA) website.

Conducting a farm carbon audit

Once you have chosen a calculator for your carbon audit, you should:

  • define the boundaries of your audit - eg what area will it cover and how long you want your carbon accounting period to be
  • gather the data you need for your carbon calculator - eg electricity and fuel used, livestock and crops data, nutrients information, changes in land use
  • input the data into the carbon calculator and find out the total

You can then use these results to compare your GHG emissions with other farms, and come up with ways to reduce your GHG emissions and save energy or other inputs.

Before you start using a carbon calculator, it’s a good idea to read more about data gathering, so you have a better idea of all the data you’ll need. You can find Defra’s carbon accounting information for farms on the ADLib website.

Bioenergy and anaerobic digestion

Slurry, manures, other farm waste and food waste create methane, which is a powerful greenhouse gas. Anaerobic digestion (AD) can be used to reduce methane emissions into the atmosphere and to generate energy from the biogas produced. The end material, known as digestate, can be used as a fertiliser. Energy crops, eg silage and maize, can also be used to increase the energy yield from the AD process.

The process can generate:

  • heat
  • biogas, which you can use as a renewable source of energy, eg for combined heat and power, electricity, injection into the gas grid or as a fuel for vehicles
  • digestate - both treated liquid and solids that you can use and sell as a fertiliser, subject to quality standards

In order to sell gas into the gas grid or use it as transport fuel biogas needs to be upgraded, or cleaned up, into Biomethane.

It is important to plan for the use of the digestate that the anaerobic digestor will produce. You can find guidance on producing and using digestate on the Biogas website.

For more information, see minimising farm waste, composting and recycling.

You can find information about AD, biogas and digestate for farmers on the Biogas website.

You can carry out AD on a small scale using your own manure, slurries and food waste from your own farm. Alternatively, you could get involved in larger scale AD, processing waste from clusters of farms and other businesses, and from local authorities that collect households’ food waste in order to avoid sending it to landfill.

Whatever your source and type of feedstock (ie the material that goes into the AD), it will need to meet quality standards. Feedstock should be either non-waste inputs, or source-segregated biodegradable waste as identified in the Quality Protocol for Anaerobic Digestate.

If you intend to use animal by-products in your feedstock, you should use a biogas plant that complies with Animal By-Products Regulations, and also comply fully with other aspects of regulations. For more information, see dealing with animal by-products.

However, not all AD schemes are suitable for your business - for example, they may not be economically viable. If you are treating food waste, you need to ensure that you have consistent access to the right feedstock and so security of supply is an important issue to consider. So it is essential to assess the pros and cons of your proposed AD before deciding whether to implement it.

You can use an AD calculator to assess its suitability for your business on the National Non-Food Crops Centre website.

AD regulations

If you decide to use AD, you must comply with regulations on:

  • environmental permitting
  • animal by-products
  • duty of care
  • health and safety
  • planning permission
  • digestate quality
  • excise duty for biogas used as a road fuel

Read about AD regulations on the Biogas website.

Costs and benefits of using AD

Implementing an AD system will cost you money, eg capital, installation and operating costs, but this will depend on the size of the anaerobic digester. How economically viable it is to install will also depend on other factors, such as the type of feedstock and operating efficiency. Because of economies of scale, larger ADs are more likely to be profitable.

New ADs may also qualify for the Enhanced Capital Allowance (ECA) scheme for quality-assured Combined Heat and Power. For more information, see the guide on first-year allowances for energy saving products.

The main cost of AD is the capital cost. However, you should also consider running costs, including insurance, maintenance, repairs and labour.

Set against these costs, AD can provide you with an income from:

  • selling renewable heat or electricity to local customers or to the gas or electricity grid
  • producing biofuel by upgrading your biogas (although this can be an expensive process)
  • saving energy costs by generating heat and power for your farm and other buildings
  • using and/or selling digestate in place of manufactured fertiliser

Incentive schemes for farmers for producing and selling renewable, gas, electricity and transport biofuel include:

  • double Renewables Obligation Certificates (ROCs), which reward renewable energy generation - find information on ROCs on the Ofgem website
  • the Renewable Feed-In Tariff (FIT) for systems below 5 megawatts - find information on the Renewable FIT (Clean Energy Cashback) scheme on the Energy Saving Trust (EST) website 
  • Renewable Transport Fuel Certificates under the Renewable Transport Fuel Obligation
  • the Renewable Heat Incentive (RHI), aims to provide financial assistance for biogas heating and combined heat and power systems - find information on RHI on the Department of Energy and Climate Change (DECC) website

Finance for farm energy efficiency

If you want to buy energy-saving equipment for your farm, the Carbon Trust offers leases, loans and other financing options for businesses seeking to reduce their energy use.

New, more efficient equipment should lower your energy bills and loan payments are calculated so that they can be offset by the anticipated energy savings. Read about finance for energy-saving projects on the Carbon Trust website.

Enhanced capital allowance (ECA) tax breaks

If you buy energy-efficient technology that is on the Energy Technology List (ETL) or is a qualifying non-listed technology or product, you could be eligible to apply for an ECA tax allowance. Find out if your product/technology qualifies for a tax allowance with the ETL search tool on the ECA website.

If the technology you have invested in, or want to invest in, does qualify for an ECA, you can claim for it as part of your normal corporate or income tax return. However, HM Revenue & Customs may want to see all the documents related to the purchase and installation of the technology, so you should keep these records. For more information, see first-year allowances for energy saving products.

Sustainable food

The UK aims to establish a food chain system that will make sustainable, healthy and affordable diets more available to people. It will also make the UK food and farming industries more resilient in the face of future risks - eg climate change, commodity price increases, disruptions to fuel supplies or transport networks, natural disasters and food contamination.

There are several incentives and opportunities in place for farmers and growers to produce sustainably farmed food. These encourage you to increase your productivity and profitability and to secure your farm business. For example, you can:

  • build up skills in innovative farming methods based on new research
  • develop farming methods to enable quick responses to domestic and international price changes
  • improve the use of IT and efficient technology on your farm
  • work to conserve and enhance biodiversity and landscapes

Food labelling schemes

As a farmer, low carbon food labelling schemes and food assurance schemes can give you a competitive edge in the market. Read about food assurance schemes on the Food Standards Agency website.

You can also read about the Carbon Reduction Label on the Carbon Trust website.

The European Union (EU) Protected Food Name Scheme can add value to regional and traditional foods, as well as helping you market these products. Read about registering your food product for the Protected Food Name Scheme on the ADAS website.

Locally produced food

Consumers in the UK are increasingly aware of the benefits of buying and eating locally produced food. As a farmer and/or grower, you can take advantage of this demand by selling your produce through farm shops and farmers’ markets. For more information, see the guide on farm shops and farmers’ markets.

For information about support for innovations in sustainable farming and food, see the guide on the Technology Strategy Board.

Sustainable farming skills

You can also improve and develop your sustainable farming skills by attending free, RDPE-funded LandSkills training programmes.

If you are a farmer in the East of England, you can also apply for a Low Carbon short Knowledge Transfer Partnerships (sKTPs) to bring new low carbon skills into your farming. Read about Low Carbon sKTPs on the East of England Development Agency website.

For more information, see agricultural skills and training.
