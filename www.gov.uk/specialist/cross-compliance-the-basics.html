Introduction

The term ‘Cross Compliance’ refers to the requirement for farming practices to meet certain standards. These apply to you if you receive direct payments under Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) support schemes or through certain Rural Development Schemes. This includes the Single Payment Scheme (SPS) - the EU’s main agricultural subsidy scheme.

The standards that have to be met as part of Cross Compliance are known as Statutory Management Requirements (SMRs) and Standards of Good Agricultural and Environmental Conditions (GAECs). They apply to all farming activities across your entire agricultural holding, regardless of the amount of land you have entered into the SPS.

This guide contains general information about SMRs and GAECs, the requirements you must meet, key dates and how to assess your current levels of compliance. There is information about the inspection process, and how direct payments can be reduced if Cross Compliance requirements aren’t met.

Cross Compliance requirements and key areas of farming activity

If you receive CAP payments - eg the single payment - or certain other Rural Development Scheme payments, you must meet Cross Compliance requirements. These requirements apply across all farming activities - with the majority falling into the following key areas:

  • record keeping
  • soil management, cultivation and ploughing
  • spraying and spreading
  • harvesting and post-harvesting activities
  • keeping livestock
  • maintaining landscapes and protecting habitats

GAECs and/or SMRs apply to each key farming activity. For example, SMRs 16, 17 and 18 apply to keeping livestock and record keeping for animal welfare purposes.

You can find a list of key dates on the Rural Payments Agency (RPA) website.

Cross Compliance requirements for 2011

Cross Compliance is a system that requires farmers to fulfil certain requirements if they are to receive their payments under the Common Agricultural Policy. It sets the baseline standards that you must meet to receive your payment.

These requirements are split into two types:

  • Domestic legal requirements to keep land in good agricultural and environmental condition. These mainly protect soils, habitats and landscape features. See the guide on standards of Good Agricultural and Environmental Condition (GAEC).
  • European legal requirements known as SMRs. These cover environmental and plant health objectives, as well as animal health and welfare. See the guide on SMRs).

Although not directly applicable to individual farmers, national requirements to maintain a level of permanent pasture - ie land under grass with no other crops for five years or more - will also influence farming practices.

You can find frequently asked questions on the Cross Compliance website.

If there is a conflict between the requirements of a land-based rural development scheme and GAEC measures, you will normally have to meet the rural development requirements. If you are in any doubt, you should contact the RPA Customer Service Centre on Tel 0845 603 7777 or via email at CSC@rpa.gsi.gov.uk.

Cross Compliance responsibility for the whole year

Although you can activate entitlements for any land at your disposal up until 16 May, you are responsible for Cross Compliance requirements for the whole calendar year. For example, if you take on some agricultural land on 1 April and it forms part of your holding on 16 May, you are liable for any breaches of Cross Compliance that occurred between 1 January and the date you took on the land. There is one exception to this rule. If the transferor in this case submits a claim in that calendar year, they will be liable for breaches during the period that the land was at their disposal.

Similarly, if you claim on some land which you then transfer out after 16 May, you will still have to ensure that cross compliance requirements relating to that land are met until the end of the calendar year. There is also an exception to this rule, namely where the transferee submits a claim in that calendar year, they will be liable for cross compliance breaches during the period that the land was at their disposal.

So, it may be wise to include Cross Compliance requirements in the terms of any contract with the new land manager.

Cross Compliance inspections

Your farm can be selected for a Cross Compliance inspection if you receive payments from:

  • SPS 
  • Area Payment for Nuts 
  • Protein Crop Premium 
  • Environmental Stewardship Schemes (ELS, OELS, UELS and HLS) 
  • Uplands Transitional Payment (UPS) 
  • Energy Crop Scheme 
  • English Woodland Grant Scheme

Inspections are carried out by the four Competent Control Authorities (CCAs), each having different areas of responsibility relating to specific standards of GAEC and the SMRs.

The four CCAs and their inspection responsibilities

CCA Areas of responsibility

Rural Payments Agency (RPA) SMRs 1, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 11 & 12 GAECs 1 to 18

Environment Agency SMRs 2 to 4 GAEC 18

Veterinary Medicines Directorate SMR 10

Animal Health SMRs 13 to 18

Each CCA will select and carry out inspections on farms that need to comply with the requirements covered by its area of responsibility. Inspections are mainly chosen by risk assessment, but there is also a random element.

Due to the random element, it’s possible for your farm to be inspected more than once in a calendar year. Your business could also be selected for inspection by more than one CCA.

You will be given advance notice of an inspection where possible, but they can be unannounced. In either case, you must co-operate with the inspectors. If you don’t, your payments may be reduced or withheld, and/or an animal movement restriction could be served on your farm.

The inspector will explain what they have found before leaving your farm. They will then write-up a full report to submit to RPA, who will use the report to assess if there has been any non-compliance.

Any breaches may result in a reduction to your payment. The level of reduction is dependent upon the seriousness of the non-compliance - see the section below on penalties for breaching Cross Compliance requirements.

RPA will write to you to confirm the findings of the inspection, but they cannot confirm any payment reduction until the results of all inspections carried out during the calendar year have been assessed.

Penalties for breaching Cross Compliance requirements

If you receive payment under the SPS, other CAP support schemes or certain rural development schemes, you face penalties if you don’t meet your Cross Compliance obligations.

Remember that your Cross Compliance requirements apply in addition to any other underlying European and UK legislation. If you don’t meet those obligations you could face other penalties, as well as losing CAP payments.

All agricultural activities are covered by Cross Compliance and you must comply with the requirements across the whole agricultural area of your holding. This is regardless of how much land you have entered into the SPS.

SPS payment reductions for non-compliance

If you negligently fail to meet a standard or requirement, your SPS payments and other direct payments can be reduced by between 1 and 5 per cent for the first instance of non-compliance. The figure depends on the extent, severity and permanence of the breach.

The starting point for a first time breach that is rectifiable is 1 per cent for SPS 2011 (including direct payments for Area Payments for Nuts, Protein Crop Premium and Aid for Energy Crops) and for Pillar 2 schemes, such as Environmental Stewardship schemes. EU regulations state that the reduction can be increased or reduced, depending on the severity of the breach. Intentional repetitive non-compliance can result in your payment being reduced by up to 100 per cent.

You can find the payment reduction matrixes which the RPA uses to assess deductions on their website.

RPA publishes an annual breakdown of non-compliances found during inspections. This includes details of the most common breaches.

You can contact the Cross Compliance Helpline on Tel 0845 345 1302 for further guidance. Alternatively, you can send an email enquiry to advice@crosscompliance.org.uk.

Cross Compliance self assessment tool

Whole Farm Approach (WFA) is an online service to support registered farmers and growers. WFA enables you to save time and money by simplifying and unifying the way in which you:

  • receive relevant news
  • report farming data to Defra and other government departments

The Cross Compliance self assessment tool allows you to quickly and easily assess your current farming practices against both the GAECs and the SMRs that constitute Cross Compliance. It will provide you with an action plan, a checklist and tailored advice to help you meet your requirements.

Register for the Defra Farm Surveys and Assessments service to access the Cross Compliance self-assessment tool.

Organisations that can help

Below are details of organisations that offer support and advice about cross compliance.

In England, the Farm Advisory System advises farmers about cross compliance. For further information, call the Cross Compliance Helpline on Tel 0845 345 1302. Alternatively, find information on the Cross Compliance website.

The National Farmers’ Union (NFU) represents the farmers and growers of England and Wales. It aims to promote successful and socially responsible agriculture and horticulture, while ensuring the long-term viability of rural communities.

You can read about the work of the NFU on the NFU website.

One of the major roles of Defra is to help the farming industry operate as efficiently as possible. Defra administers European support policies that provide around £3 billion to UK agriculture. They also oversee a number of agencies that work with arable farmers, imports and exports of crops and implement pest and disease controls. You can call the Defra Helpline on Tel 08459 33 55 77.

Farmers are likely to come into contact with local authorities over a number of farming, land use, food standards and environmental regulations. Your local authority may also be able to provide further information or resources.
