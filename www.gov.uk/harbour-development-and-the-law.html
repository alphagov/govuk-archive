Harbour development and the law

Applying for different types of harbour order to enable construction, improvement or maintenance, and the fees involved.

Introduction

The development and operation of harbours is strictly controlled by law. The regulations allow ‘harbour orders’ to be made to change existing harbour legislation - or to introduce new legislation - to allow a particular harbour to be constructed, improved, maintained, managed or developed.

There are three different types of order, depending on the specific circumstances and who is making the application.

New applications for harbour orders are made to the Marine Management Organisation (MMO). There is a detailed application process to follow which may include carrying out an environmental impact assessment and providing an environmental statement. In sensitive areas a public inquiry may be held. The MMO charges fees to process harbour orders.

This guide explains the different types of harbour order and outlines the application process. It sets out the different fees and includes links to more information on the MMO website.

Types of harbour order

The development and operation of harbours is controlled by the Harbours Act 1964, as amended. The legislation allows ‘harbour orders’ to be made. These are used to give a person or an organisation powers to make improvements or changes to the way a harbour operates. Orders are also made to enable new construction work to be carried out.

Depending on who is applying and the reason for the application, the legislation provides for three different types of order, namely:

  • section 14 harbour revision orders
  • sections 15 and 15A harbour revision orders
  • section 16 harbour empowerment orders

Section 14 harbour revision orders

A harbour authority (or an individual or body with a substantial interest in the harbour) can apply to the MMO to make a harbour revision order. The purpose of the order is to enable the authority to achieve any or all of the ‘objects’ set out in Schedule 2 of the Harbours Act. These objects amend or strengthen an authority’s existing powers so that it can improve, maintain or manage the harbour efficiently and economically. The order must be in the interests of the harbour.

Sections 15 and 15A harbour revision orders

The MMO can make these orders on their own initiative. A section 15 harbour revision order is made to enable a harbour to operate as efficiently as possible by:

  • reconstituting the harbour authority or altering its constitution
  • regulating the harbour authority’s procedures

Under a section 15A harbour revision order the MMO can withdraw from appointing members of the harbour authority. The MMO can grant that power of appointment to someone else named in the order.

Section 16 harbour empowerment orders

You can apply for a harbour empowerment order if you are:

  • constructing a new harbour
  • improving, managing or maintaining an existing harbour where the legal powers to do so don’t exist

Assessment of environmental impact

Harbour developments are likely to have a significant impact on the site’s environment, birds and wildlife due to their nature and scale.

Regulations are in place that require the effect of major developments on the environment to be assessed and controlled.

If your proposed project is included in Annex I of the Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) Directive you must carry out an EIA. Annex II of the Directive contains details of projects which need an EIA only if their impact is likely to be significant. You can see the projects listed in Annexes I and II of the EIA Directive on the European Commission (EC) website.

Other regulations protect natural habitats, birds and wildlife. You must consider the effect of your proposed project on the site. An appropriate assessment must be carried out if the impact is likely to be significant. The purpose of the assessment is to find out whether the project will have a negative effect on the site, even taking into account any mitigating, or compensating, measures that are put in place.

If the assessment finds that there will be an adverse effect on the site, your project will only be approved if:

  • there are no alternative solutions
  • it is in the public interest to go ahead
  • compensating measures are put in place

You can read about the regulations protecting birds and animals in Europe on the Natural England website.

Processing harbour orders

If you want to apply to the MMO for a harbour order, you’ll need to follow a formal procedure.

Carry out consultations

Before submitting a formal application, it is advisable to consult widely with local bodies and organisations that may have an opinion on the project. If the proposed order relates to construction works, it is best to consult with the MMO’s standard consultees, which include organisations such as Natural England and the Environment Agency.

Prepare an environmental statement

If it is likely that your project will need an environmental impact assessment you can ask the MMO for a ‘formal screening opinion’. This will tell you whether you need to prepare an environmental statement. The MMO can also give you a ‘scoping opinion’ to help you with what the statement should cover.

Get legal advice and prepare a draft order

The MMO can comment on draft orders but does not offer legal advice - it is advisable to get your own legal advice when you prepare the draft order.

Make a formal application

With your application you’ll need to include:

  • a statement saying why the order is necessary
  • six copies of the order and supporting documents
  • 14 copies of the environmental statement - if required
  • the fee

Advertise the order

You must advertise the order in the London Gazette and in local papers.

Objections and representations received

Objections and representations can be made during a 42-day period from the date the advertisement first appears.

Make changes to the draft order

You can make changes to your formal application in response to objections and comments.

Attend a public inquiry

In particularly sensitive cases or where objections have not been resolved, a public inquiry may be held. The MMO will recover from you the costs of holding the inquiry.

Order made

Once the order has been made you must advertise this in the same publications that carried the first advertisement for the order.

Timescales

How long the process takes varies depending on:

  • the complexity of your proposal
  • the level of public interest
  • how many objections there are

You can meet with the MMO ahead of submitting the formal application if you want to clarify the process. You can email the MMO at harbourorders@marinemanagement.org.uk or write to them at the following address:

Marine Management Organisation
PO Box 1275
Newcastle upon Tyne
NE99 5BN

Fees for processing harbour orders

The MMO has a fixed scale of charges for processing harbour orders. Currently the fees are:

  • £2,000 - for an order which only amends the borrowing powers of an existing authority
  • £4,000 - for any other order provided this doesn’t give an authority the power to carry out works
  • £6,000 - for an order which gives an authority the power to carry out works but only where there is no need for an environmental impact assessment
  • £10,000 - for an order which gives an authority the power to carry out works where there is a requirement for an environmental impact assessment to be undertaken

You’ll need to pay the fee when you submit your formal application.

Public inquiry costs

The MMO will recover the costs of holding a public inquiry from the applicant after the inquiry has been held and the inspector has made their report.
