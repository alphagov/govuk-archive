Becoming a British citizen, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Check if you can apply

You can apply for British citizenship if:

  • you’re 18 or over
  • you’re of sound mind - eg you can make your own decisions (or have someone applying on your behalf who can)
  • you’re of good character
  • you will continue to live in the UK
  • you’re able to communicate in English, Welsh or Scottish Gaelic
  • you’ve passed the Life in the UK Test or have completed an English for speakers of other languages (ESOL) course along with citizenship classes

This process is known as ‘naturalisation’.

You must also have:

  • lived in the UK for at least 5 years before the date of your application (unless your spouse or civil partner is a British citizen)
  • spent no more than 450 days outside the UK during those 5 years
  • spent no more than 90 days outside the UK in the last 12 months
  • been granted indefinite leave to stay in the UK, ie there’s no specific date that you have to leave
  • had indefinite leave to stay in the UK for the last 12 months (unless your spouse or civil partner is a British citizen)
  • not broken any immigration laws while in the UK

Time spent in the UK when you’re exempt from immigration control (eg as a diplomat or member of visiting armed forces) can’t be included as part of the 5 years.

Children under 18

You can apply on behalf of a child under 18 if they meet the eligibility criteria. They don’t have to pass the Life in the UK Test.

There’s more information in ‘Guide MN1’.

Part 2: If your spouse is a British citizen

If you’re married to, or the civil partner of, a British citizen, you can apply for citizenship if:

  • you’re 18 or over
  • you’re of sound mind - eg you can make your own decisions (or have someone applying on your behalf who can)
  • you’re able to communicate in English, Welsh or Scottish Gaelic
  • you’ve passed the Life in the UK Test or have completed an English for speakers of other languages (ESOL) course along with citizenship classes
  • you’re of good character

Unless your spouse or civil partner works abroad for a part of the British government, an international organisation or a company established in the UK, you must also have:

  • lived in the UK for at least 3 years
  • spent no more than 270 days outside the UK in those 3 years
  • spent no more than 90 days outside the UK in the last 12 months
  • not broken any immigration laws while in the UK

Part 3: Life in the UK Test

If you’re over 18 you have to take the Life in the UK Test as part of your application. The test includes questions about British traditions and customs, as well as proving that you can speak English.

You can book your Life in the UK Test and buy the official handbook.

If you can’t speak English, instead of taking the Life in the UK Test you can take a course in English for speakers of other languages (ESOL) along with citizenship classes.

Part 4: How to apply

There are 3 ways to apply for British citizenship:

  • an individual application
  • using the Nationality Checking Service (NCS)
  • through an agent or representative

Make an individual application

To make an individual application you must fill in one of the following forms:

Download ‘Form AN - application for naturalisation as a British citizen’ (PDF, 111KB)

Download ‘Form MN1 - application for naturalisation as a British citizen on behalf of a child under 18’ (PDF, 500KB)

You must also read the guidance booklet which tells you how to fill in the form and what other documents you need to provide:

Download ‘Guide AN - naturalisation as a British citizen’ (PDF, 202KB)

Apply using the NCS

The NCS is run by local councils and they can help you make your application. Check if your local council has an NCS.

Apply through an agent or representative

An agent or representative is a private company or individual that can help you with your application and give you advice.

You should check if an agent or representative is registered with the Office of the Immigration Services Commissioner.

Part 6: After you’ve applied

You’ll get a letter confirming your application. It can take up to 4 weeks for you to get the confirmation.

A decision about your application will usually be made within 6 months, but some applications can take longer.

You’ll be told if you need to give more information to help with your application. You should also contact the UK Border Agency if your situation changes (eg, you move house, get married or are arrested) during your application.

Nationality contact centre
ukbanationalityenquiries@ukba.gsi.gov.uk

You might be asked to attend an interview where you’ll need to speak without an interpreter.

Part 7: Citizenship ceremonies

If your application is successful and you’re over 18, you’ll be invited to attend a citizenship ceremony.

You must attend a citizenship ceremony if you’re living in the UK at the time.

Your ceremony will usually take place close to where you live.

You’re usually allowed to take 2 guests with you and you must take your invitation with you when you go to your ceremony.

Citizenship ceremonies are organised by your local council and are usually done in groups. If you want to arrange a private ceremony or have any special requirements, you can organise your citizenship ceremony with your council.

During the ceremony

You’ll have to make an ‘oath of allegiance’ (or you can make an ‘affirmation’ if you prefer not to swear by God) and a pledge. This means you’ll promise to respect the rights, freedoms and laws of the UK.

Citizenship certificate

You’ll be presented with your certificate of British citizenship and a welcome pack.

Some local councils arrange for photographs or videos of the event to be available for you to buy.
