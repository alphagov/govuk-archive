Junior Individual Savings Accounts (ISA), a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

Junior ISAs are long term, tax-free savings accounts for children.

Who can get a Junior ISA

Your child can have a Junior ISA if they:

  • are under 18
  • live in the UK
  • aren’t entitled to a Child Trust Fund (CTF) account

Your child can’t get a Junior ISA if they’re eligible for a CTF account.

How Junior ISAs work

There are 2 types of Junior ISA:

  • a cash Junior ISA, ie you won’t pay tax or interest on the cash you save
  • a stocks and shares Junior ISA, ie your cash is invested and you won’t pay tax on any capital growth or dividends you receive.

Your child can have 1 or both types of Junior ISA.

Part 2: Opening an account

Only parents or a guardian with parental responsibility can open a Junior ISA for under 16s.

To open a Junior ISA you need to:

  • choose the type of Junior ISA you want for your child - cash or stocks and shares (or both)
  • choose your account provider
  • get an application form from them

Your child can only have:

  • 1 cash Junior ISA
  • 1 stocks and shares Junior ISA

Children aged 16 to 18 can open their own Junior ISAs.

Part 3: Account providers

You can get a Junior ISA from a range of banks, building societies, credit unions, friendly societies and stock brokers.

Contact any of these direct for more information about how you can open a Junior ISA with them.

Part 4: Adding money to an account

Anyone can pay money into a Junior ISA, but the total amount can’t go over £3,600 in a tax year.

Example
If your child has £1,000 paid into their cash Junior ISA from 6 April 2012 to 5 April 2013, only £2,600 could be paid into their stocks and shares Junior ISA in the same tax year.

You can also transfer money between your child’s Junior ISAs but you can’t do this with:

  • a Junior ISA and a regular ISA
  • a Junior ISA and a CTF account

If your child moves abroad, you can still add cash to their Junior ISA.

Who the money belongs to

Money in a Junior ISA belongs to your child and can’t be taken out until they’re 18, though there are exceptions to this.

Part 5: Managing an account

Your child’s Junior ISA will be in their name, but the parent who opens this is responsible for managing the account and known as the ‘registered contact’.

The registered contact is the only person who can:

  • change the account, eg from a cash to a stocks and shares Junior ISA
  • change the account provider
  • report changes of circumstances, eg change of address

Contact your account provider to do this.

Children older than 16

If your child is 16 or older they can:

  • become the registered contact for their Junior ISAs
  • open a regular ISA

When your child turns 18 they can take out any money in their Junior ISAs.

Junior ISAs automatically turn into a regular ISA when the child turns 18.

Part 6: If your child is terminally ill or dies

The registered contact can take money out of a Junior ISA early if a child’s terminally ill.

How to take money out

Contact HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) to let them know that:

  • your child is terminally ill
  • you want to take money out of their Junior ISA

You’ll need to provide evidence that your child’s terminally ill.

If your child gets Disability Living Allowance (DLA) because of their illness, tell the HMRC when you contact them.

If your child doesn’t get DLA, HMRC will send you a form for your doctor to complete and return to the CTF office, who deal with all terminal illness cases for Junior ISAs and CTF accounts.

For more information, you can call the CTF helpline:

CTF Helpline
Telephone: 0845 302 1470
From outside the UK: +44 1355 359 002
Textphone: 0845 366 7870

What happens next

HMRC will let you and your account provider know if you can take money out of your child’s Junior ISA.

Inheritance

If your child dies, any money in their Junior ISAs will be paid to whoever inherits their estate.

This is usually one of the child’s parents but could be your child’s husband or wife if they were married.

What you need to do

You don’t need to contact HMRC but you’ll need to tell your account provider so they can close your child’s Junior ISAs.

Your account provider may need need proof to do this, eg a copy of the death certificate.
