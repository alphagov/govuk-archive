Frequently asked questions about Technical Specifications for Interoperability (TSIs)

Detail

What is a TSI?

A TSI is a common, harmonised, technical standard required to satisfy the essential requirements of interoperability which include safety, reliability and availability, health, environmental protection and technical compatibility.

Depending on their scope, TSIs may also contain other specific requirements relating to either ‘structural’ subsystems (such as infrastructure, rolling stock, energy, and control/command and signalling) or ‘functional’ subsystems (such as maintenance, traffic operation and management and telematics applications for passengers and freight services).

TSIs are prepared by the European Railway Agency (‘ERA’) under a mandate from the European Commission. The mandate specifies the format which TSIs must conform to and the method that ERA must use to prepare them.

What is the role of TSIs

A Contracting Entity, which has responsibility for the management of any project, must ensure that the essential requirements of safety, reliability and availability, health, environmental protection and technical compatibility are met. The application of TSIs removes the need for individual Contracting Entities to decide how the essential requirements should be met since they set out the means whereby, for the specific situations within their scope, these will be met. TSIs also drive standardisation by mandating interfaces and assessment methodologies.

The TSI framework is supplemented by NNTRs which, in respect of open (unresolved) points in the TSI, fulfill the role of defining how the essential requirements will be met.

What is the status of TSIs?

TSIs are ‘Decisions’ under European Community law and have effect as if they were domestic regulations (secondary legislation). As such they are binding on Member States, and because the Railways (Interoperability) Regulations 2011 make specific reference to TSIs and apply them for domestic purposes, their requirements are binding on the rail industry in the United Kingdom.

How are TSIs developed?

TSIs are prepared by the European Railway Agency (‘ERA’) under a mandate from the European Commission. The mandate specifies the format of the TSI and the method that the ERA must use to prepare it.

Once a work programme is agreed, ERA establishes Drafting Group on which each National Safety Authority has a seat as well as experts from a number of relevant European Railway organisations. The Group must prepare a document that matches the standard framework and content of a TSI.

Once the Drafting Group has completed its task, ERA sends the final draft to the European Commission, for consideration, amendment, and acceptance by the Railway Interoperability and Safety Committee (‘RISC’). Once ratified by a vote at RISC, and following subsequent approval by the Council and Parliament of the European Union, the European Commission issues the TSI as a Decision which then becomes binding upon Member States.

This process normally takes around three years and the Rail Safety and Standards Board has published Guidance on TSI Development and an associated Checklist.

What do TSIs contain?

The structure and content of a TSI is prescribed in the Interoperability Directives and can be summarised as:

Chapter 2 - establishes its scope;

Chapter 3 - specifies the essential requirements for the relevant subsystem;

Chapter 4 - lists the basic parameters of the subsystem necessary to meet the essential requirements and its interfaces with other subsystems. It also specifies the functional and technical specifications which must be met and its interfaces to achieve specified performance;

Chapter 5 - determines the Interoperability Constituents and their interfaces necessary to achieve interoperability;

Chapter 6 - describes the procedures for assessing the conformity or suitability for use of Interoperability Constituents and for performing the EC Verification of the subsystem; and

Chapter 7 - describes the implementing provisions in certain specific cases, including recommendations regarding the time objectives for the transition from the existing to the final situation where there is full compliance with the TSIs (referred to as either an implementation plan or strategy). This chapter frequently contains the answers sought by Contracting Entities and Notified Bodies to difficult compliance questions.

What are ‘Specific Cases’?

Specific cases are provisions made in TSIs to deal with particular circumstances prevailing in a particular Member State. For example, Great Britain has specific cases in the Infrastructure and Rolling Stock TSIs in relation to the structure gauge because our infrastructure is different to that found in mainland Europe.

Specific cases for all Member States are included in Chapter 7 of each TSI.

How are errors in TSIs dealt with?

Errors in TSIs may be either ‘critical’ or ‘minor’ in scope.

An error in a TSI is critical if it:

  • prevents the completion of subsystem or interoperable constituent design
  • prevents the conformity assessment of the subsystem or the interoperable constituent
  • prevents the placing into service of the subsystem or the placing onto the market of the interoperable constituent
  • prevents the interoperability of the subsystem or the interoperable constituent
  • contributes to unsafe operation not foreseen as part of the certification of the subsystem and / or the interoperable constituent or
  • contributes directly to an accident or incident

The European Commission has put in place a formal and ‘rapid’ procedure for resolving critical errors. If you think that your difficulties arise from one of the scenarios described, please email us at interoperability@dft.gsi.gov.uk to explore whether this is the best route to pursue.

Where the error has arisen because a specification is impossible to apply in one Member State or in one specified (but generic) situation, then the European Commission can be petitioned for a Specific Case to be made and included in the TSI. This is likely to be the device that is used to deal with some of the critical errors.

Non-critical errors

Other errors are regarded as minor errors, and it is the Department’s responsibility to resolve non-critical errors once they are brought to its attention. As with critical errors, please email interoperability@dft.gsi.gov.uk to discuss the problem.

Minor errors are regarded as less urgent than critical errors and will be addressed in the routine review of the TSI.

The Rail Safety and Standards Board has established a log to capture information relating to problems which have been encountered during the application of TSIs in the United Kingdom at www.rssb.co.uk/EUROPE/Pages/TSI.aspx.
