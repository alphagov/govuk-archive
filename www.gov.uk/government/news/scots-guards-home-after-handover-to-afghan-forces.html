News Scots Guards home after handover to Afghan forces

Organisations:
  • Ministry
    of Defence
Published:
14 December 2012
Policy:
Establishing stability in Afghanistan
Country:
Afghanistan

Soldiers from the Scots Guards have returned home from Afghanistan after handing security in their area of Helmand over to Afghan forces.

Over 50 Guardsmen from the Right Flank of the 1st Battalion Scots Guards have returned to their barracks in Catterick, North Yorkshire, as the checkpoints they manned have been closed and the Afghan National Civil Order Police (ANCOP) are now taking responsibility for security in Helmand’s Upper Gereshk Valley.

The Scots Guards are serving with Task Force Helmand, currently led by the British Army’s 4th Mechanized Brigade, known as ‘The Black Rats’, and the Right Flank form part of the first 500 British troops to be sent home from Afghanistan as announced by the Prime Minister last year.

The troops were based at Checkpoints ‘Barcha’ and ‘Pan Kalay’ and Observation Post ‘Tegha’ in the Upper Gereshk Valley - the northernmost area of operations for Task Force Helmand.

The Guardsmen have been working alongside the ANCOP to bring security and allow freedom of movement for traffic on Route 611, the main road between Sangin and Gereshk, but that job has been successfully handed over to the police.

Lieutenant Sayed Farid, the new Afghan Commander from the ANCOP’s 5th Brigade taking over from the Scots Guards in Pan Kalay, said:

I believe all the Afghan forces are now well-equipped, well-armed and ready to take on the security of Afghanistan.

Since we have been here we have some really good relations with the ISAF forces. They have really helped us out. We are only sad because our friends are leaving. They did their part of the job and we did ours.

Platoon Commander Second Lieutenant James Westlake, attached to the Right Flank from the Honourable Artillery Company, said:

It was quite apparent to everyone in the platoon, not just higher up, that the job is done. The time is right to be going back and the Afghan forces in those locations are ready to take over - there is just not a job for us to do by staying there.

So everybody is satisfied that it is a job well done and, once you take that into account, everybody is looking forward to getting back.

The checkpoints and observation post are the first on the main 611 highway in Task Force Helmand’s area of operations to be closed. During the closure process, the facilities were removed and the bases reduced to a more basic level for the few troops that remained until the final day.

Follwing some leave, the Scots Guards will be responsible for providing support to the battle groups deploying to Afghanistan in April 2013, when the Guardsmen will be reunited with the rest of the battalion.

The Prime Minister announced last year that 500 British personnel would be withdrawn from Afghanistan by the end of 2012, reflecting the continuing progress in Helmand. 

The drawdown is consistent with the process of transition to Afghan forces agreed by ISAF nations at Lisbon in 2010.

The Prime Minister has emphasised the UK’s commitment to Afghanistan for the long term, pledging £70 million per annum in funding for the Afghan security forces, in addition to the taking the lead in setting up an Afghan National Army Officers’ Academy in Kabul after the combat mission ends in 2014.
