Policy Leading international efforts to resolve concerns about Iran's nuclear programme

Foreign affairs, Defence and armed forces and National security
Ministers:
Alistair Burt MP and The Rt Hon William Hague MP
Country:
Iran

Issue

Despite Iran’s claim that its nuclear programme is peaceful, Britain shares international concerns that the programme may also have a military purpose.

Iran is making a disproportionate investment in the parts of the nuclear fuel cycle that could be used for a weapons programme. We are particularly concerned about the enrichment of uranium to 20% without a credible civilian use for it. The concealment of elements of Iran’s nuclear programme - eg hiding a nuclear facility at Qom until 2009 - is another concern.

Iran is also in defiance of 6 UN Security Council resolutions. It hasn’t cooperated fully with the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) by providing them access for inspections. It also signed but then failed to implement an Additional Protocol, which would have given the IAEA authority to properly assess its nuclear activities. The IAEA says that this lack of cooperation means it can’t confirm that all Iran’s nuclear material is for peaceful activities.

The UK, along with much of the international community, is deeply concerned about the consequences for regional security if Iran develops nuclear weapons.

Actions

The international community is seeking a peaceful, negotiated solution and is applying pressure on Iran through:

  • diplomatic engagement - the UK, US, France, Germany, China and Russia have held regular talks with Iran since 2012 and these are ongoing
  • economic incentives - if Iran demonstrates that it isn’t developing a nuclear weapon then the permanent members of the UN Security Council will help it develop more nuclear power stations and give it economic, agricultural and humanitarian assistance
  • economic pressure - the UK, the EU and the UN have enforced sanctions targeting Iran’s nuclear programme and its banking, oil and gas sectors

Background

Diplomatic relations between the UK and Iran have been cut off and restored several times since the 1979 Revolution. On 29 November 2011, student Basij militia entered the British embassy in Tehran and a residential compound in the north of the city, causing extensive damage. The Iranian authorities made no credible effort to intervene. The embassy is now closed and all British diplomats have been withdrawn. All Iranian diplomats were ordered to leave the UK on 2 December 2011.

Diplomatic relations haven’t been completely severed, but they are at a minimal level. Sweden now represents British interests in Iran through their embassy in Tehran, while Iran’s interests in the UK are currently represented by the Sultanate of Oman through its embassy in London.

Iran has backed Hizbollah in Lebanon, as well as Hamas and other Palestinian Rejectionist groups. It supports anti-government militia groups in Iraq and the Taliban in Afghanistan. All this undermines international efforts to bring about stability and security in these countries, and has caused serious tension between Iran and its neighbours.
