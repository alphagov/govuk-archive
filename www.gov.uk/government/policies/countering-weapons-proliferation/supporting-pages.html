Counter-Proliferation Programme

Countering weapons proliferation is central to the government’s efforts to safeguard Britain’s national security and to support stability around the world.

The FCO plays an important role in the UK’s work to stop the spread of chemical, biological, radiological and nuclear (CBRN) weapons. States that acquire CBRN weapons can threaten global stability and undermine international institutions. In the hands of terrorists, they could pose a grave and direct threat to civilians. We also support international efforts to tackle the illicit trade in conventional weapons, which contributes to regional conflict and instability, with severe humanitarian consequences.

One way the FCO combats these threats is the Counter-Proliferation Programme which supports projects around the world to enforce political will or technical capacity to reduce the threat of weapons proliferation.

Programme objectives

In the financial year 2012 - 13, the value of the Counter-Proliferation Programme is £3 million. All projects funded by the programme contribute to achieving one or more of the programme’s main objectives.

Objective 1

Countries strengthen their capacity to secure CBRN materials and expertise from acquisition by non-state actors.

Objective 2

Countries strengthen their export control regimes.

Objective 3

The international regimes, treaties and initiatives that underpin global non-proliferation, security and disarmament objectives are strengthened.

Objective 4

Support for the Non-Proliferation Treaty is reinforced ahead of the next Review Conference in 2015.

Objective 5

Progress is made towards the establishment of a WMD-free zone in the Middle East.

Objective 6

The threat posed by conventional weapons to UK, regional and global stability is reduced.

Bidding for funding

Funding is allocated to projects in a competitive bidding process, which is normally run twice per year, with a main bidding round in March, and a smaller bidding round in September. All bids are considered by a cross-government programme board that decides which projects to fund. Successful bids in the past have come from a range of academic institutions, NGOs, think tanks and other organisations with relevant contacts or expertise.

Successful bids will make clear what results the project would have in support of the programme’s goals, and will complement rather than duplicate the government’s considerable conventional diplomatic involvement on these issues.
