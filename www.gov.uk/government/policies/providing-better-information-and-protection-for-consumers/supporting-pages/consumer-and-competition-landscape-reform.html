Consumer and competition landscape reform

Consumer landscape reform

The ‘consumer landscape’ is the network of organisations in the UK that provides advice and help to consumers and enforces consumer law.

The network includes:

  • Consumer Focus
  • Citizens’ Advice service
  • Office of Fair Trading (OFT)
  • ‘Scambuster’ teams
  • Illegal Money Lending teams
  • Trading Standards offices

Some of these organisations also represent consumers or work on their behalf.

Following recent consultation, we intend to reorganise the consumer landscape to make it simpler and more efficient. This will mean replacing some organisations with others, some organisations taking on more responsibility and others giving up some responsibility.

We believe this reorganisation will make it easier for people to get the information and support they need. It should also mean traders who break the law will be prosecuted more quickly and effectively.

When will you see these changes?

We’ve already started work on some of them:

  • the Citizens Advice service has launched Adviceguide - a replacement for the OFT’s Consumer Direct helpline in April 2012
  • we’ve set up the Strategic Intelligence, Prevention and Enforcement Partnership (the National Trading Standards Board (NTSB), the Citizens Advice service, a new organisation called the Competition and Markets Authority (CMA), a new Regulated Industries Unit (RIU) and representatives from Scotland and Northern Ireland) to make sure there is a ‘joined-up’ approach to consumer issues and deal with the greatest problems for consumers
  • we’ve set up the National Trading Standards Board - now responsible for national and cross-local authority enforcement in England and Wales
  • the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities (COSLA) has set up a Scottish Consumer Protection National Enforcement Team (SCPNET), to be in charge of enforcement in Scotland

In 2013:

  • we’ll transfer the national co-ordination of consumer advice, information and education about consumers’ rights from the OFT to Citizens Advice
  • the Trading Standards Institute (TSI) will take over responsibility for educating business about consumers’ rights
  • we’ll make the Citizens Advice service responsible for consumer advocacy
  • Consumer Focus’ activities on regulated industries will move to a Regulated Industries Unit (RIU), which will remain part of Consumer Focus until 2014
  • the TSI will launch a replacement for the OFT’s Consumer Code Approval Scheme

By the end of 2014:

  • the OFT and Competition Commission will close
  • Consumer Focus’ RIU will transfer to the Citizens Advice service

  • the Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) will take over some of the OFT’s consumer responsibilities, which will include the powers to deal with anti-competitive business activities, for example:

    • tricking people into contracts that could stop them changing suppliers
    • imposing excessive surcharges to change suppliers
    • claiming other suppliers charge more than they actually do

The CMA will generally use its powers for improvements across a whole market. The CMA will usually investigate individual firms only if there’s likely to be a widespread benefit from that investigation.

Competition landscape reform

Following consultation, we have also decided to reform some of the institutions that deal with competition and anti-competitive activities in business.

These institutions are collectively known as the ‘competition landscape’. They include OFT, the Competition Commission (CC) and the Competition Appeal Tribunal (CAT).

We’re making these reforms as we feel the current system is slow-moving and inefficient. There’s too much overlap between institutions and it’s not always easy to see who’s responsible for what.

We hope to:

  • make decision-making quicker and more effective
  • help competition authorities make best use of their resources to deal with the most uncompetitive practices
  • improve speed and predictability for business

To improve the system for consumers as well as business, we intend to transfer the functions of the Competition Commission (CC) and the competition functions of the OFT to the new CMA in 2014. The CMA will regulate competition. It will have the power to:

  • investigate competition issues across the market
  • investigate public interest issues

As a single body, the CMA should make better use of public money. Additionally, businesses should find it easier to deal with just one agency.
