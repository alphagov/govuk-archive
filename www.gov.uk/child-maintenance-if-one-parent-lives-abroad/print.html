Child maintenance if one parent lives abroad, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: How to get help

If you need to get child maintenance from an ex-partner who lives abroad, the Child Support Agency (CSA) or other organisations might be able to help you depending on your circumstances.

The parent the child doesn’t live with is called the ‘paying parent’. The parent or person the child lives with is called the ‘receiving parent’.

When the CSA can help

If the paying parent lives abroad, the CSA can only help if they are:

  • a civil servant
  • working in Her Majesty’s Diplomatic Service
  • a member of the Armed Forces
  • working for a company that is based and registered in the UK
  • working on secondment for a ‘prescribed body’, like a regional health authority or local authority

Contact the CSA for more information on how it can help.

If you live in the UK and the paying parent lives in Australia, the Australian CSA might be able to help.

If one of you lives in the European Union (EU)

By law, EU countries must enforce:

  • court orders for child maintenance
  • decisions made by the CSA

Any of the following must apply:

  • one of you lives in the EU and the other lives in the UK
  • the paying parent has valuable assets located in the EU - eg property, cars or money

EU countries can also make paying parents pay CSA debts if the money owed was from when both parents lived in the UK.

If the paying parent lives outside of Australia and the EU

If you have a court order for child maintenance you can try to get this enforced in a foreign country. You can also ask foreign authorities to create a court order for you.

To do this, contact your local magistrates’ court (sheriff court in Scotland) using the links below:

  • Find a court - England and Wales
  • Find a court - Scotland

Part 2: Reciprocal Enforcement of Maintenance Orders (REMOs)

These are international agreements about child maintenance. The UK has them with over 100 countries.

If a REMO is in place, it means that:

  • child maintenance orders made by UK courts can be registered and enforced in other countries
  • child maintenance orders made in foreign countries can be registered and enforced by UK courts

If you want to try and put a REMO in place, you need to do one of the following things:

  • contact the court where the order was made if you already have a court order for child maintenance
  • contact your local magistrates’ court (sheriff court in Scotland) if you haven’t got a court order

What happens next depends on where you live.

If you live in England or Wales

The court will send your case to the REMO unit, which deals with the foreign court on your behalf. You don’t need a solicitor.

You can find out more about REMOs on the Official Solicitor website.

If you live in Scotland

If a court order already exists, the court that made it will send your case to the Justice Directorate.

If a court order doesn’t exist, you can get one by either:

  • applying to your local sheriff court
  • applying directly to the court in the country the other parent has moved to

You’ll need to use a solicitor. Some of the REMO arrangements other countries have with the UK don’t apply to Scotland.

If you live in Scotland you can find out more about REMOs on the Scottish government website.
