Introduction

Certain drugs are controlled due to their potential to cause harm if misused. The purpose of drugs licensing controls is to enable the legal uses of these substances, while preventing their misuse and diversion into illegal trade.

Precursor chemicals are substances that, whilst used legitimately in industry, may also be used in the illicit manufacture of controlled drugs.

The Home Office Drugs Licensing and Compliance Unit (DLCU) is responsible for regulating licensed users of controlled drugs and precursor chemicals in the UK. It operates a licensing regime for the manufacture, production, supply, possession, and import and export of controlled drugs. Those who wish to place precursor chemicals on the market or engage in their import and export must be licensed by the Home Office.

The National Drugs Control System (NDS) is used to administer the import and export licensing regime of the UK.

Controlled drugs licensing

Certain substances are controlled under the Misuse of Drugs Act 1971 due to the harm they can cause if misused. These are known as ‘controlled drugs’. It is illegal to manufacture, produce, possess, supply, import or export controlled drugs unless any of the following apply:

  • you are specifically exempted by the 1971 Act
  • you are authorised by The Misuse of Drugs Regulations 2001
  • you possess a Home Office licence to do so

Certain exceptions apply to some domestic licence requirements for controlled drugs for certain organisations, including:

  • Private hospitals and care homes - only require licences for Schedule 1 and 2 drugs.
  • University research departments - do not require licences to possess and supply drugs in Schedules 2, 3, 4 - Part I and Part II - and Schedule 5, but they do require licences to produce any of those drugs and to possess and/or supply drugs in Schedule 1. They also require licences to produce any of the drugs in Schedules 2 to 5

Controlled drugs are grouped into five schedules in the Misuse of Drugs Regulations 2001. A schedule is a group of drugs with common properties - for example, schedule 1 drugs are those with no recognised medical use. Some record-keeping and safe-custody requirements only apply to certain schedules of drugs. Licences are issued per schedule of controlled drugs.

This list is not exhaustive. If you are unsure whether a substance is controlled, you should contact the DLCU for advice. You can contact them by email at licensing_enquiry.aadu@homeoffice.gsi.gov.uk.

Who needs a domestic licence for controlled drugs?

Anyone wishing to manufacture, produce, possess, supply, import or export controlled drugs may need a licence from the Home Office to do so. Common users of controlled drug licences are:

  • businesses, educational institutions, and other organisations that intend to manufacture, produce, possess or supply controlled drugs within the UK
  • doctors prescribing certain drugs to addicts for treatment
  • people taking their prescribed controlled drugs abroad
  • businesses wishing to import or export controlled drugs across the UK border

Applying for a controlled drug domestic licence

Any business or individual wishing to produce, supply or possess controlled drugs may need a Home Office domestic licence.

Applying for a domestic licence is now done online. Before submitting your application, you must:

  • read the guidance regarding security
  • read the guidance regarding standard operating procedures (SOPs)
  • apply for a Criminal Records Bureau (CRB) enhanced disclosure check

Import and export licences

A licence is needed for each shipment of controlled drugs across the UK border for all controlled drugs, other than those that fall under Schedule 5 of The Misuse of Drugs Regulations 2001. Only establishments in possession of the appropriate controlled drug domestic licence and those with exemptions are able to apply for import or export licences.

The CRB enhanced disclosure checks for controlled drug domestic licence applicants

The Rehabilitation of Offenders Act 1974 (Exceptions) Order 1975 enables the DLCU to ask questions about spent convictions for the purpose of assessing an applicant’s suitability to obtain a licence.

DLCU has contracted Capita Recruitment Vetting Service (CRVS) to provide a scheme to enable DLCU licence applicants to obtain CRB enhanced disclosure checks

The DLCU does not process CRB applications. For any queries regarding the CRB application process, you should contact the CRVS helpline on 0870 850 2455.

When the DLCU has received your online application

Once the DLCU has received your online application, it will be assessed to ensure that the security and record-keeping requirements have been met. In most cases this will require a site visit by a compliance officer.

The contents of the CRB enhanced disclosures for all applicants named on the application form will also be taken into account in the decision as to whether or not to issue a licence.

You should allow a minimum of 12 weeks for your application to be processed.

If you have any questions regarding your application for a controlled drug domestic licence, you can contact the DLCU by email at licensing_enquiry.aadu@homeoffice.gsi.gov.uk.

Exceptions to domestic drugs licensing requirements

Certain exceptions apply to some domestic licence requirements for controlled drugs for certain organisations, including:

  • Private hospitals and care homes: only require licences for Schedule 1 and 2 drugs.
  • University research departments: do not require licences to possess and supply drugs in Schedules 2, 3, 4 - Part I and Part II - and Schedule 5, but they do require licences to produce any of those drugs and to possess and/or supply drugs in Schedule 1. They also require licences to produce any of the drugs in Schedules 2 to 5.

Applying for a controlled drug import or export licence

If you intend to import or export a controlled drug, you will need the relevant domestic licence, or an exemption under either the Misuse of Drugs Act 1971 or the Misuse of Drugs Regulations 2001, before you can apply for an import or export licence.

Apply for an import/export licence for controlled drugs

All applications to export controlled drugs must be accompanied by an import certificate issued by the competent authority of the importing country.

Precursor chemicals licensing

‘Precursor chemicals’ are chemicals frequently used in the illegal manufacture of controlled drugs. The Home Office regulates the trade in precursor chemicals in order to enable their legal use and prevent their diversion into the illicit market.

You can find precursor chemicals under global control by the International Narcotics Control Board (INCB)

Precursor chemicals are divided into three categories - categories 1, 2, and 3 - according to the level of risk that they pose. The regulatory requirements vary according to the category into which the precursor chemical falls, with the most rigorous controls focused on category 1 chemicals, which pose the highest risk.

Who needs a licence or registration for precursor chemicals?

Anyone wishing to possess or manufacture category 1 precursor chemicals, or place them on the market, requires a Home Office licence. Individual authorisations are required for each import and export of category 1 precursor chemicals.

Anyone wishing to manufacture category 2 precursor chemicals, or place them on the market, requires a Home Office registration. Individual authorisations are required to export them beyond the EU border to specified countries. All exports of Acetic Anyhride and Potassium Permanganate beyond the EU border require an export authorisation regardless of their destination.

Anyone manufacturing or trading category 3 precursor chemicals over and above levels set out in EU regulations requires a Home Office registration. Individual authorisations are required to export them beyond the EU border to specified countries.

Applying for a precursor chemical category 1 licence or category 2 or 3 registration

Applicants for precursor chemical category 1 licences and category 2 and 3 registrations must:

  • complete an application form
  • apply for a CRB enhanced disclosure check

Applications for precursor chemical category 1 licences and category 2 and 3 registrations should be made using the appropriate forms. They cannot be made online.

CRB enhanced disclosure checks for applicants

All applications for a category 1 licence or category 2 or 3 registration must include a CRB enhanced disclosure for each individual named on the application form.

Existing licensees altering the terms of their licence will also need to provide a CRB enhanced disclosure as part of the alteration application.

The DLCU has contracted Capita Recruitment Vetting Service (CRVS) to provide a scheme to enable DLCU licence applicants to obtain CRB enhanced disclosures. You can find guidance on CRB disclosures on the CRVS website.

The DLCU do not process CRB applications. Any queries regarding the CRB application process should be directed to the CRVS. To apply for a CRB enhanced disclosure, contact the CRVS Helpline on 0870 850 2455.

When the DLCU has received your online application

Once the DLCU has received your application, it will be assessed to ensure that all legal requirements have been met. This assessment may include a site visit by a compliance officer.

The contents of the CRB enhanced disclosures for all applicants named on the application form will also be taken into account in the decision as to whether or not to issue a licence.

You should allow a minimum of 12 weeks for your application to be processed.

End-user declarations

When you supply category 1 and 2 precursor chemicals, you must obtain an end-user declaration form from your customer. End-user declaration forms show the specific uses of the precursor chemicals that your customers have purchased.

If your business wishes to purchase precursor chemicals, you will also need to complete an end-user declaration form for your supplier.

Annual statistical returns and licensee compliance statements

All companies that produce, supply, import, export and destroy controlled drugs and precursor chemicals must complete an annual statistical return by 31 January each year.

Any companies that only produce, supply and possess controlled drugs should complete a licensee compliance statement.

Annual statistical return

Your annual statistical return provides the Home Office DLCU with information about the quantities of controlled drugs and precursor chemicals that your business has:

  • manufactured
  • held as stock
  • imported
  • exported
  • lost during manufacture

You must complete and submit your annual statistical return to the DLCU by 31 January each year.

You can submit your annual statistical return online through the Home Office online drug licensing and authorisation service

Licensee compliance statement

Your licensee compliance statement tells the DLCU that your business activity has conformed to the conditions of your controlled drugs licence(s). It also provides a record of any incidents that have occurred, and confirms that these have been correctly reported to the Home Office.

Your licensee compliance statement must be signed by a senior company representative who is responsible for regulatory affairs.

Go to the DLCU licensee compliance statement form

It must also be submitted to the DLCU by email at DLCUstatements@homeoffice.gsi.gov.uk by 31 January each year.

How will fees be paid?

Fees for all licences must be paid at the time of application, except for controlled drug and precursor chemical import/export applications, where charges will be accrued and invoiced on a quarterly basis.

Where do I send my fee?

You should not send your fees for licence applications to the Home Office Drugs Licensing and Compliance Unit (DLCU). When the DLCU receives your application, it will issue an invoice that will contain full details of how to make a payment.

You will be able to pay by one of the following methods:

  • Bankers Automated Clearing Service (BACS)
  • Clearing House Automated Payment System (CHAPS)
  • cheque
  • credit or debit card

What happens if my licence application is refused or withdrawn?

If an application for a new controlled drug or precursor chemical domestic licence, or to renew an existing one, is refused or withdrawn, a full refund of the application fee will be made.

If an application for an import/export controlled drug or licence or precursor chemical authorisation is refused or withdrawn, no charge will be included in any quarterly invoice.

Contacting the Home Office DLCU

There are a number of ways to contact the DLCU, depending on your query.

If you are applying for a domestic licence, you can call the DLCU Controlled Drugs (Domestic Licensing) helpline on 020 7035 0445 or email them at licensing_enquiry.aadu@homeoffice.gsi.gov.uk

For information on import and export licences, you can call the DLCU Controlled Drugs (Import/Export Licensing) helpline on 020 7035 0771 or email them at licensing_enquiry.aadu@homeoffice.gsi.gov.uk

If you are applying for a precursor licence, you can contact the DLCU Precursor Chemicals Licensing helpline on 020 7035 0792 or email them at PrecursorGeneralEnquiries@homeoffice.gsi.gov.uk

For queries or information on your annual returns, you can call the DLCU Annual Statistical Returns (Controlled Drugs) helpline on 020 7035 0467 or the DLCU Annual Statistical Returns (Precursor Chemicals) helpline on 020 7035 0484.
