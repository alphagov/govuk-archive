Immigration and asylum tribunal , a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

You can appeal to the immigration and asylum tribunal if you’ve been refused permission to enter or stay in the UK.

What cases the tribunal deals with

The tribunal can deal with a range of cases, eg decisions about:

  • asylum
  • entry clearance
  • leave to remain

You can also appeal against a decision to deport someone from the UK.

You may want to get legal help or advice before you appeal.

Time-limits for appealing

You must send your appeal within:

Where you’re appealing from Time to appeal after you get the decision
In detention 5 working days
Not in detention 10 working days
Outside the UK 28 days

If you miss the deadline, your appeal may not be accepted, so explain the reason for the delay.

Appeal to the tribunal

You can appeal online, or fill in an appeal form.

Part 2: Tribunal fees

You usually have to pay a fee to appeal to the tribunal.

How you want appeal decided Fee per person
Using paperwork £80
At a hearing £140

Your legal adviser should tell you if you don’t have to pay for your type of case.

Help with fees

You may not have to pay a fee if you’re getting:

  • asylum support
  • legal aid
  • services from your local council and you’re under 18

You may be able to get legal aid to pay for your appeal.

Part 3: How your appeal is decided

If you haven’t asked for a hearing

If you haven’t asked for a hearing, a judge will make a decision based on the documents in the case.

You will be sent a copy of the decision in the post.

If you’ve asked for a hearing

If you asked for your case to be decided at a hearing, you’ll get a letter with details of where to go.

The hearing will be attended by:

  • a judge or judges, sometimes with other tribunal members
  • your representative, if you have one
  • a Home Office ‘presenting officer’
  • any witnesses called to give evidence
  • an interpreter, if you’ve asked for one

You may have to give evidence at the hearing and answer questions.

The hearing will be held without you if you live outside the UK, but you can ask someone to represent you.

The tribunal’s decision

The tribunal will either decide to:

  • allow your appeal - and let you enter or stay in the country
  • dismiss your appeal - and uphold the Home Office’s original decision

You will usually get a copy of the tribunal’s decision within 10 working days of the hearing.

If you win your appeal

The judge may order the Home Office to pay you an award. This will be up to the amount you paid for the tribunal fee.

Part 4: If you lose your case

If you lose your case, you can ask for permission to appeal against the decision.

To do this, fill in the permission to appeal form.

Say why you think the decision was legally wrong or unreasonable.

Time-limits for appealing

You must send your appeal within:

Type of appeal Time to appeal after you get the decision
Fast-track case 2 working days
If you’re inside the UK 5 working days
If you’re outside the UK 28 days

Send the form with a copy of the decision to the address or fax number below:

After you send the form

A judge will decide if you can take your case to a higher tribunal, called the Upper Tribunal (Immigration and Asylum Chamber).

Find out more about appealing to the upper tribunal.
