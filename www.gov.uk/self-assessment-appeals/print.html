Appeal against Self Assessment penalties and tax bills, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Appeal against a penalty

You may be charged a penalty if, for example, you’ve missed the tax return or payment deadline.

You can appeal against the penalty if you’ve got a ‘reasonable excuse’ for being late.

What may be a reasonable excuse

A reasonable excuse is something unexpected or unusual and outside your control that prevented you from sending your tax return or payment on time, like:

  • your partner died shortly before the tax return or payment deadline
  • you had a serious or life-threatening illness (eg a heart attack) that prevented you from dealing with your tax affairs
  • you lost important documents in a fire that couldn’t be replaced in time to complete your tax return
  • postal delays that you couldn’t have predicted
  • you registered for the HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) online service before the tax return deadline but didn’t get your activation code or user ID in time

What’s unlikely to be a reasonable excuse

The following aren’t usually accepted as a reasonable excuse:

  • you relied on someone else to send your return and they didn’t
  • your cheque bounced or payment failed because you didn’t have enough money
  • you didn’t get a reminder from HMRC
  • you registered for the HMRC online service after the tax return deadline

How to appeal

You can fill in an appeal form explaining the problem as soon as you know your return or payment are late.

If HMRC sends you a penalty letter, you can use the appeal form that comes with it.

Only the nominated partner can appeal against penalties for sending partnership tax returns late.

Deadlines

You must appeal within 30 days of the date on a penalty letter from HMRC.

If you appeal later, HMRC will look at the information you give them and decide if they will consider your appeal.

Part 2: Appeal against a tax bill

If your tax bill is wrong

If you think your tax bill is wrong because you made a mistake on your tax return you don’t need to appeal - you may be able to correct it. You’ll get a new tax bill if you correct your return.

If you’ve been sent a tax bill because you didn’t send a tax return

If HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) has estimated the bill because you didn’t send a tax return, you can’t appeal against it. You must send your tax return. HMRC will send you a new tax bill when you’ve sent your tax return.

If HMRC has amended your tax bill

If HMRC have changed the amount you owe after a check on your return and you disagree with it, you can appeal against it.

You have 30 days from the date of HMRC’s letter to you (usually a tax assessment or penalty notice) to appeal.

If you appeal after 30 days, you must explain why. HMRC will look at the information you give them to decide if they can accept your late appeal.

How to appeal

You can:

  • use the appeal form if one came with the letter HMRC sent you
  • appeal in writing to the office that sent the letter

Appeal by letter

If you appeal by letter, you must include:

  • your name
  • your 10-digit unique taxpayer reference number
  • the amount of the tax you’re appealing against
  • the reasons why you’re appealing

You must sign the letter.

Part 3: Delaying payment of a penalty or tax bill if you're appealing

You may decide to delay paying a penalty or tax bill if you disagree with the amount you have to pay.

If HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) doesn’t agree with your appeal, you may have to pay interest on the amount you pay late.

To delay paying a penalty

Once you’ve appealed, HMRC won’t ask you to pay the penalty until the appeal has been settled.

To delay paying tax due

When you appeal, write to the HMRC office that sent you the letter about your tax bill or penalty, telling them:

  • why you think the amount you’ve been asked to pay is too much
  • what you think the correct amount is and when you’ll pay it

HMRC will tell you in writing if they agree.

Part 4: After your appeal

HMRC will look at your appeal and tell you their decision in writing - along with how much you must pay or be repaid.

If HMRC agrees with your appeal, you won’t have to pay interest on any tax and penalties paid late.

If they don’t agree with your appeal, they will tell you why and ask if you accept their decision. If you do, you should pay the amount due, including interest.

If you disagree with the appeal decision, you can either:

  • ask HMRC to review their decision - their letter will tell you how to do this
  • appeal to the independent tax tribunal
