Make child maintenance deductions from an employee's pay, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

The Child Support Agency (CSA) can ask you to:

  • give information about your employee so they can work out the right amount of child maintenance
  • set up a deduction from earnings order (DEO)
  • answer enquiries from organisations used by the CSA to collect payments of child maintenance on their behalf

Your legal obligations when deducting child maintenance

By law, you must:

  • give information to the CSA when requested
  • send payments to the CSA as soon as possible, but no later than the 19th day of the month following the month you took it
  • let the CSA know immediately if there are any problems with taking payments from a paying parent’s earnings
  • make regular payments - if you don’t send payments to the CSA and don’t tell them why, they can take you to court
  • tell the CSA in writing within 10 days of an employee leaving your business
  • tell the CSA in writing within 10 days if they ask you to set up a deduction from earnings order (DEO) for someone who doesn’t work for you

The ‘paying parent’ is the parent the child doesn’t live with.

Changes to statutory child maintenance services

A new child maintenance scheme (‘the 2012 scheme’) has been introduced. It’s managed by the Child Maintenance Service and will run alongside the CSA schemes.

This will change the way DEOs work. The changes probably won’t affect you immediately and you don’t need to take any action until the Child Maintenance Service asks you to.

Email the Child Maintenance Service if you have any questions about the upcoming changes.

Part 2: Deduction from earnings order (DEO)

This is a way of collecting child maintenance directly from a paying parent’s earnings or pension.

The ‘paying parent’ is the parent the child doesn’t live with.

The Child Support Agency (CSA), or an authorised debt collection agency, will send you a deduction from earnings order (DEO) if one of your employees is a paying parent who:

  • chooses to pay child maintenance direct from their earnings
  • doesn’t pay the correct amount of child maintenance on time
  • doesn’t pay at all

You must deduct the amount of child maintenance stated on the DEO from your employee’s net earnings or their pension and pay it to the CSA. This will include any arrears that may be due.

If you get more than one DEO for an employee

You could get more than one DEO if your employee is paying maintenance for children by different ‘receiving parents’. You need to make a payment for each DEO.

The ‘receiving parent’ is the parent or carer the child lives with.

Other court orders against your employee

You could get a DEO from the CSA and an attachment of earnings order (AEO) from a court.

AEOs work in a similar way to DEOs, but there are rules about which money you should take first.

You can download a leaflet that tells you more about AEOs.

Part 3: How deduction from earnings orders work

The deduction from earnings order (DEO) will give you information you need to make deductions, including:

  • the normal deduction rate (NDR) - the amount of child maintenance the Child Support Agency (CSA) takes from your employee’s net earnings in each pay period

  • the protected earnings rate (PER) or protected earnings proportion (PEP) - the amount your employee needs to keep to pay for their own living costs

  • the date the CSA expects you to start making deductions

  • how often you should make payments

The PER applies to cases opened before 3 March 2003. The PEP applies to cases opened from 3 March 2003.

Example - deducting the full NDR:

Your employee’s net earnings are £160 a week.

The NDR is £30 a week and the PER is £100 a week.

Net earnings minus the PER of £100 = £60, so you’d send the CSA the full NDR of £30.

You can deduct up to £1 for administration costs.

If you can’t make a full deduction

You must:

  • keep a record of the shortfall (the difference between the net earnings and the PER or PEP)
  • carry forward the shortfall to the next period
  • send the deduction, if there is one, to the CSA

You then deduct the full amount of the shortfall plus the NDR the next pay period. You must still leave your employee with the PER or PEP.

If the shortfall is carried forward for several weeks before being repaid, keep a record of the ongoing shortfall.

Example - where you can’t take the full NDR:

Your employee’s net earnings are £120 a week.

The NDR is £32 a week and the PER is £96 a week.

Net earnings minus the PER of £96 = £24, so you’d send the CSA £24. The shortfall of £8 is carried forward to the next pay period.

You can deduct up to £1 for administration costs.

When you make deductions, you must make sure that you leave your employee with the amount of their PER or PEP - unless the deduction is for administrative costs.

If you pay your employee holiday pay in advance

You’ll need to multiply the NDR and the PER or PEP in line with the net earnings.

This means if you double the net earnings because you’re paying 2 weeks instead of one, you’ll need to double the NDR and the PEP or PER.

Example - including holiday pay in advance:

In this example, the earnings include 2 weeks’ holiday pay paid in advance.

Your employee has net earnings of £160 a week.

The NDR is £32 a week and the PEP is £96 a week.

Net earnings are £160 x 3 = £480 (1 week’s pay + 2 weeks’ holiday pay).

Take away the PEP of £96 x 3 (£288). This leaves £192, so you’d send the CSA the full NDR of £96 (£32 x 3).

You can deduct up to £1 for administration costs.

If you get more than one DEO for an employee

Sometimes a paying parent has more than one case and is paying child maintenance for children by different receiving parents.

The number of DEOs depends on when the cases were opened:

  • for cases opened after 3 March 2003 - there will only ever be one DEO (you make one payment to the CSA and it gets split later)
  • for cases opened before 3 March 2003 - you might get more than one DEO and need to make a payment for each

The ‘paying parent’ is the parent the child doesn’t live with. The ‘receiving parent’ is the parent the child lives with.

Part 4: What the Child Support Agency counts as earnings

A deduction from earnings order (DEO) can only be made from the following earnings:

  • wages, fees, bonus, commission, overtime pay or any payments on top of wages
  • private or occupational pensions and compensation payments
  • Statutory Sick Pay
  • contractual sick pay
  • contractual maternity pay
  • contractual paternity pay
  • contractual adoption pay
  • contractual redundancy pay

Statutory pay is money that your employees are entitled to by law. Contractual pay is pay that you agree with your employees over and above statutory pay.

What doesn’t count as earnings for DEOs

Your employee can’t pay child maintenance by DEO if their only earnings are in the following categories:

  • amounts paid by a public department of the Government of Northern Ireland or any country outside the UK
  • any social security pension, allowance or benefit
  • tax credits
  • any pension or allowance paid for disability
  • guaranteed minimum pension within the Social Security Pensions Act 1975
  • Statutory Maternity Pay
  • Statutory Paternity Pay
  • Statutory Adoption Pay
  • Statutory Redundancy Pay

Part 5: Making payments to the Child Support Agency

You can make payments to the Child Support Agency (CSA) by using the Banks Automated Clearing System (BACS).

The CSA can help you set up a BACS payment.

You should send a BACS payment for each employee you make a deduction from.

You can also pay by other methods:

  • internet banking
  • telephone banking
  • cheque

When to pay the CSA

You should send the payment to get to the CSA no later than the 19th day of the month following the month that you took it.

Example
If you take the money on:

30 September - the CSA needs it by 19 October

1 October - the CSA needs it by 19 November

You must set up a deduction from earnings order (DEO) and pay the amount on the order by law or you can be prosecuted.

The CSA will send you and your employee a letter every year to remind you how much must be paid and when.

Deductions for administrative costs

You can take up to £1 towards administrative costs for each deduction, on top of the amount of the DEO. You can take it even if it reduces your employee’s income below the protected earnings proportion or protected earnings rate.

Informing your employee

You must inform your employee in writing about each deduction when they are given their pay statement. Include the amount (if any) you deduct towards your expenses.

If pay statements are not usually given, you should inform your employee in writing. You should do this no later than the employee’s pay day after the deduction was made.

If you pay the wrong amount

If you find a mistake in the amount you’ve paid, contact the CSA.

Part 6: Change of circumstances

If your employee’s circumstances change, phone the Child Support Agency (CSA) employer helpline.

Child Support Agency employer helpline
Telephone: 0845 713 6010
Textphone: 0845 713 8924

If your business stops trading, or if your employee leaves your employment, you must tell the CSA in writing within 10 days.

You should also tell the CSA if your employee changes their hours.

The CSA will send you and your employee a revised deduction from earnings order (DEO).

If the CSA cancels a DEO

The CSA will write to you and your employee to tell you the DEO has been cancelled. It will ask you to stop taking deductions from the date of the letter.

You must only stop taking deductions if the CSA tells you in writing.

Part 7: If you don't help the CSA

It’s an offence to:

  • not provide information when it’s required
  • make a false statement or representation
  • knowingly provide false information
  • delay or obstruct an inspector on purpose
  • refuse or fail to answer any questions or supply any information or to produce any document when it’s asked for

The Child Support Agency (CSA) sometimes sends inspectors to interview paying parents at work. You must let them interview your employee.

The ‘paying parent’ is the parent the child doesn’t live with.

If you’re found guilty of any of these offences you can be fined up to £1,000.
