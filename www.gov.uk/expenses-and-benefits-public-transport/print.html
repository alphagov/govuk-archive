Expenses and benefits: public transport, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

As an employer covering your employees’ public transport costs, you have certain tax, National Insurance and reporting obligations.

What’s included

Public transport costs include:

  • season tickets provided for employees
  • season ticket costs reimbursed to employees
  • loans made to employees to buy season tickets
  • contributions to subsidised or free public bus transport

Part 2: What's exempt

You don’t have to report anything to HMRC if you’re contributing to subsidised or free public bus transport. You don’t have to pay any tax or National Insurance on these costs.

An example would be when you help finance a bus route that gives your employees free or reduced-rate transport between their homes and work or between workplaces.

Part 3: What to report and pay

If the public transport costs aren’t exempt, you may have to report them to HMRC, and deduct and pay tax and National Insurance.

Season tickets provided for employees

This also includes any other public transport vouchers you provide to employees.

Who gets the benefit What to do What to deduct or pay
Employees earning at a rate of less than £8,500 per year Report on form P9D, section B Add the full cost to earnings and deduct Class 1 National Insurance through payroll
Employees earning at a rate of £8,500 or more per year Report on form P11D, section D Add the full cost to earnings and deduct Class 1 National Insurance through payroll
Directors Report on form P11D, section D Add the full cost to earnings and deduct Class 1 National Insurance through payroll

Season ticket costs reimbursed to employees

This covers arrangements where your employee buys their own season ticket but you reimburse them or cover their costs with an allowance or salary increase. This counts as earnings, so you’ll need to:

  • add the cost to your employee’s other earnings
  • deduct and pay PAYE tax and Class 1 National Insurance through payroll

Loans made to employees to buy season tickets

You should treat this as you would any other loan made to an employee.
