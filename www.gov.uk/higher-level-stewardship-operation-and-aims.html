Higher Level Stewardship: operation and aims

How Higher Level Stewardship is set up for farmers, land managers and tenants in England, its aims, and how to apply.

Introduction

Higher Level Stewardship (HLS) is one element of the Environmental Stewardship (ES) scheme - a government scheme that is open to all farmers, land managers and tenants in England. It is a voluntary scheme, designed to deliver significant environmental benefits in high priority areas.

HLS is usually combined with Entry Level Stewardship (ELS), Uplands ELS or Organic ELS options, but unlike these, entry into the scheme is subject to careful assessment and is a competitive scheme to apply for, with budgets set for each financial year. A wide range of management options are offered, which support key features of the different areas of the English countryside.

This guide explains the aims of ES, how HLS works and how to apply.

Aims of Environmental Stewardship

HLS is one element of ES delivered by Natural England on behalf of the Department for Food, Environment and Rural Affairs (Defra) as part of the Rural Development Programme for England (RDPE). The scheme encourages effective environmental management across a range of farmland, providing funding to farmers and other land managers in England.

The main objectives of ES are:

  • conserving wildlife and maintaining biodiversity
  • maintaining and enhancing landscape quality and character
  • protecting the historic environment and natural resources
  • promoting public access and understanding of the countryside
  • protecting natural resources

The secondary objectives of ES are genetic conservation - including traditional breeds of cattle and varieties of fruit trees - and flood management. ES also has a general aim of helping the natural environment to adapt to climate change, eg by reducing greenhouse gas emissions and providing and protecting carbon storage.

Farm visits and public education

One of the aims of ES is to help the public learn how the English countryside works. HLS agreement holders can be paid to host farm visits from schools (for curricular and other studies) and special interest groups for guided walks.

For more information on ES, see our guides on ES: the basics and Organic Entry Level Stewardship (OELS).

How HLS works

HLS involves more complex types of management than ELS. Land managers need advice and support, and agreements are tailored to local circumstances. HLS is a competitive scheme and each HLS application will be assessed against specific local targets.

Applications that demonstrate good environment management for target areas and represent good value for money are the most likely to be successful.

In your application, you can choose from over 90 management options and supplements, or choose from a combination of options that can occupy the same land, each of which supports key features of the English countryside. Your Natural England advisor will provide expert support and your Farm Environment Plan (FEP), which identifies the conditions and features of your land, will will help you assess the sort of management that will be most beneficial.

Higher Level Stewardship payments

HLS agreements last for 10 years with the option of a five year break clause. Payments are made every six months and relate to the options that have been chosen. HLS includes payments for capital items such as hedgerow restoration.

You can find the value of the various management options and supplements in Section 3 of the 2010 edition of HLS: ES Handbook. You can access the HLS Handbook on the Natural England website.

Target areas and themes

Natural England has produced a set of targeting maps to increase the environmental benefits delivered through HLS. These maps will help to get the most appropriate management in geographic areas where environmental outcomes are likely to be greatest. The targeting maps are the first systematic joining together of information on biodiversity, landscape, natural resource protection, public access and historic interests.

There are 110 multi-objective target areas across England that will benefit from the HLS agreements. These are areas where Natural England aims to focus delivery of HLS to maximise good environmental outcomes. The priorities for each target area are set out in a Target Area Statement. You can find HLS target areas and statements on the Natural England website.

HLS theme statements

There are features and opportunities outside target areas where HLS can be important. The theme approach allows those farmers and land managers outside the target areas, who may be moving from expiring classic schemes, or those with new HLS applications to meet theme priorities.

Find detailed information on HLS targeting on the Natural England website.

You can also get advice on how to relate HLS themes to your application from your local Natural England office.

Applying for HLS

To apply for a HLS agreement, you will need to contact your local Natural England office. Technical advice and support will be provided by Natural England advisers.

You will also have to be invited to produce a FEP before your application can be considered.

The Farm Environment Plan

In your FEP you will need to mark the location of any existing features of interest on your land and describe their condition. Guidance is available to help you create your FEP or you can use the services of an FEP surveyor.

From 1 January 2010, a Farm Overview and Opportunities form replaced Parts 3, 4 and 5 of the FEP application form. You can find the detail on these and other changes in the third edition of the HSL FEP Manual.

You can find out more about FEPs in the guide on understanding and implementing a FEP.

You can find detailed information about FEPs on the Natural England website.

You can access the HLS FEP Manual on the Natural England website.

You can access the HLS: ES Handbook on the Natural England website.

You can find all the ES forms on the forms index page of the Natural England website.

HLS and other schemes

In general, Natural England cannot pay you for any management options that you - or your landlord - are required to do under an existing scheme or obligation.

ELS, including the Uplands strands of ELS, OELS and HLS have been designed to complement each other. If you already have an ELS, Uplands ELS and/or OELS agreement, you will need to re-apply for these options when you make your HLS application. This will ensure that the elements of ES are combined in the most effective way.

To find out more about transferring land to HLS from previously existing agreements, see the pages on the Countryside Stewardship Scheme and Environmentally Sensitive Areas scheme in our guide on Environmental Stewardship: the basics.

Find out more general information about ES in the guide on Environmental Stewardship: the basics.

As a general rule, land receiving payments from the Single Payment Scheme (SPS) may be in addition to your HLS payment. Natural England has a regulatory responsibility to cross-check all land use options against those declared for the SPS to ensure that the land use declared for the SPS does not conflict or appear to conflict with the requirements of the HLS option - for example an energy crop on land that should be permanent pasture.

You can find details on HLS and other agri-environment schemes in section 1 of the HLS handbook on the Natural England website.

Additional requirements of HLS

If you are a HLS agreement holder, you must comply with your chosen management options. You must also fulfil any obligations relating to your existing ELS, Uplands ELS and/or Organic ELS options, and to cross compliance requirements.

For full details of additional requirements, you should contact the Natural England Enquiry Service on 0845 600 3078, or consult the HLS handbook on the Natural England website.

Changing an HLS agreement

You should be able to carry out the options and management required under your agreement without difficulty. However, it is possible that you may need to change the choice or location of your management options due to an unforeseen and serious situation arising, eg flooding or severe weed infestation.

It is important that there is continuity of management during your agreement if the environmental benefits are to be achieved. Amendments are, therefore, only to be used for changes that are absolutely necessary.

For the detail on making changes, you should read the HLS handbook on the Natural England website.

Breaches and appeals

You will be in breach of your agreement if you fail to comply with any agreement conditions or make false or misleading statements in your application or other correspondence.

You can find guidance on breaches and appeals procedures on the Natural England website.

Organisations that can help

You can get detailed information on HLS from the Natural England website. Natural England is responsible for conserving and enhancing the natural environment across England, and for running the HLS scheme on behalf of Defra.

You can find HLS guidance on the Natural England website, or contact the Natural England Enquiry Service on 0845 600 3078.

For information on completing a FEP, access the FEP features manual on the Natural England website.

For information about payments, find the HSL payments booklet on the Natural England website.

You can also find HLS management options and capital works information on the Natural England website.

Find out more in the guides on ES: the basics, ELS and OELS.

Find regulations, licences, standards and trade bodies relevant to farming in your business sector.

You can also contact the Natural England Enquiry Service on 0845 600 3078.

Defra aims to help the farming industry operate as efficiently as possible. Defra administers European support policies that provide around £3 billion of funding for UK agriculture, including HLS. It also administers the Whole Farm Approach, which offers a more efficient way for farmers and growers to meet regulatory requirements.

You can also call the Defra helpline on 08459 33 55 77.

The National Farmers’ Union (NFU) represents the farmers and growers of England and Wales. It aims to promote successful and socially responsible agriculture and horticulture, while ensuring the long-term viability of rural communities.

You can speak to an NFU business advisor by contacting the NFU Callfirst helpline on 0870 845 8458 (NFU membership number required).

You can find out about the NFU’s services on their website.

The Rural Payments Agency (RPA) is an Executive Agency of Defra and provides services such as rural payments, rural inspections and livestock tracing. It also maintains the Rural Land Register of farmed land in England

For more information, contact the RPA Customer Service Centre on 0845 603 7777.

In England, the Farm Advisory System advises farmers about cross compliance. For further information, call the Cross Compliance helpline on 0845 345 1302.

Find information on cross compliance requirements on the Cross Compliance website.

You can learn more about cross compliance in the guides on this site on cross compliance: the basics, Statutory Management Requirements (SMR) and standards of Good Agricultural and Environmental Condition (GAEC).

Farmers are likely to come into contact with local authorities, which enforce various regulations about farming, land use, food standards and environmental matters. Your local authority may also be able to provide further information or resources.

Find contact details for your local authority through our Contacts Directory.
