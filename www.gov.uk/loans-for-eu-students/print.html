Benefits & credits: Loans for EU students

Notes

Part 1: Overview

Student loans for European Union (EU) students

You can apply for a loan to help with the cost of tuition:

  • up to £9,000 for full-time EU students
  • up to £6,750 for part-time EU students

There are rules on eligibility and how much money you’ll get.

Loans (plus interest) have to be paid back and repayments start once you finish your course and start earning more than £21,000 (if you’re living in the UK).

Part-time students may start repaying their loan before their course finishes.

Extra help

You might also be able to get:

  • scholarships and bursaries from your college or university

Part 2: What you'll get

Help with tuition fees

To help pay your tuition fees you can get a loan, called a ‘Tuition Fee Loan’.

Full-time EU student Tuition Fee Loan
Full-time up to £9,000
Full-time at a private university or college up to £6,000
Part-time EU student Tuition Fee Loan
Part-time up to £6,750
Part-time at a private university or college up to £4,500

Part 3: Eligibility

Eligibility

Eligibility depends on:

  • your nationality or residency status
  • your course
  • your university or college

EU students

To get a Tuition Fee Loan you must be an EU national, or the family member of an EU national

To qualify for additional help you must:

  • be an EU national, or the family member of an EU national
  • have lived in the UK for 3 years or more before your course starts
  • be living in the UK on the first day of the academic year in which your course starts

Migrant workers

Migrant workers from the EEA or Switzerland and children of Turkish workers may also qualify for a Tuition Fee Loan and additional help. For more detail download the guide ‘How you are assessed and paid’ (PDF, 300KB).

Your course

This should be one of the following:

  • a first degree, eg, a Bachelor of Arts, Science or Education (BA, BSc or BEd)
  • a Foundation Degree
  • a Certificate of Higher Education
  • a Diploma of Higher Education (DipHE)
  • a Higher National Certificate (HNC)
  • a Higher National Diploma (HND)
  • Initial Teacher Training
  • further training of youth and community workers

Generally you’ll only get student finance if you’re doing your first higher education qualification. However, you may still get it if, for example:

  • you’ve changed course
  • you’ve withdrawn from your course but decide to start again
  • you’re ‘topping up’ a higher education qualification – for example, you’ve finished a foundation degree and now want to do an honours degree

Part-time courses

Part-time courses need to be least 25% of an equivalent full-time course (for example 4 years part-time instead of 1 year full-time). This is known as ‘course intensity’. You can check course intensity with your university or college.

Your university or college

This should be at a university, college or other institution that offers a qualifying course.

Part 4: How to claim

The application process is different if you’re applying for a Tuition Fee Loan and additional help.

Tuition Fee Loan only

When to apply

  • full-time students can apply from April 2012
  • part-time students can apply from August 2012
  • deadline – up to 9 months after the start of the academic year

Check when the academic years starts with your university of college.

Application forms

  • forms for full-time students are available in April 2012

Documents to support your application

You need to send proof of identity when you first apply for student finance. The application form will tell you how to do this.

You might need other documents to prove things like income or any children you have:

  • guidance available: April 2012

What happens next?

It can take up to 6 weeks to process an application and you may be asked to provide more information – for example, household income.

Tuition Fee Loan and additional help

The application process is different, you apply in the same way as students from England.

Part 5: Further information

When you’ll get your money

Tuition Fee Loans are paid to your university or college at the start of each term.

Paying back your student loan

You have to pay back your student loan (plus interest) once you finish your course and start earning more than £21,000 a year.
