Your employment contract: how it can be changed, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Consultation

Usually, you and your employer must agree for a contract to be changed.

If you want to make a change, speak to your employer and explain why. You can’t insist on making changes unless they’re covered by a statutory right (eg not working on a Sunday).

If your employer wants to make changes, they should

  • consult or negotiate with you or your representative (eg from a trade union or staff association)
  • explain the reasons why
  • listen to alternative ideas you may have

Part 2: Types of change

If your employer changes any terms in your written statement with your agreement, they must confirm the change in writing within a month. You must be told exactly what has changed in the statement.

If your employer changes terms and conditions that aren’t in the written statement (eg like the right to sick leave), they should tell you where to find information about the change (eg a company handbook, noticeboard or intranet site).

Collective agreements and changes to written statements

A collective agreement may exist between your employer and trade unions or a staff association. If your employer makes agreed changes to the terms of the collective agreement, they should inform you in writing.

Negotiations under a collective agreement can lead to changes in the terms of your written statement (eg pay or working hours). If you are covered by a collective agreement, any changes will apply to you whether you are a member of a union or staff association or not.

Flexibility clauses

Flexibility clauses are terms in your contract that give your employer the right to change some of your terms and conditions. A flexibility clause could be that your job location can be changed.

Your employer isn’t allowed to make completely unreasonable changes using a flexibility clause.

Example telling you to move to another country to work at 1 day’s notice would be completely unreasonable.

Changes of employer

If your employer changes, usually you must be given a new written statement of particulars within 2 months.

However, if the name of the business changes or there’s a new employer but no other changes in terms and conditions, your employer doesn’t need to issue a new written statement. However, they should inform you in writing of the changes within a month.

Disciplinary measures

Sometimes changes to your terms and conditions like a demotion can happen as a result of a disciplinary measure.

Your employer should make sure the staff handbook or intranet site contains information about how this could happen in the section dealing with disciplinary procedures.

Part 3: Problems with changes

Problems can arise if:

  • an employer tries to change your contract without agreement or re-employs you on new terms and conditions
  • there is a breach of contract where one of the terms in a contract is broken (eg an employer doesn’t pay agreed wages or employees don’t work agreed hours)

Talk to your employer

If you have a problem over contract changes, try to solve things informally by talking to your employer or through mediation.

You can also get advice from your trade union representative (if you’re a member of a union), Citizen’s Advice or ACAS. If you live in Northern Ireland you can also get advice from Labour Relations Agency (LRA) .

Legal action

If the problem can’t be solved, you may have the right to take legal action. Get advice before you do - legal action can be expensive. If you’re a trade union member, you may be able to get legal advice from your union.

Making a change without agreement

If your employer makes a change to contracts without your agreement (including by using flexibility clauses unreasonably), you may:

  • have the right to refuse to work the new conditions
  • say you are working any new terms under protest and you’re treating the change as a breach of contract
  • resign and claim constructive dismissal
  • be able to take a case to an employment tribunal

In Northern Ireland you would take your case to an industrial tribunal.

Working new terms and conditions

If you disagree with new terms and conditions but don’t say or do anything, this may count as agreeing to the changes.

Re-employment on new terms and conditions

Employers may, as a last resort, end your contract and re-employ you on new terms and conditions.

Employers who are dismissing employees must follow the legally required:

  • redundancy procedure in England, Wales and Scotland
  • statutory minimum dismissal in Northern Ireland

If your employer does dismiss and re-employ you, you may be able to take a case to a tribunal and claim:

  • breach of contract
  • unfair dismissal

Breach of contract claims

If you can’t solve things informally with your employer, you may be able to take a breach of contract case to a civil court, an employment tribunal. If you live in Northern Ireland you can take your case to an industrial tribunal.

If you go to a tribunal, your employer may be able to make a counter-claim.

Claims and counter-claims can only go to a tribunal if they:

  • are related to an employment contract issue
  • still haven’t been solved when the employee ends their employment with your company

Also, the claim or counter-claim mustn’t be related to a personal injury or things like a contract term that deals with intellectual property rights.

Time limits

Usually, you must make a claim to a tribunal within 3 months of ending your employment at the company. Your employer must make a counter-claim within 6 weeks of receiving a copy of your claim.

Compensation limits

If a tribunal agrees with your claim, they may award you compensation. You can only get compensation for financial loss (eg non-payment of wages) in breach of contract claim. The maximum compensation that can be given is £25,000.
