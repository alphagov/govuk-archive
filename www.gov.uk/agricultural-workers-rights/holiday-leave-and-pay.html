Part 4: Holiday leave and pay

The annual leave year starts on 1 October and ends on 30 September the following year.

The amount of leave you can take depends on the number of days you work each week.

Below is the annual leave entitlement for workers who work a complete annual leave year for the same employer.

Days worked each week Annual leave (days)
More than 6 38
More than 5 but not more than 6 35
More than 4 but not more than 5 31
More than 3 but not more than 4 25
More than 2 but not more than 3 20
More than 1 but not more than 2 13
One or less 7.5

Guaranteed overtime hours can be included in the number of days you work each week as long as:

  • you have to work them as part of your contract
  • you’re paid for this overtime even if there’s no work for you to do

Any other overtime that you work can’t be counted.

If you work a different number of days a week

If you work a different number of days each week you need to calculate your annual leave entitlement separately.

‘Qualifying days’ are the number of days you work (including guaranteed overtime) or take:

  • annual leave
  • statutory ordinary adoption leave
  • statutory ordinary maternity leave
  • statutory paternity leave
  • sick leave (paid or unpaid)

To calculate your leave entitlement you should:

  1. Work out the number of weeks from 1 October to the day before you start your holiday.

  2. Work out the number of qualifying days in that time.

  3. Divide the number of qualifying days by the number of weeks in step 1 - this gives you the number of days you worked each week.

  4. Work out your annual leave entitlement using the above table.

Example

Before going on leave, you work for 25 weeks.

In that time you’ve worked 125 days, taken 2 days’ sick leave and taken statutory paternity leave of 10 days.

Divide total qualifying days (137) by weeks worked (25) = 5.48 days worked on average per week.

Annual leave entitlement = 35 days.

Incomplete leave years

If you’ve not been employed by the same employer for an entire leave year, you’ll need to calculate your annual leave differently.

  1. use the table above to work out what your annual leave would be for a year

  2. work out the number of weeks you’ve worked continuously

  3. divide the number of weeks you have worked continuously by 52

  4. multiply the number of days from step one, by the answer to step three

This gives you the amount of leave you have.

Example

You work more than 5 days but less than 6 days a week = 35 annual leave.

You’ve worked for 30 weeks continuously.

30 weeks divided by 52 = 0.576.

35 days multiplied by 0.576 = 20 days.

Bank holidays

Bank holidays are included in your annual leave entitlement.

If a public holiday falls on a day you normally work, your employer can either ask you:

  • not to work - the day is an annual leave day
  • to work - you should be paid overtime for the hours you worked (the day remains as annual leave to be taken in the future)
