Staying in touch with someone in prison, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Letters and telephone calls

You can contact a prisoner by writing to them - normally there’s no limit on the amount of letters you can send.

You can also visit them.

Letters that can be opened

Most letters sent to (and from) prison are checked by prison staff.

Letters from solicitors and courts aren’t opened.

Telephone calls

The prisoner has to call you using a prison phone. Mobiles are banned.

Prison staff can listen to most types of call and they can be recorded.

Some calls are not monitored or recorded - eg to the prisoner’s legal adviser.

Part 2: The internet and social media

You can’t email the prisoner and must not update any social networking website on the prisoner’s behalf - eg Facebook or Twitter.

Part 3: Banned items

Things you can’t send (or give to) a prisoner include anything that:

  • is indecent or obscene
  • threatens the security of the prison
  • is written in code
  • the prisoner has written that they want to publish and be paid for

It’s a criminal offence to send or give a prisoner:

  • illegal drugs
  • alcohol
  • weapons
  • a camera
  • a mobile phone

If you’re unsure what you can send or give, contact the prison.

Part 4: Sending money

To send the prisoner money either send a cheque or postal order made payable to ‘HM Prison Service’. Send it to the Governor of the prison.

You should include:

  • your name and address
  • the prisoner’s name and prison number

The money is then paid into their prison account.

Privately-run prisons

If you want to send money to someone in a privately-run prison, contact the prison directly.

Do not send cash to the prisoner.

Part 5: Visiting someone in prison

The prison will send you a document called a ‘visiting order’ stating a booking number.

Some prisons also let you book visits by email.

If you’re visiting a prisoner on remand you don’t need a visiting order - just contact the prison.

Prisons have different rules on things like when and how often you can visit - and the number of visitors allowed at the same time.

Contact the prison about:

  • their facilities - eg to check accessibility if you’re disabled
  • what happens on the day

You can only visit a prisoner if they want you to visit them.

How often you can visit someone in prison

A convicted prisoner is allowed at least two 60-minute visits every 4 weeks.

A prisoner on remand (waiting for their trial) is allowed three 60-minute visits a week.

If you live a long way from the prison, you may be able to make longer visits, but less often.

Part 6: Get help with travel costs to prison

If you’re aged over 18, you may get help with the cost of visiting a close relative or partner in prison.

You must be claiming a certain benefit(s) like:

  • Income Support
  • Income-based Jobseeker’s Allowance
  • Employment and Support Allowance

Find out more by contacting the prison or the Assisted Prison Visits Unit.
