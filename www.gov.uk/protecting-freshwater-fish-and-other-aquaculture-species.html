Protecting freshwater fish and other aquaculture species

How to reduce the risk of exotic and non-exotic diseases on fish and shellfish farms: reporting infections, species affected and Confirmed Designation.

Introduction

If you introduce certain fish and shellfish diseases to places where they are not currently found, they can cause significant economic and environmental damage. These ‘notifiable’ diseases are classified as either exotic or non-exotic. All notifiable diseases are strictly controlled. If you suspect that your stock is infected you are legally required to tell the Fish Health Inspectorate (FHI).

The FHI will normally request the restriction of fish and shellfish on your site so they can investigate the suspected outbreak and test for the disease. These restrictions will be lifted if the tests are negative or - if a disease is confirmed - once the FHI is satisfied your site has been properly cleared, cleansed and disinfected.

This guide gives details of each notifiable disease and explains their symptoms and the species they affect. It also provides advice to help you reduce the possibility of outbreaks of disease at your site.

Notifiable fish and shellfish diseases

For many years, the government has tried to prevent serious fish, shellfish and crustacean diseases being introduced to Great Britain. This has been a significant factor in making sure Great Britain has maintained a high aquatic animal health status, remaining free from the most serious diseases.

In the early 1990s national fish health rules were replaced by EU rules. These are designed to encourage trade within the single market and, at the same time, protect parts of the EU with a high aquatic animal health status - eg Great Britain.

Notifiable diseases

EU Council Directive 2006/88/EC sets out legislation to prevent and control certain diseases in aquatic animals. These diseases are ‘notifiable’ - ie the owner or anyone else attending to the animals must immediately report suspicion of notifiable diseases to the FHI.

Notifiable diseases are categorised as either exotic or non-exotic. Exotic diseases are those diseases not currently present in the EU. They could have a significant economic and environmental impact if they were introduced. If an Exotic disease is detected, all infected fish and shellfish must be destroyed as soon as possible. See the page in this guide on exotic notifiable diseases.

Non-exotic diseases are those that are present in parts of the EU, under containment and subject to long-term eradication. They are controlled to prevent them spreading to unaffected areas of the EU. See the page in this guide on non-exotic notifiable diseases.

As well as exotic and non-exotic diseases, there are other notifiable diseases that are present in the EU and which are controlled by national programmes. See the page in this guide on other notifiable diseases.

The FHI also has powers to prevent the spread of new and emerging diseases, that are not listed but that have the potential to present a significant economic or environmental threat to our aquatic animal populations.

Measures to prevent the spread of notifiable and emerging diseases

The spread of notifiable diseases is controlled by:

  • strict rules for importing live fish, molluscs and crustacea - see the guide on importing live fish, molluscs and crustacea
  • regular monitoring of fish, shellfish and crustacean farms by the FHI to check for disease
  • speedy containment of outbreaks of serious disease where detected by the FHI or notified by someone else - see the page in this guide on how fish and shellfish diseases are contained
  • operators of fish farms, shellfish farms, crustacean farms, and fisheries taking all the necessary precautions when buying, selling, keeping and moving live aquatic animals - see the guide on moving fish and shellfish

How fish and shellfish diseases are contained

Where a notifiable disease is suspected in aquatic animals (fish, molluscs or crustaceans), the FHI will undertake an investigation and samples will be taken for diagnostic testing. The FHI will also apply controls to the affected area in the form of an initial designation notice in order to minimise the risk of any further disease spreading.

Initial designation notice

An initial designation notice will be issued where the FHI suspect that a listed or notifiable disease is present, and will remain in force while diagnostic tests are undertaken to determine this.

An initial designation notice is a temporary ‘standstill’ notice that is usually served to the owner or operator of the affected site or area. It will be served in the form of a written notice, describing the control area and the restrictions applied to prevent any further spread of disease.

These control measures restrict movements of aquatic animals into, out of and within the affected area. Where relevant, controls also extend to the movement of equipment and vehicles liable to transmit disease and the application of additional site biosecurity measures while there is evidence of clinical disease and a risk of transfer of infection.

If the tests results prove negative for a notifiable disease, the FHI will lift an initial designation notice immediately.

Confirmed Designation

Where the presence of a notifiable disease is confirmed through diagnostic testing, and the affected site is unable to undertake an immediate FHI-supervised site clearance and disinfection, the FHI will issue a Confirmed Designation.

A Confirmed Designation is a public and legal order restricting aquatic animal movements into, out of and within the affected area without prior written permission from the FHI, and remains in force until the disease is no longer present in the designated area. Under the Confirmed Designation, additional control measures may also be applied to vehicle and equipment movements and disinfection, additional site biosecurity measures and the display of public information notices.

A confirmed designation will not be lifted until the FHI are satisfied that the disease in question is no longer present. Under the current policy of the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra), this is achieved by either:

  • a complete stock cull and disinfection of all waters within the designated area, or
  • completion of a satisfactory monitoring and retest programme to standards specified in European legislation

Sites undergoing a monitoring and retest programme must expect to remain under a Confirmed Designation for a minimum of four years and will be instructed to undertake a sequential/staged site clearance and disinfection wherever this is practical.

Exotic notifiable diseases

Exotic notifiable diseases are not currently present within the EU - if they were to be introduced to the EU, they could cause significant economic problems for the aquaculture sector. They could also harm wild species. In general, there is no treatment for exotic notifiable diseases, and any outbreak could only be controlled by killing or destroying all affected animals. If you suspect that your stock is infected with one of these diseases, you must notify the Fish Health Inspectorate as soon as possible.

Exotic notifiable diseases affecting fish

There are two exotic notifiable fish diseases listed, epizootic ulcerative syndrome (EUS) and epizootic haematopoietic necrosis (EHN).

EUS is a serious disease that affects over 100 species of fresh and brackish water fish. There is no treatment for the disease and it has a mortality rate of up to 80 per cent. The external symptoms of EUS are:

  • red spots
  • blackish burn-like marks
  • deep ulcers at the base of the fins and over the body

EHN is a serious systemic viral disease that is known to affect rainbow trout and perch. There is no treatment for EHN and it has a mortality rate of up to 100 per cent in perch, lower in rainbow trout. The external symptoms of EHN are:

  • abdominal distension
  • darkening of the body
  • reddening fin bases
  • skin ulcers

For details of how EUS and EHN are diagnosed and controlled, download Centre for Environment, Fisheries and Aquaculture Science (Cefas) guidance on notifiable diseases in fish from the Aquatic animal health movements (AAHM) website (PDF, 975K).

Exotic notifiable diseases affecting shellfish

There are three Exotic shellfish diseases listed - infection with:

  • Bonamia exitiosa - download Cefas information on Bonamiosis from the AAHM website (PDF, 106K)
  • Perkinsus marinus - download Cefas information on Perkinsosis from the AAHM website (PDF, 123K)
  • Mikrocytos mackini - download Cefas information on Mikrocytosis from the AAHM website (PDF, 90K)

All of these are parasitic diseases that affect various species of oyster.

Find maps and guidance on current confirmed designations for fish and shellfish diseases on the AAHM section of the Defra website.

Exotic notifiable diseases affecting crustaceans

There are two exotic crustacean diseases listed, Taura syndrome and Yellowhead disease. Both these diseases affect several species of shrimp and prawn, and Yellowhead disease is also known to affect one species of krill. You can find out about List I crustacean diseases on the Network of Aquaculture Centres in Asia and the Pacific (NACA) website.

Exotic notifiable diseases affecting molluscs

Ostreid herpesvirus 1 μvar (OsHV-1 μvar) is a virulent viral disease of the Pacific oyster - Crassostrea gigas - also known as the rock or cupped oyster.

OsHV-1 μvar is a notifiable disease in England and Wales.

A sampling and testing programme requires sampling from farms or mollusc farming areas keeping Pacific oysters in compartments covered by the programme. You can find the latest developments on the programme on the European Commission website.

You can find news and the latest maps of Oyster Herpesvirus containment areas on Defra’s Aquatic animal health and movements website.

If you import Pacific oysters to stock your farm, see the page on authorisation and documentation for live fish, mollusc and crustacea importers in the guide on importing live fish, molluscs and crustacea. Emergency controls may also ban or apply controls on imports from containment areas and areas previously subject to containment measures.

Non-exotic notifiable diseases

Non-exotic notifiable diseases are present in parts of the EU. If they were to spread to other areas of the EU, they could cause significant economic problems for the aquaculture sector. They could also harm wild species. For many of these diseases there is no treatment, and in such cases outbreaks are controlled by killing or destroying all affected animals. If you suspect that your stock is infected with one of these diseases, you must notify the Fish Health Inspectorate as soon as possible.

Non-exotic notifiable diseases affecting fish

There are four non-exotic fish diseases listed:

  • infectious salmon anaemia (ISA)
  • infectious haematopoietic necrosis (IHN)
  • viral haemorrhagic septicaemia (VHS)
  • Koi herpesvirus (KHV)

ISA is a serious viral disease that mainly affects farmed salmon although some other farmed species are also at risk. There is no treatment for the disease and it has a mortality level of up to 90 per cent. The external symptoms of ISA are:

  • pale gills
  • distended abdomen
  • lethargy
  • lack of appetite
  • protruding eyes
  • gasping at the surface of the water

IHN is a serious viral disease that affects salmon and trout. There is no known treatment and mortality can be up to 100 per cent in juvenile rainbow trout. The external symptoms of IHN are:

  • lethargy with periods of frenzy
  • darkening of skin
  • pale gills
  • distended abdomen
  • protruding eyes

VHS is a serious viral disease that affects rainbow trout in particular but many other species are at risk. There is no known treatment and mortality levels can be up to 80 per cent. The external symptoms of VHS are:

  • bleeding
  • protruding eyes
  • anaemia
  • swimming in spirals

KHV is a serious viral disease that affects all varieties of common carp. There is no treatment and mortality can be 100 per cent. The external symptoms of KHV are:

  • lethargy or erratic behaviour
  • loss of balance
  • sunken eyes
  • patches of dead tissue in the gills

For details of how ISA, IHN, VHS and KHV are diagnosed and controlled, download Cefas guidance on notifiable diseases in fish (PDF, 975KB).

Non-exotic notifiable diseases affecting shellfish

There are two non-exotic shellfish diseases listed - infection with:

  • Bonamia ostreae - download Cefas information on Bonamiosis from the AAHM website (PDF, 106KB)
  • Marteilia refringens - download Cefas information on marteiliosis from the AAHM website (PDF, 120KB)

Infection with Bonamia ostreae affects certain species of oyster, including the European flat oyster (the native oyster Ostrea edulis). External symptoms may include yellow to black discolouration and perforated ulcers.

Infection with Martilia refringens affects certain species of oyster and mussel. Oysters that are infected may lose pigmentation and turn yellow. The mantle may become translucent and the shell may stop growing. You can download Cefas information on marteiliosis from the eFish Business website (PDF, 120KB).

Non-exotic notifiable diseases affecting crustaceans

White spot disease affects a number of species of decapods crustacean, including prawns, shrimp and crayfish. You can find out about white spot disease on the Network of Aquaculture Centres in Asia and the Pacific website.

Other notifiable diseases

Certain other notifiable diseases are controlled in the UK as they could have both an economic and environmental impact if they spread throughout the country or to other parts of the EU. In the UK these diseases are controlled through national programmes approved by the EU. If you suspect that your stock is infected with one of these diseases, you must notify the Fish Health Inspectorate as soon as possible.

These diseases only affect and are:

  • bacterial kidney disease (BKD)
  • infection with Gyrodactylus salaris (GS)
  • spring viraemia of carp (SVC)

BKD is a bacterial disease that particularly affects wild Pacific salmon but other salmonid fish are also at risk. There is no known treatment and mortality levels can be very high. The external symptoms of BKD are:

  • lethargy
  • skin darkening
  • protruding eyes
  • blisters on the sides of the fish

GS is an external parasite that primarily affects wild Atlantic salmon in freshwater. There is no treatment other than eradication and mortality levels can be up to 95 per cent. The external symptoms of GS are:

  • white or grey appearance
  • secondary fungal infections

SVC is a serious viral disease that affects all species of carp as well as other species of coarse fish. There is no known treatment and mortality levels can be 100 per cent. The external symptoms of SVC are:

  • darkening of the skin
  • protruding eyes
  • abdominal swelling
  • pale gills
  • trailing faecal casts
  • protrusion of the anus

For details of how BKD, GS and SVC are diagnosed and controlled, download Centre for Environment, Fisheries and Aquaculture Science (Cefas) guidance on notifiable diseases in fish from the aquatic animal health movements website (PDF, 975KB).

Preventing disease on your fish or shellfish farm

There are a number of steps you can take to make sure you are doing everything possible to prevent disease being introduced to your fish farm.

Fish and shellfish health

Steps you can take to protect the health of your fish and shellfish include:

  • stocking only certified, disease-free fish and shellfish from a reputable supplier
  • training your staff in hygienic fish and shellfish handling and disease prevention methods
  • keeping health and treatment records

Husbandry

Good fish and shellfish husbandry practices include:

  • using a pathogen-free water supply, such as a borehole or a spring, or ultraviolet/ozone treated water
  • supplying water to each holding facility independently, avoiding water flowing from one tank to another
  • preventing fish and shellfish from being under undue stress from over-stocking or poor water quality
  • carrying out regular veterinary checks and introducing vaccination programmes and disease treatments where necessary
  • removing dead fish and shellfish on a daily basis and disposing of them correctly
  • managing pests to prevent the spread of disease from vermin or predators

Moving fish and shellfish

When you move fish and shellfish there are various measures you can take to prevent disease, such as:

  • exercising caution when you transfer fish and shellfish between different farms
  • properly cleaning and disinfecting lorries and tanks used to transport fish and shellfish before loading and after each delivery of fish and shellfish
  • making only single-site deliveries by lorry, wherever possible

Operating practices

There are many ways that you can increase biosecurity in the day-to-day running of your farm, including:

  • keeping production, processing and recreational areas independent and separate from one another
  • isolating each unit on the farm and using separate equipment in each unit
  • containing water, blood, packaging and organic waste that comes from slaughter and processing sites and not allowing them to be taken back to production areas
  • providing disinfection facilities between production areas
  • requiring visitors to wear protective clothing
  • disinfecting hands, boots and equipment when moving between different holding facilities
  • limiting access to production areas, for example by locating a car park some distance away
  • providing disinfectant facilities and signs requiring visitors to disinfect their boots and equipment when they enter and leave the farm

For specific biosecurity recommendations for shellfish farms, download the FHI shellfish biosecurity measures plan from the AAHM website (PDF, 501KB).

Download twenty steps towards fish farm biosecurity from the AAHM website (PDF, 361KB).

Access the index to fish and shellfish disease prevention and health management guides on the AAHM website.

Preventing disease at your fishery

Every year the Fish Health Inspectorate (FHI) and the Environment Agency typically investigate outbreaks of disease at over 100 stillwater fisheries, that resulted in the deaths of thousands of fish and damaged the businesses or clubs that operate them. Recovering from an outbreak of disease can take a long time and can be very expensive.

In many cases, these outbreaks could have been avoided by:

  • the prompt identification of signs of disease
  • reducing stress with proper stocking numbers and correctly managed habitats
  • improving water quality
  • introducing biosecurity measures

Disease identification

By promptly identifying the signs of disease, you can take action to prevent deaths among your fish. So it is very important to check the health and condition of your fish on a regular basis. Signs to look out for include changes in behaviour as well as any external clinical symptoms of disease. Download guidance on recognising fish diseases from the Environment Agency website (PDF, 305KB).

Fish stocking density

Having large numbers of fish in your fishery can be attractive to anglers, but only if the fish are healthy. Over-stocking your fishery can cause a number of problems, including:

  • increased risk of diseases spreading
  • reduced water quality as oxygen levels are lowered and toxin levels are increased
  • increased fish stress through competition for food and space
  • reduced numbers of plants leading to habitat damage and reduced water quality

Download guidance on stocking your fishery from the Environment Agency website (PDF, 693KB).

Fish habitats

Having different habitats in your fishery is good for the health of your fish, and improves the appearance of your site. Habitat features include:

  • trees and bushes on the bankside
  • aquatic plants in the water
  • areas of deep and shallow water
  • small islands

These help to reduce competition between fish and lower stress levels. Aquatic plants put oxygen into the water, helping to improve its quality and prevent disease. Download guidance on improving habitats from the Environment Agency website (PDF, 1.37MB).

Water quality

Without good quality water your fish cannot survive and thrive. Poor water quality, or any sudden changes to quality, can increase the stress levels of your fish. For details of the issues you need to monitor, download guidance on de-oxygenation in fisheries from the Environment Agency website (PDF, 399KB).

Biosecurity

There are certain steps you can take to improve the biosecurity of your fishery, including:

  • breeding your own fish, or stocking only from reputable suppliers
  • disinfecting key pieces of equipment
  • using a borehole or spring as your water source if possible
  • making improvements to the security of your site

Download Environment Agency biosecurity guidance from the aquatic animal health movements website (PDF, 95KB).

What to do if your fish are dying

If your fish are dying, you need to report it to the Environment Agency Incident Hotline on 0800 80 70 60. Lines are open 24 hours a day.
