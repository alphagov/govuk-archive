Child Benefit for 16 to 19 year olds, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

If your child is aged 16 to 19, you can get Child Benefit if they are:

  • staying on in eligible education
  • starting an approved training course
  • leaving education but registering with the armed forces or certain careers services (you can get Child Benefit extensions for a maximum of 20 weeks)

You must report your child’s education plans to the Child Benefit Office.

Part 2: Education

You can get Child Benefit until your child turns 20 if they’re in eligible education. They must be accepted onto the course before they turn 19.

Courses that count

Education must be full-time (more than an average of 12 hours’ supervised study a week) and ‘non-advanced’. This includes:

  • GCSEs
  • A levels
  • NVQ/SVQ level 1, 2 or 3
  • BTEC National Diploma, National Certificate and First Diploma
  • SCE higher grade or similar

It doesn’t include ‘advanced’ education, eg:

  • a degree
  • Diploma of Higher Education
  • NVQ level 4 or above
  • BTEC Higher National Certificate (HNC) or Higher National Diploma (HND)
  • teacher training

Study abroad

If your child’s studying abroad, you can usually only get Child Benefit for the first 12 weeks they’re away. You’ll keep getting it after this if they’re only abroad because they’re either:

  • on an educational exchange or visit (with written agreement from their school or college)
  • in education that counts for Child Benefit in a country in the European Economic Area (EEA) or Switzerland

EEA countries

The EEA includes:

  • all countries in the EU
  • Norway, Iceland and Liechtenstein

If you’re not sure whether a course counts or not, contact the Child Benefit Office.

Part 3: Training

You can still get Child Benefit up to your child’s 20th birthday if they are on an approved, unpaid course.

Your child needs to have started, enrolled or been accepted onto an approved course before their 19th birthday.

Approved courses are:

  • England - Foundation Learning Programmes or Access to Apprenticeships
  • Wales - Foundation Apprenticeships, Traineeships and Skillbuild/Skillbuild+ (if started before 1 August 2011)
  • Scotland - Get Ready for Work and Skillseekers
  • Northern Ireland - Jobskills and Training for Success, including Programme Led Apprenticeships

A course provided by an employer as part of a job doesn’t count.

If you’re not sure whether a course counts or not, contact the Child Benefit Office.

Part 4: Breaks in education or training

You may get Child Benefit for 6 months if the break is for a good reason, eg your child is ill, changing course or pregnant.

You may be able to get Child Benefit for longer than 6 months if your child is:

  • in hospital or residential care
  • getting medical treatment abroad

Voluntary breaks

You can’t normally get Child Benefit if your child has taken a voluntary break in education or training, eg if they travel overseas for personal reasons.

After the break

If your child goes back to education or training after a break, you should make a new claim.

If your child doesn’t go back into eligible education or training, you may have to pay back some of the payments you got during the break.

Part 5: Your child leaves education or training

If your child leaves education or training at or after 16, Child Benefit will stop.

When this happens depends on things like when your child’s birthday is, or the reasons why they left the education or training.

When your child leaves When payments stop
The year they turn 16 31 August after their 16th birthday (if your child turns 16 on 31 August, payments stop then)
After age 16 Last day of February, 31 May, 31 August or 30 November - whichever comes first

Example

Your child is 16 and gets their exam results in July. The results mean they can’t do the course they wanted.

Child Benefit payments will stop on 31 August.

You can apply for a 20-week extension of Child Benefit if your child is under 18 and leaves education or training if they register with local careers services or the armed forces.

Child Benefit stops straightaway if your child:

  • starts paid work for 24 hours a week or more
  • gets certain benefits (ie Income Support, Employment and Support Allowance, tax credits, Incapacity Benefit or income-based Jobseeker’s Allowance)

You must tell the Child Benefit Office if your child starts work or gets benefits.

Your child returns to school or college for exams

If your child was entered for exams before they left school or college, you can normally still get Child Benefit.

The exams must be for the course they were doing before they left - and the course must have been an eligible education or training course.

Child Benefit will normally carry on from the date of the last exam until the week that includes the earliest of:

  • the last day of February
  • 31 May
  • 31 August
  • 30 November

If your child leaves education but then goes back

If your child returns to education or training and it counts for Child Benefit, you can make another claim.

Your child needs to have started, enrolled or been accepted on a course before their 19th birthday.

Part 6: Extensions: careers services and the armed forces

You can apply for a 20-week Child Benefit extension if your 16 or 17-year-old leaves education or training but registers with your local careers services or the armed forces.

The education or training must have counted for Child Benefit and you must apply within 3 months of your child leaving.

To qualify, you must have been getting Child Benefit immediately before the extension.

Your child needs to be registered with one of the following ‘qualifying organisations’:

  • in England – the local careers service or Connexions
  • in Scotland or Wales – the local careers service
  • in Northern Ireland – the careers service of the Department for Employment and Learning, or an Education and Library Board
  • the Ministry of Defence, eg if your child is waiting to join HM Forces
  • a similar organisation to others in this list in any European Economic Area (EEA) Country

EEA countries

The EEA includes:

  • all countries in the European Union (EU)
  • Norway, Iceland and Liechtenstein

Changes that affect the extension

The extension ends if your child:

  • turns 18
  • starts paid work for 24 or more hours a week
  • gets certain benefits (eg Income Support) or tax credits
  • is no longer registered with a qualifying organisation

If your child stops working or getting benefits before the 20 weeks are up, tell the Child Benefit Office.

You may be able to reclaim Child Benefit for them up to the end of the extension period.

Part 7: Reporting education plans

You must keep the Child Benefit Office up to date about your child’s education or training plans.

The Child Benefit Office will write to you during your child’s last school year to ask what your child’s plans are. They’ll contact you:

  • between January and June
  • mid-June or mid-November if you live in Scotland

You’ll be sent a form to fill in. You don’t need to reply until you know if your child is staying on in education or training.

You also have to tell the Child Benefit Office about any changes to your child’s education or if they leave their course.

You can also report your child’s education plans online:

  • tell the Child Benefit Office your child is staying in education
  • tell the Child Benefit Office your child is leaving education
