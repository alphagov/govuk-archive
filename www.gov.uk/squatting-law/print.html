Squatting and the law, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

Squatting is when someone enters and occupies an empty or abandoned property that they don’t own or rent, without the owner’s permission.

A tenant who enters a property with the permission of the landlord, but who falls behind with rent payments, is not a squatter.

Although squatting isn’t illegal, it’s normally a crime for a person to enter someone’s home without permission and refuse to leave when the owner asks.

Part 2: Squatting and squatters' rights

Simply being on another person’s property without their permission is not usually a crime. But if squatters commit other crimes when entering or staying in a property, the police can take action against them.

These crimes could include:

  • causing damage when entering the property
  • causing damage while in the property
  • not leaving when the owner of the property asks them to (if it’s a residential property)
  • not leaving when they’re told to by a court
  • stealing from the property
  • using utilities like electricity or gas without permission
  • making a lot of noise or fly-tipping

Squatters’ rights

There’s a law that makes it illegal to threaten or use violence to enter a property with people in it. The law was introduced to stop landlords using violence to evict their tenants.

However, this law doesn’t apply to a displaced residential occupier or a protected intending occupier. This means that if you break down the door of your own home where you’re living, you aren’t committing a crime.

You may have to prove you’re a displaced residential occupier or protected intending occupier if the police are called.

Squatters taking ownership of a property

It’s difficult and very rare for squatters to take ownership of a property. To do this, they would have to stay in a property without the owner’s permission for at least 10 years.

Part 3: Getting your property back

Getting an interim possession order

If you own or occupy the property that has been squatted, you can use an interim possession order (IPO) to get your property back quickly. If you follow the right procedure, you can usually get one issued by the courts within a few days.

To get final possession of the property, you must also make an application for possession when you apply for the IPO.

Use form N130 to apply for an interim possession order and for possession.

Exceptions

You can’t use an IPO if:

  • you’re also making a claim for damages caused by the squatters - instead make an ordinary claim for possession under Part 55(A) of the Civil Procedure Rules
  • more than 28 days have passed since you found out about the squatters
  • you’re trying to evict former tenants, sub-tenants or licensees - instead see Private renting: evictions or Council and housing association evictions.

Once squatters are served with an IPO, they must leave the property within 24 hours. If they don’t, they’re committing a crime and could serve up to 6 months in prison.

It’s also a crime for them to return to the property within 12 months.

Part 4: Where to get help

If you’re squatting

If you’re squatting, you’re legally classed as homeless. Read the advice from Shelter and contact your local council for help.

To report squatters

If you see someone breaking into a property, call the police.

If you suspect someone is squatting in a property, report them to your local council or the police.

Displaced residential occupiers

If you find squatters in your home who won’t leave, you’re a ‘displaced residential occupier’ and the squatters are committing a crime. Call the police.

Protected intending occupiers

If there are squatters in a property you’re planning on moving into, you may be a ‘protected intending occupier’. Give the squatters a statement showing you are the rightful occupier.

You can find the legal definition of a protected intending occupier, and full details of the statement you need to write, in section 12A of the Criminal Law Act 1977. Citizens Advice can provide help and advice.

You should then call the police.
