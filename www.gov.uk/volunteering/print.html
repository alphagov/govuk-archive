Volunteering, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Find volunteer placements

You can search for volunteer placements:

  • on the Do-it website
  • in your local community through Community Service Volunteers and by checking local noticeboards (eg, in your library) or newspapers
  • on the VSO website, if you are looking for overseas placements
  • by contacting your local Volunteer Centre

Part 2: Volunteering for young people

You can also register for volunteer placements through:

  • the National Citizen Service website, if you’re 16 to 17 years old in England or Wales, or 15 to 16 in Northern Ireland.
  • the International Citizen Service website, if you’d like to volunteer abroad and are between 18 and 25

Part 3: Your rights

Most volunteers don’t have a contract of employment, so don’t have the rights of an employee or worker.

You’re usually given a volunteer agreement that explains:

  • the level of supervision and support you’ll get
  • what training you’ll get
  • whether you’re covered under the organisation’s employer or public liability insurance
  • health and safety issues
  • any expenses the organisation will cover

The volunteer agreement isn’t compulsory, but sets out what you can expect from the organisation. It doesn’t form a contract between you and the organisation. The National Council for Voluntary Organisations (NCVO) has information on volunteers’ legal status.

Part 4: Pay and expenses

You won’t be paid for your time as a volunteer but you may get money to cover expenses. This is usually limited to covering food, drink and travel or any equipment you need to buy.

If you get any other payment or benefit in kind, you may be classed as an employee or worker, rather than a volunteer. This means you have certain employment rights, like getting the minimum wage.

Example 1

You volunteer to work at a company to get some work experience. You’re given travel expenses even though you walk to work. This is payment, rather than out-of-pocket expenses. It means you must be paid at least the minimum wage.

Example 2

You’re an unpaid intern at a record company, but you’re given free CDs as a perk. The CDs are ‘benefits in kind’. They mean you must be paid at least the minimum wage.

Part 5: When you can volunteer

Age limits

There’s no upper age limit on volunteering. However, some organisations’ insurance policies don’t cover people under 16 or over a certain age (usually 80).

Under 14s can’t work for a profit-making organisation, even if they’re not paid.

Some local councils have bylaws with extra rules about the work young people can do. For example, they may see a charity shop as a profit-making organisation and not let young people volunteer in them.

Aged 50+? Find volunteering opportunities with the Retired and Senior Volunteering Programme.

If you’re on benefits

You can volunteer and claim benefits if:

  • the only money you get from volunteering is to cover expenses, like travel costs
  • you continue to meet the conditions of the benefit you get

Download a guide to volunteering while on benefits (PDF, 372KB)

If you’ve got a criminal record

You can volunteer but depending on your offence you may not be able to volunteer in certain roles. You might need a criminal records check if you want to volunteer with children or vulnerable adults.
