Tax and National Insurance after State Pension age, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

When you reach State Pension age you stop paying National Insurance.

You only pay Income Tax if your taxable income - including your pension - is more than your tax-free allowances (the amount of income you’re allowed before you pay tax).

Should you be paying tax?

HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) should send you a Pension Coding form to help work out if you should be paying tax and how much. You should get this before you reach State Pension age.

If you’re within a month of reaching State Pension age and you haven’t been sent a form, contact HMRC.

Or you can download one below:

Download ‘Form P161 Pension Coding form’ (PDF, 62KB)

You won’t automatically get a Pension Coding form if you’re self-employed.

Part 2: Filling in your Pension Coding form

If you don’t return your Pension Coding form you might end up paying too much tax.

Filling in the form

You’ll need to tell HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC):

  • whether you’ve decided to claim your State Pension now or later
  • the date you expect payments to start
  • the expected weekly amount you’ll get

You’ll also have to give details of:

  • other pensions you get
  • any income from Jobseeker’s Allowance, Incapacity Benefit or Employment and Support Allowance
  • income from employment if you’re still employed
  • savings income
  • details of any charity donations you’ve made using Gift Aid

You only need to include taxable income on the form - there’s a list of taxable and non-taxable income on the HMRC website.

HMRC has put together a set of notes to help you fill out the form.

Download ‘Pension coding form - help and guidance’ (PDF, 55KB)

Sending off the form

If you got the form in the post, use the reply envelope to send it back. Otherwise send it to:


HM Revenue & Customs
Pay As You Earn
PO Box 1970
Liverpool L75 1WX

Part 3: Making sure you've stopped paying National Insurance

National Insurance contributions build up your entitlement to your State Pension.

If you’re employed you pay Class 1 National Insurance contributions as a percentage of your earnings.

If you’re self-employed you pay Class 2 contributions at a flat weekly rate and Class 4 contributions annually, as a percentage of your taxable profits.

What happens at State Pension age

At State Pension age:

  • you stop paying Class 1 and Class 2 contributions, even if you’re still working
  • you still have to pay Class 4 contributions if you have taxable profits from the year you reach State Pension age - the next year, you’ll be exempt

If you continue working, you can get an Age Exemption Certificate to make sure you don’t pay National Insurance - contact HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) to get one.

Part 4: Tax if you carry on working and get a pension

If you keep working (or return to work) the tax you pay depends on your overall taxable income and the type of pension you’re getting.

If you’re just getting the State Pension

You’ll need to pay tax if your job, State Pension and any other income add up to more than your tax-free allowance.

If tax is due, HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) normally asks your employer to take it from your earnings - this is called PAYE (Pay As You Earn).

HMRC might ask you to fill in a Self Assessment tax return if your situation’s complicated (eg if you’ve foreign income or a lot of investment income).

If you get more than one pension

You’ll have several tax codes and payslips. You can get your tax code corrected if you think it’s wrong.

If you return to work after retirement

If you start a new job after retiring, your employer has to tell HMRC so they can get your tax right.

If your total income is more than your tax-free allowance you’ll have to pay tax. HMRC will give you a tax code and collect tax through PAYE.

If you’re earning a wage and getting a pension but you’re still on a low income you can get pension credit.

Part 5: Age-related tax allowances

As you get older there are allowances that can reduce your tax.

Increased Personal Allowance from age 65

If you get a Personal Allowance this increases in the tax year of your 65th birthday, depending on your taxable income - more information on the allowance amounts is online.

Married Couple’s Allowance

You can claim this if you’re married or in a civil partnership and at least one partner was born before 6 April 1935. It gets taken off your tax bill - the amount that’s deducted depends on your income.

You can find out more about Married Couple’s Allowance online.

Maintenance Payments Relief

You can get an allowance to reduce your tax bill for maintenance payments you make to an ex-spouse or civil partner if:

  • you or they were born before 6 April 1935
  • you’re separated or divorced and you’re making payments under a court order
  • the payments are for the maintenance of your ex-partner (as long as they haven’t re-married or formed a new civil partnership) or your children under 21

How much you can get

For the 2012-13 tax year Maintenance Payments Relief can reduce your tax bill by the lower of the following:

  • £296 - where you make maintenance payments of £2,960 or more a year
  • 10% of the money you’ve actually paid - where you make payments of less than £2,960 a year

How to claim

You can claim Maintenance Payments Relief by contacting HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC).

You can’t claim a tax reduction for any voluntary payments you make.

There’s more information about Income Tax rates and allowances online.

Part 6: Claiming back tax or National Insurance

If you’ve overpaid tax or National Insurance you can claim it back. If you’re on a low income and have savings, you can claim back tax deducted from the savings interest before you received it.

How to claim

You can claim a tax refund if you’ve:

  • had too much deducted from your pension
  • overpaid through your job
  • overpaid National Insurance

If you complete a tax return, you can also correct mistakes and claim a refund via Self Assessment.

Claiming back tax on savings interest

If you’re on a low income (less than £155 a week) you can register to have savings interest paid tax-free. You can also claim a refund of any tax you’ve overpaid on interest.

If your taxable income is only slightly higher than your tax-free allowance you can pay tax at a rate of 10% instead of the usual 20%.

You can check if you can get your interest tax-free or pay tax at the 10% rate online.
