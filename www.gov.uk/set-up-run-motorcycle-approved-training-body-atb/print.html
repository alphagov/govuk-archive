Set up and run a motorcycle approved training body (ATB), a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

You must be an approved training body (ATB) with the Driving Standards Agency (DSA) to provide:

  • compulsory basic training (CBT) for learner motorcyclists
  • direct access scheme (DAS) training to riders learning to ride a large motorcycle

CBT and DAS training must be given by instructors certified by the DSA working for your ATB.

The sites you use to provide training from must also be approved by the DSA.

Part 2: Rules for being an ATB

To be an approved training body (ATB) you must meet certain rules.

‘Fit and proper’

You must be a ‘fit and proper’ person to run the ATB.

When deciding if you’re a ‘fit and proper’ person, the Driving Standards Agency will look at if you have:

  • had any convictions in the last 4 years
  • any motoring convictions
  • been disqualified from driving
  • any penalty points on your licence
  • any court proceedings pending against you
  • any penalty points on your licence

Training

As an ATB you must provide compulsory basic training (CBT) and direct access scheme (DAS) courses which meet the legal requirements.

The CBT syllabus gives full details on what must be provided. The CBT  and DAS guidance also give best practice.

Download ‘CBT syllabus and guidance notes’ (PDF, 115KB)

Download ‘DAS motorcycle training guidance’ (PDF, 109KB)

An instructor is only allowed to supervise up to 2 trainees during on-road tuition.

Your instructors must be in radio contact with all trainees  at all times during on-road tuition.

Training sites

You must have the use of a suitable site or sites for training in the off-road parts of the CBT course.

These sites must be authorised by the Driving Standards Agency (DSA) before they’re used.

Instructors

You must make sure that all your instructors have a valid instructor certificate for the type of training they give. This must show the name of your ATB.

At least 1 of your instructors must have successfully completed the DSA’s assessment course for motorcycle training .

DSA-assessed instructors are allowed to train other people that you want  to appoint as instructors - this is known as ‘down-training’.

You should have at least 1 DSA-assessed instructor for every 10 instructors that have been down-trained.

Monitoring

You should send all required information about the courses you provide to DSA including:

  • reporting any incidents
  • dates when you’re providing CBT courses
  • the CBT certificates of completion you issue

The ATB manual gives more details about what you have to send.

Download ‘ATB manual’ (PDF, 190KB)

Part 4: Apply for CBT site authorisation

All the sites you intend to use for the practical training and riding elements of the compulsory basic training (CBT) course must be authorised by the Driving Standards Agency (DSA).

The sites must be authorised before you use them.

To apply for authorisation, download the application form and send it to DSA.

Download ‘Request for site inspection for compulsory basic training’ (PDF, 20KB)

You’ll also need to include:

  • a written permission letter, signed by the site owner on official letter headed paper
  • a draft plan of the intended site, eg an annotated satellite image

Site inspection

DSA will:

  • inspect the site
  • send a site report to you
  • send a unique site code to you if the site is suitable

The site report will explain any rules set by DSA. This will include how many trainees are allowed on the site.

You should make sure all your instructors are aware of these details.

Changes to authorised sites

You must tell DSA straight away if a site is altered or added to.

Download the site authorisation form and send it to DSA if the site has been altered.

Download ‘Request for site inspection for compulsory basic training’ PDF, 20KB)

  • a draft plan showing any changes to the original authorised site

Stopping using a site

You must tell the DSA straight away if you stop using a site for CBT.

You can email the DSA - you’ll need to include your approved training body number and the site’s unique code.

Part 5: Providing the CBT course

The compulsory basic training (CBT) course is made up of 5 parts.

The course syllabus and the order in which the parts must be delivered is set out in law.

Download ‘CBT syllabus and guidance notes’ (PDF, 115KB)

CBT certificate of completion

You must give trainees a CBT certificate of completion (DL196) when they reach the required standard.

The certificate must be completed and signed by the instructor, usually the  one who conducted part E of the CBT course.

The name and address of your approved training body (ATB) should be included on the certificate.

Ordering DL196 certificates

You can buy books of DL196 certificates from the Driving Standards Agency by using the online order form. Each book contains 25 certificates and costs £200.

Download ‘DL196 order form’ (PDF, 150KB)

Part 6: How your ATB is monitored

The Driving Standards Agency (DSA) monitors the standard of instruction given by:

  • approved training bodies (ATBs)
  • instructors delivering compulsory basic training (CBT) courses

The must make your instructors available for regular quality  assurance assessments.

You should tell your local DSA CBT manager the dates and times when your ATB will be running CBT courses.

After an assessment

You’ll get a letter of confirmation and report from DSA after an assessment visit if the training delivered meets the required standard.

The DSA can conduct more assessment if:

  • the training falls short of the required standard
  • there are breaches of regulations
  • there are reports of failure to follow the conditions of appointment

DSA will consider withdrawing an instructor’s authority to deliver courses if they fail to achieve the required standard during the subsequent assessments.

DSA can withdraw your ATB’s authorisation to deliver training courses if assessments show that it doesn’t consistently provide full and proper CBT courses.

Disagreeing with DSA’s decision

You can appeal to DSA if you or an instructor disagrees with DSA’s decision to withdraw their authority.
