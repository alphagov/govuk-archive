The UK and international affairs, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

The UK belongs to:

  • the European Union (EU) - a partnership of 27 European countries
  • Nato (the North Atlantic Treaty Organisation) - an alliance of 28 countries from Europe and North America
  • the Commonwealth - an association of 53 independent countries (mostly former British territories)
  • the United Nations (UN) - an international organisation with 193 member countries

Part 2: The European Union (EU)

The EU is a partnership of 27 European countries that work together on common issues. The UK joined the EU in 1973.

View a map and list of EU countries.

The UK and the EU

As a member of the EU, the UK follows various laws setting out the EU’s powers and how it can use them.

UK government ministers take part in EU discussions and decision-making, and final decisions are made jointly by all member countries.

There’s more information about the UK’s role in the EU on the Foreign & Commonwealth Office website.

The Council of the EU

This is the main decision-making part of the EU. It’s sometimes also called the Council of Ministers and approves European laws jointly with the European Parliament.

Presidency of the council rotates every 6 months from one member country to the next. The country holding the presidency takes charge of all the meetings.

Meetings are attended by the relevant minister from each country - so if health matters are being discussed, the UK Secretary of State for Health will attend.

The UK last held presidency of the Council of the EU from July to December 2005.

The European Council

This isn’t the same as the Council of the EU. It meets 4 times a year and sets the EU’s overall priorities.

It’s made up of the leaders of each member country and the President of the European Commission.

The European Commission

This is similar to the UK civil service and puts into practice the agenda set by the European Council. It drafts new laws and makes sure member countries follow them.

Every member country nominates 1 Commissioner, responsible for a specific policy area eg education or transport. They’re independent of their countries and serve the EU as a whole.

The European Courts

Each member country sends a judge to the European Court of Justice in Luxembourg. The court makes sure that European laws are interpreted and applied in the right way.

The Court of Auditors also has 1 representative from each member country. It reviews the accounts of the EU.

Part 3: Nato

Nato (the North Atlantic Treaty Organisation) is an alliance of 28 countries from Europe and North America.

Nato’s role

Its functions are to:

  • provide security and stability in the Euro-Atlantic area
  • provide a transatlantic forum to discuss joint concerns
  • defend the territories of member countries
  • contribute to crisis management and conflict prevention
  • promote partnership and co-operation between member countries

Membership of Nato is central to the UK’s defence policy.

How Nato works

Each Nato decision must be agreed by every member country. This means all decisions have the full backing of member countries.

Nato’s most important decision-making body is the North Atlantic Council. Every member country sits on this.

At the head of Nato is the Secretary General. You can read more information about Nato on the Nato website.

Part 4: The European Parliament

The European Parliament makes decisions on new European laws jointly with the Council of the European Union (EU).

There are 736 Members of the European Parliament (MEPs), including 72 from the UK, who represent the people in their part of the country.

The role of the European Parliament

The European Parliament meets in Strasbourg for 1 week each month. The rest of the time, the MEPs are based in Brussels.

The Parliament is consulted about all major decisions. Its role varies between:

  • co-decision, where a proposal requires the agreement of both the Council and the Parliament
  • consultation, where the Parliament can influence draft legislation but the Council has the final decision
  • decisions where the Parliament’s influence is greater

The Parliament also approves:

  • the appointment of the European Commission
  • international agreements

Parliament and the Council have joint responsibility for the European budget.

Elections

Elections for MEPs take place every 5 years, across all 27 member states of the EU. The last elections were in June 2009, and the next will be in June 2014.

The UK is divided into 12 regions, and each region has between 3 and 10 MEPs.

More information

You can find out more about the Parliament on the European Parliament website.

Part 5: The Commonwealth

There are 53 members of the Commonwealth, including the UK. They co-operate in areas of common interest.

Most of the Commonwealth members used to be British territories.

The Commonwealth’s aims

The Commonwealth promotes:

  • international peace and security
  • democracy
  • liberty and equal rights
  • economic and social development

It represents nearly 2 billion people - nearly a third of the world’s population.

Head of the Commonwealth

The Queen is the Head of the Commonwealth. She’s also the Head of State in the UK and 15 other independent countries called ‘Commonwealth realms’.

There’s more information on the Queen and the Commonwealth on the British Monarchy website.

Commonwealth Secretariat

This is based in London. It helps organise the Commonwealth Heads of Government meeting, which happens every 2 years. It also helps organise other meetings.

The Commonwealth Secretariat website has more information about how it works.

The Commonwealth Games

These happen every 4 years. England, Scotland, Wales, Northern Ireland, Guernsey, Jersey and the Isle of Man all compete separately.

In 2014, the Commonwealth Games will be held in Glasgow. The Commonwealth Games Federation website has more information.

Part 6: The United Nations (UN)

The UN was formed in 1945 by the UK and 50 other countries. Now, nearly every country in the world belongs to the UN, with 193 member countries.

The UN headquarters are in New York, with offices in other locations.

The General Assembly

This meets to talk about the world’s biggest problems and each member has 1 vote.

Decisions on major things like peace and security, admitting new members and the UN budget need a two-thirds majority vote. Other issues just need a simple majority - the largest share of the votes.

There’s more information about the General Assembly and a list of UN member countries online.

The Security Council

This is the most important part of the UN. It’s responsible for maintaining peace and international security and can meet at any time.

There are 15 council members and 5 of these are permanent - they are:

  • the UK
  • China
  • France
  • Russia
  • the US

The other 10 members are elected by the General Assembly every 2 years.

Decisions made by the Security Council need 9 out of 15 ‘yes’ votes. If there’s a ‘no’ vote from 1 of the 5 permanent members a decision can’t pass.

You can read more information about the Security Council on the UN Security Council website.

The Secretariat

This does the support work to carry out decisions made by the General Assembly or the Security Council. At its head is the Secretary General, who serves for 5 years.
