Benefits & credits: Legal aid

Notes

Part 1: Overview

Legal aid can help you pay for legal advice, mediation or representation in court if you can’t afford it.

This could include help for housing, debt, work, family, benefits or education problems.

You can also get legal aid if you’re accused of a crime.

Legal aid rules are different in Scotland and Northern Ireland.

Part 2: What you'll get

You could get free legal advice or someone to speak or negotiate for you.

In some cases, you could get a solicitor or barrister to represent you in court.

Type of legal aid What you’ll get
Legal help Advice on your rights and options, help with negotiations and paperwork
Help at court Someone to speak for you, but not formally represent you, in a civil court
Family mediation Help working out an agreement with a former partner, without going to court
Family help Help or representation in family disputes, eg to draw up a legal agreement
Legal representation Solicitor or barrister to get your case ready and speak in your defence in court

You may have to pay some money towards the legal costs of your case.

What you can get legal aid for

You can get legal aid for problems like:

  • being arrested, questioned or charged by the police
  • debt - eg if you’re visited by bailiffs
  • housing - eg if you’re being evicted
  • employment - eg unfair dismissal
  • family issues - eg divorce or separation
  • benefit payments - eg problems claiming Incapacity Benefit
  • education - eg disagreeing with a special educational needs decision about your child

What you can’t get legal aid for

You don’t usually get legal aid for:

  • a personal injury
  • a boundary dispute
  • libel or slander
  • representation at an employment tribunal
  • making a will (unless you’re over 70, disabled or have a disabled child)

Part 3: Eligibility

Civil (non-criminal) cases

For civil cases (eg a debt, family or housing problem) use the legal aid eligibility calculator to see if you can get help.

You’ll have to give details of your income, benefits and property. If you’re under 18, you may need to give information about your parents’ or guardians’ income.

Criminal cases

For criminal cases, your solicitor or barrister will check if you can get legal aid. You’ll have to give details of your income and savings.

You’ll automatically qualify if you’re under 16 (or under 18 and in full-time education) or on certain benefits.

If you’re not eligible

If you can’t get legal aid, you can get advice from a local legal adviser or solicitor.

Part 4: How to claim

To claim legal aid, contact a legal adviser in your area.

The legal adviser will apply for you.

You’ll have to show the adviser that your case has merit.

The adviser can help you very quickly in urgent cases - eg to keep you or your child safe from abuse.

What you need to know

Make sure you have details of you and your partner’s:

  • benefits
  • income, savings and spending
  • National Insurance numbers
